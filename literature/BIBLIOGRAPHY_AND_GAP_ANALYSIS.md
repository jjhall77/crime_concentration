## PART 3: CONFLICTS AND DEBATES

### 3.1 How Concentrated Is Crime, Really?

This is the central methodological dispute. Three camps:

**Camp A: The law holds.** Weisburd (2015), Weisburd et al. (2024). The 50-X bandwidth is narrow (IQR 3.2-5.7%). Across 47 studies, the pattern is remarkably consistent. Andresen & Weisburd (2025) argue that critics using the inverted 5-X metric (Hipp & Kim) produce artificially wide bandwidths; converting back to 50-X restores the tight range.

**Camp B: Concentration is inflated.** Chalfin et al. (2021), Bernasco & Steenbeek (2017). When places vastly outnumber crimes (120K segments, 300 homicides), some apparent concentration is mathematically inevitable. Standard Gini coefficients and cumulative-proportion metrics are biased upward in sparse-data conditions. Hipp & Kim (2017) find the bandwidth varies more across cities than Weisburd acknowledges, especially across city sizes and metrics.

**Camp C: Concentration is real but not exceptional.** Eck et al. (2017). Crime concentrates at roughly the same level as many other spatial phenomena. If the pattern is ubiquitous rather than distinctive, the policy implications change -- concentration may be a generic spatial property, not a special feature of crime.

**Your NYC angle:** NYC data is uniquely positioned to arbitrate. With ~120K street segments and millions of annual CFS/complaint records, NYC is one of the few cities where the sparse-data problem is substantially reduced for most crime types. You can show how concentration estimates change across crime types as sparsity increases (millions of petit larcenies vs. ~300 homicides), directly testing the Chalfin et al. critique while holding geography constant.

### 3.2 Micro-Places vs. Neighborhoods

**Early position:** Micro-places are the primary unit; neighborhoods are too coarse. (Sherman 1989, Weisburd 2015, O'Brien & Winship 2017.)

**Emerging integration:** Neighborhoods shape how crime clusters at the micro level. Schnell, DeWitt & Spencer (2024) find two distinct spatial transmission patterns -- neighborhood-scale vs. micro-place-scale clustering -- that vary across Chicago community areas. O'Brien & Ciomek (2023) find disorder-to-violence aggravation is strongest at addresses but amplified by neighborhood context. O'Brien, Ciomek & Tucker (2022) show concentration itself is a neighborhood-level characteristic.

**Unresolved:** The mechanisms by which neighborhoods produce or prevent micro-level clustering are specified only loosely. Variance decomposition tells you *how much* variability is at each level but not *why*.

**Your NYC angle:** You have nested geographies (physical blocks within census tracts within NTAs within precincts) and multiple data sources. You can replicate the variance decomposition approach (Schnell et al. 2017, Steenbeek & Weisburd 2016) in NYC and test whether neighborhood-level characteristics (demographics, land use, precinct boundaries) predict within-neighborhood concentration profiles, extending O'Brien et al. (2022) beyond Boston.

### 3.3 Temporal Stability vs. Spatial Mobility

**Stability claim:** Weisburd et al. (2004) trajectories show most segments maintain consistent crime levels over 14 years. Walter et al. (2023) find 47-69% of segments maintain stable classifications across six cities.

**Mobility claim:** Levin et al. (2017) show that the *level* of concentration is stable but the *specific segments* shift year to year. The top 5% isn't the same 5%.

**Implication:** If specific locations shift, retrospective hot-spot targeting will always lag. If they're stable, targeting works. The truth is somewhere in between -- and no one has good estimates of *how fast* hot spots form or dissolve.

**Your NYC angle:** With CFS data spanning historic and year-to-date periods, plus complaints going back decades, you can measure spatial mobility at fine temporal resolution. How many physical blocks remain in the top 5% across consecutive years? How quickly do emerging hot spots materialize? Prieto Curiel (2021) did weekly temporal analysis in Mexico City; you could do analogous work at the weekly or monthly level in NYC.

### 3.4 The "Eyes on the Street" Paradox

Jacobs (1961) argued foot traffic deters crime through informal surveillance. Diaz et al. (2025) find the opposite: Montevideo street markets increase thefts. The effect depends on whether passersby are "familiar strangers" who will intervene (small markets) vs. anonymous crowds providing criminal opportunities (large markets).

**Unresolved:** No one has tested this in a U.S. context at the micro level.

### 3.5 Administrative Data as Condition Proxies

Taylor et al. (2024) find 311 litter calls predict litter changes -- but so do theoretically unrelated 311 categories (information requests). This suggests 311 patterns reflect call propensity (demographics, technology access) rather than on-the-ground conditions. If so, any analysis using 311 as a disorder proxy is potentially optimizing on noise.

**Your NYC angle:** You have both 311 and crime data for NYC. You can directly test convergent and discriminant validity of 311 complaint categories as disorder proxies, extending the Philadelphia findings to a different city with richer data.

## PART 4: OPEN QUESTIONS AND NYC OPPORTUNITIES

### 4.1 Multi-Source Concentration Comparison (High Priority)

**Gap:** Most studies use a single data source (complaints OR calls for service OR arrest records). No published study systematically compares concentration patterns across measurement approaches at the same micro-units.

**Your data:** You have CFS (demand-side: what the public reports), complaints (official reports: what police record), shootings (incident-based), and 311 (disorder proxy) -- all geocoded to the same city.

**What you could do:** Measure concentration (50-X, generalized Gini, Chalfin counterfactual) for each data source at the physical block level. If concentration patterns differ substantially across sources, it tells us the "law" is partly a property of the measurement system, not just of crime. If they converge, it strengthens the substantive claim.

### 4.2 Crime-Type-Specific Concentration Profiles (High Priority)

**Gap:** Most studies aggregate "crime" or test one type (robbery, burglary, violent crime). Weisburd et al. (2024) note specific crime types show tighter concentration but the evidence is thin. No study has systematically compared concentration across the full crime typology at micro-units.

**Your data:** The key_codes.csv output shows the extraordinary granularity of NYPD complaint data -- from shoplifting to homicide, from identity theft to strangulation. CFS data separates disorder from narcotics calls.

**What you could do:** Compute concentration for every major offense category at the physical block level. Rank crime types by concentration strength. Test whether the Weisburd bandwidth holds for each type individually or only in the aggregate. Test whether the Chalfin sparse-data correction makes a bigger difference for rare crimes (homicide) vs. common ones (petit larceny).

### 4.3 Physical Blocks as Micro-Units (Methodological Contribution)

**Gap:** The field uses street segments (most common), addresses (O'Brien & Winship), or intersections. Physical blocks -- the area enclosed by streets -- are ecologically meaningful but rarely used as micro-units.

**Your data:** You have `physical_blocks.gpkg` and `intersection_nodes.gpkg`.

**What you could do:** Compare concentration at physical blocks vs. intersection nodes vs. census blocks. If physical blocks reveal different patterns than segments, you've shown the MAUP (Modifiable Areal Unit Problem) matters more than previously acknowledged.

### 4.4 Disorder-to-Violence Aggravation at Scale (Medium Priority)

**Gap:** O'Brien & Ciomek (2023) tested the aggravation hypothesis (disorder escalating to violence) in Boston at three nested scales. No replication exists outside Boston.

**Your data:** CFS disorder calls + CFS narcotics calls + complaint data with violent crime types + 311 disorder categories.

**What you could do:** Replicate the O'Brien & Ciomek multi-level aggravation model in NYC. Test whether disorder CFS at time *t* predicts violent complaints at time *t+1* at the address/block/neighborhood level. NYC's size gives you far more statistical power than Boston.

### 4.5 Temporal Dynamics of Hot Spot Formation (Medium Priority)

**Gap:** The literature describes where crime *was* concentrated. Very little work tracks the life course of individual hot spots: onset, escalation, persistence, dissolution. This is the "preceded by does not equal predicted by" problem flagged in the outline.

**Your data:** CFS data with timestamps, covering both historic and recent periods.

**What you could do:** Define physical-block-level hot spots at monthly resolution. Track how blocks enter and exit hot-spot status. Estimate average "careers" of hot spots (duration, intensity trajectories). Test early-warning indicators: does a spike in disorder CFS predict a block becoming a violent-crime hot spot within 3-6 months?

### 4.6 Within-Neighborhood Concentration Variation (Medium Priority)

**Gap:** O'Brien, Ciomek & Tucker (2022) showed this in Boston. Needs cross-city replication.

**Your data:** Multiple nested geographies (blocks within tracts within NTAs within precincts).

**What you could do:** Compute within-NTA or within-precinct concentration (generalized Gini). Test whether neighborhood composition (land use, demographics, housing stock) predicts concentration profile. Compare whether NYC neighborhoods resemble or differ from Boston.

### 4.7 Police Concentration Alignment (Lower Priority but Novel)

**Gap:** Dau et al. (2022) found police patrol in Antwerp was spatiotemporally unrelated to crime concentration. No published U.S. study has tested this.

**Your data:** You don't currently have patrol/GPS data. But CFS response times and dispatch data could serve as a proxy for police presence allocation.

**What you could do (if data obtainable):** Test whether NYPD patrol allocation aligns with crime concentration at the precinct or block level. If you can get CompStat deployment data or GPS traces, this becomes a high-value contribution.

### 4.8 The 311 Discriminant Validity Test (Lower Priority)

**Gap:** Taylor et al. (2024) showed this in Philadelphia for litter calls. Needs extension to other 311 categories and other cities.

**Your data:** NYC 311 data from 2020-present with hundreds of complaint types.

**What you could do:** Test whether crime-relevant 311 categories (noise, illegal parking, graffiti, drug activity) predict later crime changes at the block level better than theoretically irrelevant categories (pothole complaints, heat/hot water). If irrelevant categories predict equally well, it confirms the general-propensity interpretation.
