## Sherman1995 — Hot Spots of Crime and Criminal Careers of Places

**Citation:** Sherman, L. W. (1995). Hot spots of crime and criminal careers of places. In J. E. Eck & D. Weisburd (Eds.), *Crime and Place* (Crime Prevention Studies, Vol. 4, pp. 35-52). Monsey, NY: Criminal Justice Press.

**Book:** *Crime and Place* (Crime Prevention Studies, Vol. 4), pp. 35-52

**Key Findings:**
- Reiterates the Minneapolis finding: 3% of places produced 50% of police calls (323,000 calls, 1986). Crime is six times more concentrated by place than by individual offender (cf. Wolfgang's 18% of individuals → 50% of arrests).
- Proposes six dimensions of **criminal careers of places**, paralleling criminal career research on individuals: (1) onset, (2) recurrence, (3) frequency, (4) career length/desistance, (5) intermittency, and (6) crime type specialization.
- Develops routine activities theory for places via three crime controllers: **guardians** (protect targets), **handlers** (control offenders), and **place managers** (regulate place behavior) — drawn from Eck (1994) and Felson (1986).
- Place careers can be terminated by "capital punishment" (demolition), incapacitation (seizure/boarding up), or vigilante action — but the physical structure's persistence means careers may resume with new occupants.
- Intermittency in place careers is explained by economic conditions of place owners (Eck's "impaired immune system" analogy), changes in traffic flow, seasonal weather, and internal security changes.
- Crime type specialization at places is theoretically predicted by rational choice (situational features structure offender choices) but evidence is mixed: disorder and predatory crimes were not highly correlated in Minneapolis, though domestic calls and residential burglary were.
- Proposes four hypotheses for explaining crime variation at similar facilities (e.g., taverns): **patron** (who congregates there), **management** (how behavior is regulated), **behavior setting** (configuration of people, rules, space, time, objects), and **neighborhood** (contextual effects independent of patrons/management).
- Advocates for **longitudinal cohort studies of place types** (taverns, convenience stores) as a more powerful design than whole-city analyses, holding place function constant to isolate career dimensions.
- Policy: place-focused interventions are more tractable than offender- or community-level strategies because places are "bite-sized" and numerous enough for controlled experiments. Cites Minneapolis hot spots patrol experiment (Sherman & Weisburd, 1995), Jersey City drug market experiment (Weisburd & Green, 1995), and San Diego landlord experiment (Eck, 1995).

**Methods:** Theoretical and conceptual chapter synthesizing the Minneapolis empirical findings with routine activities theory, criminal career concepts, and emerging experimental evidence. Not an empirical study — uses case illustrations (Drake Place, D.C.; Dallas hotel; Minneapolis bar) to develop the criminal careers of places framework.

**Relevance:** The key conceptual bridge between the 1989 empirical finding and the theoretical framework that the concentration literature builds on. Goes beyond Sherman et al. (1989) by formalizing the six career dimensions, integrating Eck's place manager concept, and proposing the patron/management/behavior-setting/neighborhood hypotheses that later work tests. The "immune system" analogy for place management and the call for place-type cohort studies remain underexplored in the literature. Directly relevant to our project's questions about crime-type specificity and temporal dynamics of concentration.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "If future crime is six times more predictable by the address of the occurrence than by the identity of the offender, why aren't we doing more about it? Why aren't we thinking more about wheredunit, rather than just whodunit?" (p. 37)

## Farrell2015 — Crime Concentration Theory

**Citation:** Farrell, G. (2015). Crime concentration theory. *Crime Prevention and Community Safety*, *17*(4), 233-248.

**Journal:** *Crime Prevention and Community Safety*, Vol. 17, No. 4, pp. 233-248

**Key Findings:**
- Proposes "crime concentration theory" as a unifying framework synthesizing repeat offending, repeat victimization, hot spots, and hot products as manifestations of a single underlying concentration phenomenon.
- Argues that crime concentration is not unique to places — it operates across all units of analysis (people, places, products, times).
- The concentration pattern follows a well-established J-curve distribution found throughout the physical, biological, and social sciences.
- Connects the concentration of crime to broader opportunity structures — the convergence of motivated offenders, suitable targets, and the absence of guardians at specific places and times.

**Methods:** Theoretical synthesis and review of crime concentration research across multiple units of analysis (offenders, victims, places, products). Draws on routine activities theory, environmental criminology, and repeat victimization literature to argue for a unified concentration framework.

**Relevance:** Provides the theoretical glue connecting micro-place concentration to broader patterns across offenders, victims, and products. Essential for Section 2 of the literature review — situates the law of crime concentration within a larger framework rather than treating it as an isolated place-level phenomenon.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "Crime concentration theory proposes that the concentration of crime in offenders, victims, places and other units is the norm, and that crime occurs at the intersection of motivated offenders and criminal opportunities." (p. 234)

## EckWeisburd1995 — Crime Places in Crime Theory

**Citation:** Eck, J. E. & Weisburd, D. (1995). Crime places in crime theory. In J. E. Eck & D. Weisburd (Eds.), *Crime and Place* (Crime Prevention Studies, Vol. 4, pp. 1-33). Monsey, NY: Criminal Justice Press.

**Journal:** *Crime Prevention Studies*, Vol. 4, pp. 1-33

**Key Findings:**
- Three theoretical perspectives explain the importance of places for crime: rational choice, routine activity theory, and crime pattern theory — these perspectives are mutually supportive but provide different mechanisms.
- Five areas of empirical research establish the importance of places: crime concentration around facilities, high concentration at some addresses and absence at others, preventive effects of place features, offender mobility, and target selection.
- Evidence suggests fears of displacement are exaggerated; under some circumstances the opposite occurs — diffusion of benefits to unprotected locations.
- Distinguishes between "macro" approach (neighborhoods, communities) and the new "micro" approach examining places themselves (addresses, block faces, establishments).

**Methods:** Theoretical review and synthesis of the crime-and-place literature as of the mid-1990s. Integrates rational choice, routine activities, and crime pattern theory into a unified framework for understanding why crime concentrates at specific micro-places.

**Relevance:** A foundational theoretical piece that established the intellectual agenda for micro-place crime research. Bridges the gap between opportunity theories and the empirical concentration findings, providing the theoretical architecture that later work (Sherman, Weisburd) builds upon.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "Concern with place is very much central to this approach... the criminology of places fits neatly within several existing theories of crime." (p. 3)

## CohenFelson1979 — Social Change and Crime Rate Trends: A Routine Activity Approach

**Citation:** Cohen, L. E. & Felson, M. (1979). Social change and crime rate trends: A routine activity approach. *American Sociological Review*, *44*(4), 588-608.

**Journal:** *American Sociological Review*, Vol. 44, No. 4, pp. 588-608

**Key Findings:**
- Crime requires the convergence in space and time of three minimal elements: (1) motivated offenders, (2) suitable targets, and (3) the absence of capable guardians.
- The dispersion of routine activities away from households increases the opportunity for crime, explaining why crime rates rose from 1947-1974 despite improving social and economic conditions.
- Target suitability reflects value, physical visibility, access, and inertia of a target against illegal treatment.
- Victimization risk is far lower at or near home than elsewhere, and far lower among relatives than among non-relatives.
- Hawley's three temporal components of community structure (rhythm, tempo, timing) prove useful in analyzing how routine activities facilitate criminal opportunity.

**Methods:** Theoretical framework development with macro-level empirical analysis. Used National Crime Survey victimization data (1973-1974), time budget data, and UCR trends (1947-1974). Constructed a "household activity ratio" combining female labor force participation and single-adult households to test the hypothesis that routine activity shifts away from the household increase crime.

**Relevance:** The theoretical engine behind the entire crime-at-places literature. The convergence-in-space-and-time framework explains why crime concentrates at specific micro-places — where offender awareness spaces, suitable targets, and absent guardianship intersect. Cited by virtually every paper in the corpus.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "Most criminal acts require convergence in space and time of likely offenders, suitable targets and the absence of capable guardians against crime." (p. 588)

## Clarke1995 — Situational Crime Prevention

**Citation:** Clarke, R. V. (1995). Situational crime prevention. *Crime and Justice*, *19*, 91-150.

**Journal:** *Crime and Justice*, Vol. 19, pp. 91-150

**Key Findings:**
- Situational prevention reduces opportunities for specific crime categories by increasing risks/difficulties and reducing rewards, drawing on routine activity theory, rational choice, and environmental criminology.
- Displacement of crime has not proved to be the serious problem once thought; increasing evidence suggests "diffusion of benefits" to non-targeted crimes and places.
- An integrated model of the opportunity structure for crime links targets, victims, and crime facilitators — all shaped by socioeconomic structure, physical environment, and lifestyles/routine activities.
- Even deeply motivated crimes (violence, sexual offenses) are substantially affected by situational contingencies — e.g., whether an assault becomes a homicide depends on the presence of a lethal weapon.
- Successful applications span diverse contexts: aircraft hijacking, robbery in post offices and convenience stores, phone crime reduction through Caller-ID.

**Methods:** Theoretical essay and literature synthesis reviewing the evolution of situational crime prevention. Presents an integrated model of the opportunity structure for crime with a 12-technique taxonomy of prevention measures. Draws on case studies and evaluations from multiple countries.

**Relevance:** Provides the theoretical link between opportunity theory and place-based interventions. The 12-technique framework directly informs what can be done at hot spots once they are identified — connecting the concentration literature to actionable prevention strategies.

**Notable Quotes:**
- Situational prevention comprises "measures directed at highly specific forms of crime that involve the management, design, or manipulation of the immediate environment." (p. 91)

## Weisburd2015 — The Law of Crime Concentration and the Criminology of Place

**Citation:** Weisburd, D. (2015). The law of crime concentration and the criminology of place. *Criminology*, *53*(2), 133-157.

**Journal:** *Criminology*, Vol. 53, No. 2, pp. 133-157

**Key Findings:**
- Only 4.3% of empirical articles in *Criminology* (1990-2014) examined microgeographic units of analysis, compared with 66.1% focused on people.
- The law of crime concentration states: "for a defined measure of crime at a specific microgeographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages for a defined cumulative proportion of crime."
- Across eight cities using street segments as the common unit, 50% of crime was concentrated in approximately 4-6% of street segments in larger cities and 2-4% in smaller cities.
- Crime concentration stays within a narrow bandwidth across time despite strong volatility in crime incidents — in Seattle over 16 years, despite a 24% decline in crime, concentration remained between 4.7-6% of segments for 50% of crime.
- Growing interest in microplace studies: from 2.6% of *Criminology* articles in early 1990s to over 6% in 2010-2014.

**Methods:** Review and empirical analysis as the 2014 Sutherland Address. Examined units of analysis in 719 empirical articles published in *Criminology* (1990-2014). Conducted the first cross-city comparison of crime concentration using street segments as a common geographic unit across eight cities (Sacramento, New York, Cincinnati, Seattle, and others). Examined both cross-sectional concentration and temporal stability.

**Relevance:** The single most important paper in the corpus — the paper where the "law of crime concentration" gets its name and quantitative expression. Every other paper in this literature review is responding to, replicating, or critiquing the claims made here. The 4-6% bandwidth for 50% of crime is the benchmark that subsequent work tests.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "For a defined measure of crime at a specific microgeographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages for a defined cumulative proportion of crime." (p. 138)
- "It is time for criminology to take another turn in direction." (p. 151)

## Weisburd2024 — Crime Concentrations at Micro Places: A Systematic Review

**Citation:** Weisburd, D., Zastrowa, T., Kuen, K., & Andresen, M. A. (2024). Crime concentrations at micro places: A review of the evidence. *Aggression and Violent Behavior*, *78*, 101936.

**Journal:** *Aggression and Violent Behavior*, Vol. 78, 101936

**Key Findings:**
- Systematic review of 47 papers reporting 49 estimates of crime concentration at micro-geographic units.
- Median finding: 4.5% of streets account for 50% of crime; 1.25% account for 25%.
- Interquartile bandwidth is narrow: 3.2%-5.7% for the 50% threshold.
- Concentration is 7-14 times greater than what a random distribution would produce.
- Specific crime types show even tighter concentration than aggregate measures.
- Patterns hold across cities worldwide, supporting the "law" designation.

**Methods:** Systematic review of 47 empirical papers from 2004-2023. Standardized comparison of concentration metrics across studies using the 50-X and 25-X frameworks. Meta-analytic synthesis of concentration bandwidths.

**Relevance:** The definitive systematic review establishing the empirical robustness of the law of crime concentration. Demonstrates that the finding is not city-specific and that the bandwidth is narrower than critics suggest. Anchors the evidence base section of the literature review.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "The median concentration is 4.5% of street segments for 50% of crime, with an interquartile range of 3.2%-5.7%." (abstract)

## SchnellBragaPiza2017 — Spatial Variability of Violent Crime in Chicago

**Citation:** Schnell, C., Braga, A. A., & Piza, E. L. (2017). The influences of community areas, neighborhood clusters, and street segments on the spatial variability of violent crime in Chicago. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(3), 469-496.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 469-496

**Key Findings:**
- Street segments account for 56-65% of total variability in violent crime (2001-2014), replicating Steenbeek & Weisburd's (2015) results from The Hague (58-69%).
- During the 2001-2014 crime decline, the share of variability attributed to street segments increased while neighborhood/community-area shares decreased.
- Crime reduction was concentrated in specific hot spots rather than evenly distributed across the city.
- The variance decomposition approach shows that micro-places are the primary unit of spatial variability, but neighborhoods are not irrelevant.

**Methods:** 359,786 violent crime incidents (2001-2014) geocoded to 41,926 street segments nested within 342 neighborhood clusters and 76 community areas. Linear mixed models with variance decomposition.

**Relevance:** Key U.S. replication of the variance decomposition approach pioneered in The Hague. Demonstrates that micro-places dominate spatial variability in a major American city, while also showing that crime declines are spatially selective — strengthening the case for place-based intervention.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "The data suggest that violence has become more concentrated at a smaller number of street segments in community areas with declining crime trends." (p. 490)

## OBrienWinship2017 — The Gains of Greater Granularity (Boston)

**Citation:** O'Brien, D. T. & Winship, C. (2017). The gains of greater granularity: The presence and persistence of problem properties in urban neighborhoods. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(3), 649-675.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 649-675

**Key Findings:**
- Less than 1% of addresses generated 25% of all crime and disorder reports in Boston (2011-2013).
- 95-99% of variance was attributable to the address level — substantially exceeding street-segment findings.
- Problem properties are identifiable and relatively stable across years, though roughly 15% show crime in one year but not another.
- The address level provides genuinely new information beyond what neighborhood or street-segment analysis reveals.

**Methods:** 2+ million geocoded 911/311 calls in Boston (2011-2013) at 123,265 addresses nested in 13,767 street segments and 178 census tracts. Multilevel Poisson models.

**Relevance:** Pushes the grain of analysis finer than street segments to individual addresses, showing that concentration intensifies dramatically at finer scales. Supports a "problem properties" approach to crime prevention and demonstrates the address level is not merely a disaggregation of the street-segment finding.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "Less than 1% of addresses generated approximately 25% of all reports, including 911 and 311 reports." (p. 649)

## LevinRosenfeldDeckard2017 — Law of Crime Concentration in St. Louis

**Citation:** Levin, A., Rosenfeld, R., & Deckard, M. (2017). The law of crime concentration: An application and recommendations for future research. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(3), 635-648.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 635-648

**Key Findings:**
- Spatial inequality (overall concentration level) is highly stable over 15 years (2000-2014) — roughly 50% of crime stays in the top 5% of segments year after year.
- However, the specific segments constituting the top 5% shift over time (spatial mobility).
- Poisson simulations show a nontrivial portion of apparent concentration is attributable to random variation, especially in low-crime contexts.
- Crime-free segments (~40% of all segments) inflate concentration statistics.
- The critical distinction between stable concentration levels and unstable specific locations has direct implications for hot-spot policing.

**Methods:** 15 years of crime in St. Louis (2000-2014) across 16,056 street segments. Poisson simulations to estimate random-chance concentration. Separate analysis of spatial inequality versus spatial mobility.

**Relevance:** Introduces the crucial distinction between spatial inequality (stable) and spatial mobility (shifting). This complicates the simple narrative that "the same places stay hot" and has direct implications for whether retrospective hot-spot targeting can work prospectively.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "The distinction between spatial inequality and spatial mobility is critical: the level of concentration is stable, but the specific locations that constitute the hot spots shift." (p. 645)

## HippKim2017 — Crime Concentration Across Cities of Varying Sizes

**Citation:** Hipp, J. R. & Kim, Y.-A. (2017). Measuring crime concentration across cities of varying sizes: Complications based on the spatial and temporal scale employed. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(3), 595-632.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 595-632

**Key Findings:**
- Considerable variability in crime concentration across 42 southern California cities — concentration in the top 5% of segments ranged widely depending on metric and city size.
- Four major challenges identified: (1) no agreed definition of "narrow bandwidth"; (2) questionable comparability across different-sized cities; (3) random variation inflates apparent concentration, especially in low-crime cities; (4) different temporal assumptions yield different results.
- Results challenge the universality of the law of crime concentration.
- Smaller cities tend to show more extreme concentration statistics, partly as a mathematical artifact.

**Methods:** 42 southern California cities (40,000+ population), 2005-2012. Five Part 1 crime types examined with three different measurement approaches reflecting different temporal assumptions.

**Relevance:** The primary critical challenge to Weisburd's law. Demonstrates that apparent consistency may partly be an artifact of how concentration is measured and compared across cities of different sizes. Andresen & Weisburd (2025) later argue much of this disagreement stems from metric choice (5-X vs. 50-X).

**Notable Quotes:**
- "We do not know the effect of these metric variations on citizens... we still lack standards for what constitutes 'similar' concentration." (p. 627)

## HabermanSorgRatcliffe2017 — Crime Concentration Across Temporal Scales

**Citation:** Haberman, C. P., Sorg, E. T., & Ratcliffe, J. H. (2017). Assessing the validity of the law of crime concentration across different temporal scales. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(3), 547-567.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 547-567

**Key Findings:**
- Weisburd's law holds across all temporal scales — hour of day, day of week, and season.
- Concentration percentages at each time slice closely match the overall Weisburd bandwidth.
- Some micro-places experience robberies across all time periods; others show isolated temporal spikes.
- Temporal patterns remain underexamined in the broader crime-and-place literature.

**Methods:** 17,918 street robberies in Philadelphia (2009-2011) examined across three temporal scales. Cumulative proportion analysis at each time period.

**Relevance:** Extends the law of crime concentration into the temporal domain. Demonstrates that concentration is not just a spatial phenomenon but persists at every temporal slice, with direct implications for time-specific resource allocation.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "The law holds across all temporal scales tested, suggesting a robust empirical regularity that transcends both space and time." (p. 564)

## AndresenLinningMalleson2017 — Spatial Stability of Property Crime in Vancouver

**Citation:** Andresen, M. A., Linning, S. J., & Malleson, N. (2017). Crime at places and spatial concentrations: Exploring the spatial stability of property crime in Vancouver BC, 2003-2013. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *33*(2), 255-275.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 33, No. 2, pp. 255-275

**Key Findings:**
- Property crime in Vancouver is highly concentrated: as few as 5% of street segments/intersections account for most crime, depending on crime type.
- Spatial stability is present when considering all street segments/intersections, but when restricting to segments with crime, stability is only present in recent years.
- Despite significant crime drops in Vancouver over the study period, spatial patterns and concentrations remained relatively stable.
- The spatial point pattern test is more informative than traditional trajectory analysis for measuring stability.

**Methods:** Georeferenced crime data (2003-2013) for four property crime types (commercial burglary, mischief, theft from vehicle, theft of vehicle) in Vancouver. Crime concentrations at street segment and intersection level. Nonparametric spatial point pattern test for pairwise and longitudinal stability.

**Relevance:** Important international (Canadian) replication of crime concentration and spatial stability findings. Demonstrates that concentration persists through the crime drop, though the stability of specific hot spots is more nuanced than aggregate statistics suggest.

**Notable Quotes:**
- "Despite the crime drop that has occurred in Vancouver, there is still spatial stability present over time at levels suitable for theoretical development." (abstract)

## WalterTillyerAcolin2023 — Spatiotemporal Crime Patterns Across Six U.S. Cities

**Citation:** Walter, R. J., Tillyer, M. S., & Acolin, A. (2023). Spatiotemporal crime patterns across six U.S. cities. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *39*, 983-1011.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 39, pp. 983-1011

**Key Findings:**
- 47-69% of street segments maintained the same classification over the study period across all six cities (Chicago, LA, NYC, Philadelphia, San Antonio, Seattle).
- Segments transitioning from high to low crime (or vice versa) were rare.
- All six cities showed considerable stability and consistent concentration.
- Some segments differ significantly from their spatial neighbors (outliers within clusters), revealing important street-to-street variability.

**Methods:** Crime data from six U.S. cities. Local Moran's I to identify spatial clusters and outliers. Temporal analysis of classification stability across years.

Provides the strongest evidence that the policy translation of crime concentration research — directing police to hot spots — produces real crime reductions with minimal displacement. ## GroffWeisburdYang2010 — Street to Street Variability in Crime Trajectories (Seattle)

**Citation:** Groff, E. R., Weisburd, D., & Yang, S.-M. (2010). Is it important to examine crime trends at a local "micro" level?: A longitudinal analysis of street to street variability in crime trajectories. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *26*, 7-32.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 26, pp. 7-32

**Key Findings:**
- Applied trajectory analysis to 24,023 street segments in Seattle over 16 years (1989-2004), identifying 22 trajectory groups collapsed into 8 patterns: crime free (49.5%), low stable (32%), low decreasing (9.2%), low increasing (3.8%), moderate stable (1.2%), high decreasing (2.4%), high increasing (0.9%), and chronic high (1.0%).
- In a surprising number of cases, individual street segments have trajectories which are unrelated to their immediately adjacent streets — significant street-to-street heterogeneity.
- Used Ripley's K-function and bivariate K-function to quantify spatial relationships. High-crime trajectories showed greatest spatial clustering; crime-free and low-stable segments were most uniformly distributed.
- "Bad neighborhoods" contain "good streets" and vice versa.

**Methods:** 1,697,212 crime incident reports (1989-2004) for 24,023 street segments in Seattle. GBTM then Ripley's K-function and bivariate K-function for spatial pattern analysis.

**Relevance:** First study to systematically examine the spatial distribution of temporal crime trajectories. Demonstrates that adjacent segments can follow completely different trajectories, justifying the micro-place focus.

## CurmanAndresenBrantingham2015 — Vancouver Street Segment Trajectories

**Citation:** Curman, A. S. N., Andresen, M. A., & Brantingham, P. J. (2015). Crime and place: A longitudinal examination of street segment patterns in Vancouver, BC. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, *31*, 127-147.

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*, Vol. 31, pp. 127-147

**Key Findings:**
- First replication of Weisburd et al. (2004) outside Seattle. 16 years (1991-2006) of Vancouver calls-for-service.
- Majority of street blocks showed stable crime trends; a minority revealed decreasing trends. Unlike Seattle, no increasing trajectories.
- 7.8% of segments accounted for 60% of crime. Crime drop (-40%) was more widespread than Seattle's but still driven by a small percentage of segments.

**Methods:** 1+ million crime reports (1991-2006) at the street segment level. GBTM (zero-inflated Poisson via Proc Traj) and k-means non-parametric cluster analysis.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that the Seattle trajectory findings generalize to a Canadian city. The k-means alternative offers a methodological advance for datasets violating GBTM distributional assumptions.

## PayneGallagher2016 — Trajectories of Crime at Addresses in Cincinnati

**Citation:** Payne, T. C. & Gallagher, K. (2016). The importance of small units of aggregation: Trajectories of crime at addresses in Cincinnati, Ohio, 1998-2012. *Criminology, Criminal Justice, Law & Society*, *17*(1), 20-36.

**Journal:** *Criminology, Criminal Justice, Law & Society*, Vol. 17, No. 1, pp. 20-36

**Key Findings:**
- Applied GBTM at the **address level** — the smallest unit used in any trajectory study — across 125,226 addresses in Cincinnati over 15 years (1998-2012).
- Six trajectories: low-stable (58.3%), two increasing (16.7%, 6.6%), two decreasing (12.3%, 3.6%), and high-stable (2.5%).
- The high-stable trajectory comprises just 2.5% of addresses yet consistently accounts for one-third of all crime.
- Places with very different trajectories often located on the same street segment — high-crime addresses exist among "clouds" of low-crime places.
- Retail and large apartment complexes (40+ units) overrepresented in highest-crime trajectory.

**Methods:** 2,247,751 police incidents (1998-2012) at 125,226 addresses. Zero-inflated Poisson GBTM via Stata traj. Spatial analysis with ArcGIS.

**Relevance:** Pushes the trajectory framework to the finest geographic resolution. Demonstrates that street segments mask important variation at the address level.

## Luo2026 — Crime Trajectories Across 12 U.S. Cities

**Citation:** Luo, X. I. (2026). Examining crime trajectories at micro geographic locations across varied urban contexts in the U.S. *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10940-026-09656-8

**Journal:** *Journal of Quantitative Criminology* (online first, 2026)

**Key Findings:**
- Most comprehensive multi-city trajectory study: 12 U.S. cities (Seattle, San Antonio, Memphis, LA, Scottsdale, Riverside, Minneapolis, Akron, Asheville, Reading, Santa Monica, Diamond Bar), 297,199 street segments, 2010-2018.
- Cities stratified by size, growth, and employment presence — first study to systematically vary city context.
- Crime concentration confirmed across all 12 cities: 50% of violent crime in 0.3-4.3% of segments; 50% of property crime in 0.9-6.1%.

**Relevance:** Broadest and most recent test of trajectory generalizability. Concentration is universal; trajectory dynamics vary by city characteristics.

## NaginOdgers2010 — Group-Based Trajectory Modeling in Clinical Research

**Citation:** Nagin, D. S. & Odgers, C. L. (2010). Group-based trajectory modeling in clinical research. *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, *6*, 109-138.

**Journal:** *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, Vol. 6, pp. 109-138

**Key Findings:**
- Nontechnical overview of GBTM and growth mixture modeling (GMM).
- GBTM treats groups as approximations of a continuous distribution; GMM allows within-group variation.
- Model selection guidelines: BIC for number of groups (not sole criterion); posterior probabilities ≥0.7 for adequate classification.
- Cautions against reifying groups as "real" distinct entities — trajectory groups are statistical approximations.
- Extensions: predictors of group membership, time-varying covariates, treatment effects, dual trajectory modeling.

**Methods:** Methodological review with clinical demonstrations (aggression, obesity, anxiety, substance use).

**Relevance:** Primary methodological reference for GBTM as used in all place-based trajectory studies. Essential for understanding assumptions, limitations, and best practices.

## NaginJonesPassosTremblay2018 — Group-Based Multi-Trajectory Modeling

**Journal:** *Statistical Methods in Medical Research*, Vol. 27, No. 7, pp. 2015-2023
