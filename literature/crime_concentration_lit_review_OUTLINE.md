## 1. THE FINDING

**Opening move:** Start with the striking simplicity of the claim. Something like: *A small share of places accounts for a large share of crime, and this ratio is remarkably stable across cities, crime types, and time periods.*

### Key points to develop:

- **Sherman (1995) — Criminal careers of places.** Building on the 1989 Minneapolis empirical finding (3% of places → 50% of calls; concentration six times greater by place than by individual offender), this chapter formalizes six dimensions of criminal careers of places: onset, recurrence, frequency, career length/desistance, intermittency, and crime type specialization. Introduces three crime controllers (guardians, handlers, place managers) and four hypotheses for crime variation at similar facilities (patron, management, behavior setting, neighborhood). Proposes longitudinal cohort studies of specific place types as the ideal design. [IN FOLDER: 02-Sherman.pdf]

- **Weisburd (2015) — the formalization.** This is where "the law of crime concentration" gets its name and its quantitative expression. Weisburd analyzed data from multiple cities and proposed a "bandwidth" of 4–6% of street segments accounting for ~50% of crime. The framing as a "law" was deliberately provocative — meant to signal that this wasn't just an interesting pattern but an empirical regularity with the consistency to guide policy. [LOOK UP: Weisburd, D. (2015). "The Law of Crime Concentration and the Criminology of Place." *Criminology*, 53(2), 133–157. — This is the single most important citation in the review. If you don't have the PDF, get it.]

- **Weisburd, Zastrowa, Kuen & Andresen (2024) — the systematic review.** 47 papers, 49 estimates. Median: 4.5% of streets → 50% of crime. IQR: 3.2%–5.7%. Concentration is 7–14× greater than random distribution. This is the strongest evidence that the finding is not city-specific. [IN FOLDER: 1-s2.0-S1359178924000697-main (1).pdf]

- **Why it struck the field as important:** Two reasons. First, the consistency — the numbers were eerily similar across very different urban contexts. Second, the policy implication — if crime is this concentrated, place-based interventions become enormously efficient relative to offender-based or community-wide strategies. The finding gave empirical teeth to hot spots policing.

### Tone note:
Present the finding with appropriate respect but don't write it as gospel. You're building toward later sections that complicate it. The reader should feel the power of the claim here, then feel that power qualified as the review progresses.

## 2. INTELLECTUAL ROOTS

**Opening move:** The empirical finding didn't emerge from a vacuum. The theoretical groundwork was laid decades before anyone had the geocoded data to test it.

### Key threads to trace:

- **The Chicago School and its legacy.** Shaw & McKay, social disorganization, the neighborhood as the unit of analysis. This was the dominant spatial tradition in criminology for most of the 20th century. Crime concentration at micro-places was partly a rebellion against this — an argument that neighborhoods are too coarse, that the action is at a finer grain. But also an inheritance: the Chicago School established that crime has a spatial structure at all. [LOOK UP: Shaw, C.R. & McKay, H.D. (1942). *Juvenile Delinquency and Urban Areas*. — You need to briefly characterize the neighborhood-level tradition before you can explain why the micro-place turn felt like a break from it. Also consider Park, Burgess & McKenzie (1925) *The City* for the ecological model.]

- **Jane Jacobs (1961) — "eyes on the street."** *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* argued that the physical design of streets and buildings shapes informal social control. This is a direct precursor to the micro-place perspective: it's about specific blocks, specific sidewalks, specific building configurations — not neighborhoods in the aggregate. Jacobs was not a criminologist, but her observational framework profoundly influenced environmental criminology. [LOOK UP: Jacobs, J. (1961). *The Death and Life of Great American Cities*. New York: Random House. — Note: Díaz, Fossati & Trajtenberg (2025) in your folder directly test the "eyes on the street" thesis using Montevideo street markets and find it doesn't work the way Jacobs imagined. You can foreshadow that here.]

- **The Brantinghams — crime pattern theory.** Patricia and Paul Brantingham developed the geometry of crime: nodes, paths, edges, and the idea that crime occurs at predictable spatial intersections of offender activity spaces and target locations. This was the first explicit theory of why crime would concentrate at specific places rather than being evenly distributed. [LOOK UP: Brantingham, P.L. & Brantingham, P.J. (1993). "Nodes, Paths and Edges: Considerations on the Complexity of Crime and the Physical Environment." *Journal of Environmental Psychology*, 13(1), 3–28. Also their 1981 book *Environmental Criminology*.]

- **Routine activities theory — Cohen & Felson (1979).** Crime requires the convergence in space and time of a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian. This is the theoretical engine behind crime concentration: places where convergence happens regularly will have more crime. The theory shifts the focus from offender motivation (why do people commit crime?) to opportunity structure (where and when do the conditions for crime exist?). [LOOK UP: Cohen, L.E. & Felson, M. (1979). "Social Change and Crime Rate Trends: A Routine Activity Approach." *American Sociological Review*, 44(4), 588–608.]

- **The gradual turn away from offender-centered criminology.** Make this a narrative point, not just a list. Through the 1960s–80s, the dominant question was "why do individuals offend?" Place was backdrop. The shift toward opportunity and place — driven by Jacobs, the Brantinghams, Cohen & Felson, and Clarke's situational crime prevention — was a reorientation of the entire field's causal framework. Sherman's empirical work then provided the data to back the theoretical intuition.

- **Farrell — crime concentration theory.** Synthesizes the terminology: repeat offending, repeat victimization, hot spots, hot products. Argues these are all manifestations of a single underlying concentration phenomenon. [IN FOLDER: cpcs.2015.17.pdf — Use this to show that concentration is not just a place-level finding but part of a broader pattern across units of analysis.]

### Structural note:
This section should read as intellectual history, not a list of theorists. The narrative arc is: neighborhoods → design of specific places → opportunity at specific times and places → empirical confirmation of concentration. Each step builds toward the finding described in Section 1.

## 3. THE EVIDENCE BASE

**Opening move:** The law of crime concentration has been tested in enough cities that the question is no longer whether the pattern exists but how precisely it holds and where its boundaries are.

### Key studies to walk through:

- **Seattle — Weisburd et al. (2004, 2012).** The trajectory work. Weisburd's longitudinal analysis of Seattle street segments (1989–2002) showed that crime trajectories at the street level were remarkably stable, with most segments maintaining consistent crime levels over 14 years. Only a small number of segments showed significant increases or decreases. This is foundational for the stability claim. [LOOK UP: Weisburd, D., Bushway, S., Lum, C. & Yang, S.-M. (2004). "Trajectories of Crime at Places." *Criminology*, 42(2), 283–321. Also Weisburd, D., Groff, E.R. & Yang, S.-M. (2012). *The Criminology of Place: Street Segments and Our Understanding of the Crime Problem*. Oxford University Press.]

- **Tel Aviv — Weisburd & Amram (2014).** Extended the finding to a non-U.S. context. Roughly the same concentration ratios held. Important for the generalizability claim. [LOOK UP: Weisburd, D. & Amram, S. (2014). "The Law of Concentrations of Crime at Place: The Case of Tel Aviv-Jaffa." *Police Practice and Research*, 15(2), 101–114.]

- **The Hague — Steenbeek & Weisburd (2015).** Another international replication. Steenbeek decomposed variance across geographic levels and found street segments accounted for 58–69% of total crime variability. [LOOK UP: Steenbeek, W. & Weisburd, D. (2016). "Where the Action Is in Crime? An Examination of Variability of Crime Across Different Spatial Units in The Hague, 2001–2009." *JQC*, 32(3), 449–469.]

- **Chicago — Schnell, Braga & Piza (2017).** Street segments accounted for 56–65% of variability in violent crime (2001–2014), replicating The Hague finding. During the crime decline, the street-segment share *increased* — crime reduction was concentrated in specific hot spots, not evenly distributed. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9313-x.pdf]

- **Cincinnati — Haberman, Sorg & Ratcliffe (2017).** Tested the law across temporal scales (hour, day, season). Concentration held at every slice. Some micro-places showed robbery across all time periods; others had isolated spikes. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9327-4 (1).pdf]

- **Boston — O'Brien & Winship (2017).** Pushed the grain finer: less than 1% of *addresses* generated 25% of all crime/disorder reports. 95–99% of variance at the address level. The address level provides genuinely new information beyond street segments. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9330-9.pdf]

- **St. Louis — Levin, Rosenfeld & Deckard (2017).** 15 years of data. Spatial inequality (the overall concentration level) is stable, but spatial mobility (which specific segments are hot) shifts. ~40% of segments are crime-free, inflating concentration statistics. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9332-7.pdf]

- **42 Southern California cities — Hipp & Kim (2017).** Considerable variability across cities. Challenged the universality of the law by showing that concentration in the top 5% of segments ranged widely depending on metric and city size. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9328-3.pdf]

- **Six U.S. cities — Walter, Tillyer & Acolin (2023).** 47–69% of street segments maintained stable classifications over time. Transitions from high to low (or vice versa) were rare. Consistent concentration across all six cities. [IN FOLDER: s10940-022-09556-7.pdf]

- **Vancouver — Andresen, Linning & Malleson.** Property crime concentration and spatial stability. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9295-8.pdf]

### What they consistently find:
Across all these studies, the core pattern holds: a small percentage of places generates a disproportionate share of crime. The exact percentages vary, but Weisburd's 4–6% bandwidth captures the central tendency.

### What varies:
The degree of concentration differs by crime type (violent crime tends to concentrate more than property crime), by unit of analysis (addresses show more concentration than street segments, which show more than neighborhoods), and by city (though the range is narrower than critics sometimes suggest). Temporal scale matters less than spatial scale.

### The stability question — trajectory analysis:
This is where GBTM (group-based trajectory modeling) becomes central. Weisburd et al. (2004) pioneered the application of GBTM to street segments in Seattle (14 years), finding 84% of segments followed stable trajectories and that the crime drop was driven by a small subset of declining micro-places, not uniform citywide decline. This has been replicated extensively:

- **Groff, Weisburd & Yang (2010)** — Applied GBTM to Seattle street segments, finding distinct trajectory groups including chronic-high segments that are spatially heterogeneous: high-crime streets frequently sit adjacent to low-crime streets. This "good streets in bad neighborhoods" finding undermines purely ecological explanations. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/s10940-009-9081-y.md]

- **Curman, Andresen & Brantingham (2015)** — Replicated in Vancouver. Compared GBTM with k-means clustering and found qualitatively equivalent results. K-means produced more parsimonious groupings. Both methods identified stable trajectory groups. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/s10940-014-9228-3.md]

- **Wheeler, Worden & McLean (2016)** — Albany. Used GBTM at the street segment level. Found spatial heterogeneity consistent with Groff et al. — neighboring segments often diverge dramatically in trajectory. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/s10940-015-9268-3.md]

- **Payne & Gallagher (2016)** — Cincinnati. Applied GBTM at the address level (even finer grain than street segments). Found 2.5% of addresses in the chronic-high trajectory accounted for roughly one-third of all crime. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/633-the-importance-of-small-units-of-aggregation...md]

- **Andresen, Curman & Linning (2017)** — Extended Vancouver analysis with spatial point pattern tests, confirming trajectory group stability. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/s10940-016-9301-1.md]

- **Luo (2026)** — Replicated GBTM across 12 U.S. cities simultaneously. Confirmed the finding is not city-specific: most places follow stable trajectories, with a small chronic-high group accounting for disproportionate crime. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/s10940-026-09656-8 (1).md]

- **Nagin & Odgers (2010)** — Methodological guide to GBTM: recommends posterior probability ≥ 0.7 per group as the adequacy criterion for assignment quality. Nagin (2005) cautions that "the objective is not to identify the 'true' number of groups." [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/annurev.clinpsy.121208.131413.md]

- **Nagin, Jones, Passos & Tremblay (2018)** — Multi-trajectory modeling extension, allowing joint trajectory groups across multiple outcome types simultaneously. [IN FOLDER: to_be_processed_parsed/nagin-et-al-2016-group-based-multi-trajectory-modeling.md]

The emerging picture from trajectory analysis: concentration as a structural feature is highly durable — 70-85% of places follow stable trajectories. The crime drop is not uniform but driven by a small subset of declining micro-places. Chronic-high places (typically 1-3% of units) account for 20-50% of crime and persist across decades. But the *specific places* do shift to some degree (Levin et al., Walter et al.), and adjacent places can follow completely different trajectories (Groff et al., Wheeler et al.).

### The generalizability question:
Does the ~5%/~50% ratio actually hold, or is it a rough heuristic? Weisburd et al. (2024) say the bandwidth is narrow (IQR 3.2%–5.7%). Hipp & Kim (2017) say it varies more than that. The honest answer: the finding is robust as a central tendency, contested at the margins, and potentially inflated by measurement choices (see Section 5).

## 4. WHAT CONCENTRATION DOESN'T TELL YOU

**Opening move:** The descriptive finding is powerful, but descriptive findings are not theories. Concentration tells you *that* crime clusters; it does not tell you *why*.

### Key arguments:

- **Concentration is a pattern, not an explanation.** The fact that 5% of places account for 50% of crime is consistent with multiple causal stories: (a) the built environment channels activity to those places; (b) social dynamics (collective efficacy, social disorganization) vary at the micro level; (c) policing patterns create self-reinforcing feedback loops (more patrol → more detection → more recorded crime); (d) measurement artifacts concentrate recorded crime at places that are actually proxy locations. The finding alone cannot distinguish among these.

- **The "preceded by ≠ predicted by" logic.** This is crucial for your thesis. The fact that crime has historically clustered at certain places does not automatically mean you can forecast which places will be hot next. Retrospective description ≠ prospective prediction. Hot spots analysis tells you where crime *was*, and it often turns out crime stays there (stability), but the literature is less clear on how well you can identify *emerging* hot spots before they fully materialize. [NOTE: Develop this point carefully. If your thesis involves prediction/forecasting, this is where you establish the gap.]

- **The mechanisms remain underspecified.** Routine activities, crime pattern theory, and place management theory all provide *frameworks* for why concentration might occur, but rigorous micro-level causal evidence is thin. Díaz et al. (2025) is one of the few quasi-experimental tests: street markets add "eyes on the street" but *increase* thefts rather than deterring crime. The mechanism depends on who the "eyes" belong to and whether they have the motivation and capacity to intervene. [IN FOLDER: s10940-025-09636-4 (2).pdf]

- **The multi-level problem.** O'Brien & Ciomek (2023) show that aggravation (disorder → violence) operates most strongly at the address level, but neighborhood context amplifies it. Schnell, DeWitt & Spencer (2024) show that neighborhoods shape how crime clusters at the micro level. This means a purely micro-level explanation of concentration misses the contextual architecture. [IN FOLDER: o-brien-ciomek-2022 and schnell-et-al-2022]

- **O'Brien, Ciomek & Tucker (2022) — concentration as a neighborhood characteristic.** Two neighborhoods can have identical crime rates but very different concentration profiles — one with a sharp hotspot, another with diffuse crime. This means concentration itself varies in ways that aggregate metrics obscure. [IN FOLDER: s10940-021-09495-9 (1).pdf]

### Structural note:
This section is your pivot. Sections 1–3 build up the finding. This section starts taking it apart — not to demolish it, but to show that it needs more theoretical work than it has received. The tone should be respectful but intellectually honest: *the finding is real, but it has been asked to carry more weight than a descriptive pattern can bear.*

## 5. THE MEASUREMENT PROBLEM

**Opening move:** How much of what we "know" about crime concentration is an artifact of how crime is recorded, geocoded, and counted? The answer: more than the field has comfortably reckoned with.

### Key problems:

- **The sparse data problem.** When a city has 120,000 street segments and 300 annual homicides, some apparent concentration is mathematically inevitable even under random distribution. Chalfin, Kaplan & Cuellar (2021) demonstrated this rigorously: standard metrics significantly overestimate concentration in sparse-data conditions. They propose a randomization-based counterfactual. [IN FOLDER: chalfin-et-al-2021]

- **Bernasco & Steenbeek (2017) — generalized Lorenz curves.** Made the same point from a different angle: standard Gini coefficients and cumulative proportion metrics are biased when places outnumber crimes. Their generalized versions correct for this. [IN FOLDER: s10940-016-9324-7 (1).pdf]

- **Andresen & Weisburd (2025) — the metric wars.** Compared seven metrics. The 50-X metric (50% of crime in X% of places) is the most parsimonious and policy-relevant. Critics using the inverted 5-X metric (Hipp & Kim) produce artificially wide bandwidths that make concentration look inconsistent — but converting back to 50-X restores the narrow range. The debate is real, but some of the apparent disagreement is about metric choice, not substance. [IN FOLDER: s10940-025-09620-y.pdf]

- **Eck et al. — "Compared to what?"** Crime concentrates at about the same level as many other spatial phenomena. If concentration is not distinctive to crime, the policy implications may be different than proponents suggest. [IN FOLDER: s40163-017-0070-4.pdf — Use this carefully. It's a powerful challenge to the exceptionalism narrative around crime concentration.]

- **Geocoding errors and address-level aggregation.** The difference between crimes that *happen at* a place and crimes that *get assigned to* a place. Geocoding can assign crimes to the nearest intersection or block centroid. Address-level data inherits whatever the reporting officer entered. These are organizational routines producing data, not a neutral window onto reality.

- **Reporting biases.** Crimes that go unreported don't appear in the data. Places with higher reporting rates (due to demographics, police presence, 911 access, institutional presence) will appear more crime-prone. This is especially concerning for property crime and disorder.

- **Taylor, Lockwood & Wyant (2024) — the 311 example.** 311 litter-complaint shifts predicted later assessed litter, but so did theoretically *unrelated* 311 categories (information requests). The pattern suggests 311 calls reflect a general propensity to call — driven by demographics and technology access — not specific on-the-ground conditions. If "big data" proxies lack discriminant validity, data-driven policing may be optimizing on noise. [IN FOLDER: Can streetblock 311 physical incivility call count shifts...]

- **The denominator problem.** Concentrated relative to *what*? Per unit of area, per resident, per opportunity, per visitor? A nightlife district may look concentrated per area but normal per visitor. A residential block may look concentrated per capita but unremarkable per housing unit. The choice of denominator shapes the finding. [LOOK UP: Andresen, M.A. (2006). "Crime Measures and the Spatial Analysis of Criminal Activity." *British Journal of Criminology*, 46(2), 258–285. — A good reference on the ambient population / denominator problem.]

### Structural note:
This is the most methodologically dense section. Don't let it become a laundry list of technical complaints. The through-line is: *administrative data is not a neutral window onto reality, and the metrics we use to summarize it embed assumptions that shape our conclusions.* Every point should connect back to that.

## 6. POLICY UPTAKE AND ITS DISCONTENTS

**Opening move:** The primary policy translation of crime concentration research has been hot spots policing — directing patrol resources to the places where crime clusters. The evidence that it "works" is strong by criminological standards. The evidence that it works *well enough, equitably enough, and sustainably enough* is weaker.

### Key arguments:

- **The meta-analytic evidence.** Braga, Papachristos & Hureau (2014); Braga, Turchan & Papachristos (2019). Multiple meta-analyses show statistically significant crime reductions from hot spots policing, with little evidence of displacement and some evidence of diffusion of benefits. By the standards of the Campbell Collaboration, this is among the best-supported interventions in criminal justice. [LOOK UP: Braga, A.A., Turchan, B. & Papachristos, A.V. (2019). "Hot Spots Policing of Small Geographic Areas Effects on Crime." *Campbell Systematic Reviews*, 15(3). Also Braga, A.A., Papachristos, A.V. & Hureau, D.M. (2014). "The Effects of Hot Spots Policing on Crime: An Updated Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Justice Quarterly*, 31(4), 633–663.]

- **The underspecified treatment problem.** The literature tells you *where* to send officers but not *what to do* when they get there. "Hot spots policing" is not a single intervention — it encompasses directed patrol, problem-oriented policing, saturation patrol, and community engagement, among others. Most experimental evaluations bundle these together or leave treatment details vague. The "what works" question is really "what works where, doing what, for how long?" [LOOK UP: Weisburd, D., Telep, C.W. & Braga, A.A. (2010). "The Importance of Place in Policing." Report for the Swedish National Council for Crime Prevention. Also consider: Groff, E.R. et al. (2015). "Does What Police Do at Hot Spots Matter?" *JQC*, 31(3), 413–439.]

- **The police concentration question.** How concentrated is policing *on* crime? The spatiotemporal alignment between police presence and crime occurrence may be weaker than the hot spots framework assumes. [IN FOLDER: 2021_03_04_Police Concentration_Preprint.pdf — Use this to question whether the policy translation is as straightforward as proponents claim.]

- **The legitimacy critique.** Concentrating enforcement in the same places — which tend to be low-income, minority neighborhoods — over and over has costs that the experimental literature largely ignores. Procedural justice research (Tyler, 2006) suggests that *how* policing is conducted matters as much as *where*. If hot spots policing erodes trust, the long-term costs may outweigh short-term crime reductions. [LOOK UP: Tyler, T.R. (2006). *Why People Obey the Law*. Princeton University Press. Also Rosenbaum, D.P. (2006). "The Limits of Hot Spots Policing." In Weisburd & Braga (eds.), *Police Innovation*.]

- **Displacement vs. diffusion.** The evidence leans toward diffusion of benefits (crime reductions spreading beyond the targeted area) rather than displacement (crime moving elsewhere). But this evidence comes from the same studies that are evaluating the intervention — it's not independent. And the absence of displacement in the short term doesn't preclude it over longer periods or across different crime types.

- **The equity problem.** Hot spots policing as currently practiced raises distributional justice questions. The places that receive the most police attention are disproportionately communities of color. Even if the intervention reduces crime, the collateral consequences — stops, searches, arrests for minor offenses, surveillance — fall on residents who are not committing the crimes. The concentration literature provides the *rationale* for this pattern but not the *reckoning* with its costs. [NOTE: This is a point where your thesis can make a contribution — if it addresses how prediction interacts with equity, flag that here.]

### Structural note:
This section is where the review becomes most directly policy-relevant. The tone should be: *the evidence base is real and impressive, but the gap between "hot spots policing reduces crime in randomized experiments" and "hot spots policing is good policy at scale" is wider than the field often acknowledges.*

## 7. THE FRONTIER

**Opening move:** If the empirical finding is well established and the policy translation is mature (if imperfect), where does the field go from here?

### Candidates for the frontier:

- **Harm-weighted concentration.** Not all crimes are equal. A street segment with 50 thefts and one with 5 homicides look the same in standard concentration metrics but represent very different public safety problems. Harm indices (Cambridge Crime Harm Index, etc.) weight crimes by severity and reveal a different geography. The question: does harm concentrate more tightly or more diffusely than crime counts? [LOOK UP: Sherman, L.W., Neyroud, P.W. & Neyroud, E. (2016). "The Cambridge Crime Harm Index: Measuring Total Harm from Crime Based on Sentencing Data." *Policing*, 10(3), 171–183. Also: Mitchell, R.J. (2019). "Frequency versus Volume of Crime at Places." *JQC*, 35(4), 825–853.]

- **Temporal dynamics.** How fast do hot spots form? How fast do they dissolve? What are the early warning signs of an emerging hot spot? Most of the literature is retrospective — it describes where crime *was* concentrated. The frontier is genuinely prospective: can we identify places *on their way up* before they fully materialize? This connects directly to the "preceded by ≠ predicted by" argument in Section 4. [IN FOLDER: s10940-021-09533-6.pdf — weekly crime concentration — has some relevant temporal analysis.]

- **The relationship between micro-place concentration and broader spatial processes.** Schnell, DeWitt & Spencer (2024) argue that neighborhoods are not passive containers but actively shape how micro-place concentration operates. The next step is to model these cross-level dynamics explicitly — not just partition variance between levels but specify the mechanisms by which neighborhood context produces (or prevents) micro-level clustering. [IN FOLDER: schnell-et-al-2022]

- **Prospective vs. retrospective targeting.** The current evidence base is overwhelmingly retrospective: we know crime concentrated *there* last year, so we deploy *there* this year. But Levin et al. (2017) show that the specific hot segments shift. If the geography is more mobile than the aggregate statistics suggest, retrospective targeting will always lag. The field needs methods — and theories — that support genuinely prospective identification. [NOTE: If your thesis involves predictive modeling or forecasting, this is your direct landing zone. Make this point sharply.]

- **Investment and the built environment.** Tillyer, Acolin & Walter (2025) show that residential investment reduces crime, with effects strongest in disadvantaged neighborhoods. This suggests a non-enforcement path to reducing concentration: change the place rather than policing the place. But the gentrification-displacement tension is real. [IN FOLDER: 1-s2.0-S0264275124007066-main.pdf]

- **The data revolution.** Google Street View, satellite imagery, sensor networks, Waze data (used by Díaz et al.), social media geotagging — these offer alternatives to administrative crime data with its reporting and geocoding biases. But they introduce their own biases (digital divide, algorithmic mediation). The frontier is methodological pluralism, not just bigger data.

- **From concentration to explanation.** Ultimately, the field needs to move from "crime concentrates" (a description) to "crime concentrates *because*" (an explanation). This means testing specific mechanisms — place management, guardianship, opportunity, physical design, social cohesion — at the micro level with designs capable of supporting causal inference. The quasi-experimental work (Díaz et al.) is a model, but there's precious little of it.

### Structural note:
This section should feel forward-looking without being speculative. Each candidate for "the frontier" should be grounded in a limitation or gap identified earlier in the review. The reader should finish thinking: *these are the questions that need answering* — and your thesis should be positioned to answer at least one of them.

### Recurring themes to thread throughout:
- **The tension between empirical regularity and theoretical explanation.** The finding is strong; the theory is weak.
- **The unit of analysis question.** Addresses vs. segments vs. blocks vs. neighborhoods — each reveals different patterns.
- **The measurement-substance blur.** How much concentration is "real" vs. how much is a product of how we count.
- **The retrospective-prospective gap.** Describing where crime was ≠ predicting where it will be.
- **The policy-equity tension.** Effective intervention vs. fair intervention.
