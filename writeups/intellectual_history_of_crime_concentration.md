# The Intellectual History of Crime Concentration

A guide to the scholarly tradition that produced the "law of crime concentration at places," tracing ideas from their origins to the current frontier.

---

## 1. The Prehistory: Crime as a Property of Communities (1830s-1980s)

The spatial study of crime is old. Guerry (1833) and Quetelet (1842) mapped crime across French departments in the nineteenth century, launching what would become a long tradition of linking geography to deviance. For over a century, the dominant unit of analysis was the community or neighborhood.

The Chicago School solidified this tradition. Shaw and McKay (1942) demonstrated that delinquency rates remained stable in certain Chicago neighborhoods even as their ethnic composition turned over entirely, arguing that social disorganization — poverty, residential instability, ethnic heterogeneity — was a property of places, not people. This was a radical insight for its time, but the "place" in question was still the neighborhood: a collection of hundreds or thousands of addresses treated as a single unit.

For the next four decades, criminology largely stayed at this resolution. The question was *which neighborhoods* had high crime, and the answer pointed to structural disadvantage.

---

## 2. The Opportunity Turn: From Motivation to Convergence (1960s-1990s)

Three theoretical currents shifted attention from why offenders are motivated to where and when criminal opportunities arise.

**Jane Jacobs (1961)** argued in *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* that the physical design of streets and buildings shapes informal social control. Busy sidewalks with mixed uses generate "eyes on the street" — natural surveillance that deters crime. Empty, single-use spaces invite it. This was the first influential argument that micro-level physical features of places matter for crime, independent of who lives there.

**Routine Activities Theory.** Cohen and Felson (1979) proposed that crime requires the convergence in space and time of three minimal elements: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian. They used this framework to explain a puzzle: why did crime rise from 1947 to 1974 despite improving social conditions? Because routine activities dispersed people away from households, increasing the availability of unguarded targets. The theory was originally macrosociological — explaining national trends — but its spatial logic was immediately applicable to micro-places. If crime requires convergence, then the places where offenders, targets, and absent guardians reliably intersect should concentrate crime (Cohen & Felson, 1979).

**Crime Pattern Theory.** Brantingham and Brantingham (1993) introduced a geometric framework: nodes (where people spend time), paths (how they travel between nodes), and edges (boundaries between land uses). Offenders encounter criminal opportunities where their awareness spaces intersect with suitable targets — which happens at predictable spatial locations. This explained *why* crime concentrates at specific micro-places rather than being evenly distributed.

**Situational Crime Prevention.** Clarke (1995) extended opportunity theory into an actionable prevention framework, demonstrating that modifying the immediate environment — increasing effort, increasing risks, reducing rewards — could prevent crime at specific locations with minimal displacement. The accumulating evidence suggested that rather than simply moving crime elsewhere, place-based interventions often produced a "diffusion of benefits" to nearby untreated locations.

Eck and Weisburd (1995) synthesized these three streams — rational choice, routine activities, and crime pattern theory — into a unified framework for understanding crime at places. They distinguished the emerging "micro" approach (addresses, street segments, establishments) from the older "macro" tradition (neighborhoods, communities), arguing that micro-places deserved independent theoretical and empirical attention.

---

## 3. The Empirical Breakthrough: Minneapolis (1989)

Sherman, Gartin, and Buerger (1989) provided the first city-wide empirical demonstration of extreme micro-place concentration. Analyzing 323,000 calls for service in Minneapolis during 1986, they found that **3% of places generated 50% of police calls**. The finding was striking because crime was six times more concentrated by place than by individual offender — a comparison to Wolfgang's classic finding that 18% of individuals produce 50% of arrests.

Sherman (1995) built on this by proposing that places have "criminal careers" analogous to individual offending careers, with six dimensions: onset, recurrence, frequency, career length, intermittency, and crime-type specialization. He introduced the concept of "place managers" — people who regulate behavior at specific locations (drawn from Eck, 1994) — alongside the existing framework of guardians (who protect targets) and handlers (who control offenders). The policy implication was clear: place-focused interventions are more tractable than offender- or community-level strategies because places are stationary and experimentally manipulable (Sherman, 1995).

## 4. From Finding to "Law": Formalization (2004-2015)

### The Seattle Trajectory Study

Weisburd, Bushway, Lum, and Yang (2004) conducted the foundational longitudinal study. Tracking 29,849 street segments in Seattle over 14 years (1989-2002), they found that 4-5% of segments accounted for about 50% of crime incidents in *every single year*. More remarkably, 84% of all street segments followed stable crime trajectories — their crime levels barely changed. Only about 2% of segments belonged to increasing trajectory groups. The crime drop in Seattle was not a general citywide phenomenon but was driven by a relatively small group of micro-places with declining trajectories. Street segments of all types — stable, increasing, decreasing — were spread throughout the city, not clustered in single neighborhoods (Weisburd et al., 2004).

### The Sutherland Address

Weisburd (2015) — delivered as the 2014 Sutherland Address to the American Society of Criminology — formalized the finding as the **"Law of Crime Concentration at Places"**: for a defined measure of crime at a specific microgeographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages for a defined cumulative proportion of crime. Across eight cities using street segments as the common unit, 50% of crime was concentrated in approximately 4-6% of street segments. This bandwidth held across time despite strong volatility in total crime — in Seattle, despite a 24% decline, concentration remained between 4.7-6% (Weisburd, 2015).

Weisburd also documented how marginal the criminology of place remained within the discipline: only 4.3% of empirical articles in *Criminology* from 1990-2014 examined microgeographic units, compared with 66.1% focused on people. The address was a call for criminology to "take another turn in direction" (Weisburd, 2015, p. 151).

### The Variance Decomposition Approach

Steenbeek and Weisburd (2016) pioneered the multilevel variance decomposition approach in The Hague (2001-2009), showing that 58-69% of the variability of crime was attributable to street segments, with most remaining variability at the district level (24-38%). The intermediate neighborhood level accounted for only 5-7% — adding little beyond what micro and macro levels explained. This directly supported the micro-place focus of the law.

## 5. The Replication Wave and Theoretical Unification (2015-2017)

### The 2017 JQC Special Issue

A 2017 special issue of the *Journal of Quantitative Criminology* simultaneously replicated and complicated the law across multiple cities and methodologies:

- **Schnell, Braga, and Piza (2017)** replicated the variance decomposition in Chicago, finding that street segments account for 56-65% of violent crime variability — closely matching The Hague results. During the crime decline, the micro-place share *increased*.

- **O'Brien and Winship (2017)** pushed the grain even finer in Boston, finding that less than 1% of addresses generated 25% of all crime and disorder reports, with 95-99% of variance at the address level.

- **Levin, Rosenfeld, and Deckard (2017)** introduced a critical distinction in St. Louis: **spatial inequality** (the overall concentration level) is highly stable over 15 years, but **spatial mobility** (which specific segments constitute the top 5%) shifts over time. The top 5% is not the same 5% year after year.

- **Hipp and Kim (2017)** mounted the primary critical challenge, finding considerable variability in concentration across 42 southern California cities. They identified four complications: no agreed definition of "narrow bandwidth," questionable comparability across different-sized cities, random variation inflating apparent concentration, and different temporal assumptions yielding different results.

- **Haberman, Sorg, and Ratcliffe (2017)** demonstrated in Philadelphia that concentration holds across all temporal scales — hour of day, day of week, and season — suggesting the pattern transcends both space and time.

- **Bernasco and Steenbeek (2017)** showed that standard Lorenz curves and Gini coefficients overestimate concentration in sparse-data situations, proposing generalized versions as corrections.

- **Andresen, Linning, and Malleson (2017)** confirmed spatial stability of property crime concentration in Vancouver despite significant crime drops over 2003-2013.

### Farrell's Crime Concentration Theory

Farrell (2015) proposed "crime concentration theory" as a unifying framework, arguing that concentration across offenders, victims, places, and products are all manifestations of a single underlying phenomenon following a J-curve distribution. This moved the field beyond documenting place-level patterns toward recognizing concentration as a fundamental property of crime across all units of analysis.

## 6. The Measurement Debates (2017-2025)

The replication wave revealed that *how* concentration is measured profoundly shapes what is found, generating three interrelated methodological debates.

### The Sparse-Data Problem

Chalfin, Kaplan, and Cuellar (2021) demonstrated that standard concentration metrics significantly overestimate crime concentration when the number of places far exceeds the number of crimes — a near-universal condition for serious crimes. NYC has approximately 120,000 street segments but only about 300 annual homicides; some apparent concentration is mathematically inevitable. Their randomization-based counterfactual reveals that substantive ("marginal") concentration, while real, is considerably weaker than raw metrics suggest.

Mohler, Brantingham, Carter, and Short (2019) offered a parametric alternative: modeling crime counts as negative binomial and computing Lorenz curves via the inferred continuous Gamma distribution. Applied to Chicago homicides, this method showed yearly concentration was less than half the level implied by standard estimators.

### The Metric Wars

Andresen and Weisburd (2025) argued that much apparent disagreement about the law's bandwidth stems from metric choice, not substance. Critics using the inverted 5-X metric (what proportion of crime falls in the top 5% of places) produce bandwidths ranging from 15-90%, making concentration appear inconsistent. Converting back to the 50-X form (what proportion of places contain 50% of crime) restores the narrow range.

### Is Crime Concentration Special?

Eck, Lee, O, and Martinez (2017) asked a disruptive question: compared to what? They showed that crime concentrates at roughly the same level as hospital visits, traffic accidents, code violations, and many other spatial phenomena. If the J-curve distribution is practically universal in spatial data, then crime concentration may be a generic spatial property rather than a distinctive insight from criminology — with different implications for policy than the "law" framing suggests.

## 7. International Replication (2015-2025)

The law has been tested across an expanding geographic range:

- **The Hague, Netherlands**: Steenbeek and Weisburd (2016) — 58-69% of variance at the segment level
- **Vancouver, Canada**: Andresen et al. (2017) — spatial stability despite the crime drop
- **Belgium**: Hardyns, Snaphaan, and Pauwels (2019) — concentration confirmed using 200m grid cells
- **Brazil**: de Melo, Matias, and Andresen (2015) — concentration holds but spatial patterns differ more across crime types than in North America
- **Tokyo and Osaka**: Amemiya and Ohyama (2019) — first test in low-crime Asian cities, concentration confirmed for eight property crime types
- **Tel Aviv-Jaffa**: Weisburd and Amram (2014) — concentration consistent with U.S. findings
- **Mexico City**: Prieto Curiel (2023) — weekly "heartbeat" patterns of concentration
- **Six U.S. cities**: Walter, Tillyer, and Acolin (2023) — 47-69% of segments maintained stable classifications across Chicago, LA, NYC, Philadelphia, San Antonio, and Seattle

Weisburd, Zastrowa, Kuen, and Andresen (2024) conducted the definitive systematic review of 47 papers reporting 49 estimates: the median finding is 4.5% of streets for 50% of crime, with an interquartile range of 3.2-5.7%.

---

## 8. The Multi-Level Turn: Neighborhoods Are Not Dead (2022-2024)

The micro-place revolution initially positioned itself against neighborhood-level criminology. But recent work argues the two levels are complementary, not competing.

Braga and Clarke (2014) proposed the integration: social disorganization theory explains variation *between* neighborhoods, while opportunity theory explains variation *within* neighborhoods at micro-places.

Schnell, DeWitt, and Spencer (2024) advanced this further, arguing that neighborhoods are not passive containers but actively shape how crime patterns form and propagate at micro-places. Two distinct transmission patterns emerged: neighborhood-scale transmission (crime spreading across a community area) and micro-place transmission (clustering tightly around specific locations).

O'Brien, Ciomek, and Tucker (2022) showed that neighborhoods have characteristic concentration profiles — two neighborhoods with identical crime rates can have very different concentration structures (one with a sharp hotspot, another with diffuse crime). Compositional factors like land-use diversity best explain these differences.

O'Brien and Ciomek (2023) found that the direct aggravation effect (disorder escalating to violence) operates most strongly at the address level, while neighborhood-level trends work through specific problem addresses. The cross-scale interaction, not either level alone, is where the action is.

## 9. Policing Applications: From Pattern to Practice

The concentration finding has been operationalized primarily through hot spots policing — directing patrol and enforcement resources to the small number of places producing the most crime.

Braga (2001) conducted the first systematic review of hot spots policing, finding consistent evidence that focused police interventions at crime hot spots are effective in reducing crime. Braga, Turchan, Papachristos, and Hureau (2019) updated this with a comprehensive meta-analysis of 65 studies (78 tests), confirming statistically significant crime reductions with minimal displacement — and in many cases, a diffusion of benefits to surrounding areas.

Groff, Ratcliffe, Haberman, Sorg, Joyce, and Taylor (2015) demonstrated through the Philadelphia Policing Tactics Experiment that *what* police do at hot spots matters. Offender-focused tactics produced 42% reductions in violent crime, while foot patrol and problem-oriented policing did not achieve significant effects. The concentration finding alone does not prescribe the intervention; it identifies the where, not the how.

Connealy and Toman (2025) used Google Street View to assess the environmental characteristics of hot spots longitudinally, finding that individual hot spots vary in their stability trajectories even when aggregate patterns appear static. This has implications for the durability of policing strategies targeting specific locations.

## 10. Current Frontiers (2022-2026)

The field has largely moved beyond documenting concentration and now focuses on explanation, mechanism, and boundary conditions.

### Causal Mechanisms

Diaz, Contreras, Magaloni, and Weintraub (2025) conducted one of the few quasi-experimental mechanism tests, exploiting the temporal variation of open-air markets in Mexico City to test Jacobs' "eyes on the street" hypothesis — and partially refuted it, finding that market activity increased some crime types rather than suppressing them.

Tillyer, Walter, and Acolin (2025) demonstrated that neighborhood investment reduces crime at the street-segment level, suggesting a non-enforcement pathway for hot-spot intervention.

### Aggregation Artifacts

The question of what the "law" actually measures has intensified. When researchers aggregate multiple crime types into "total crime" or "7 Major Felonies," mathematical mechanisms — diversification across imperfectly correlated crime types, volume dominance by high-count categories, zero-inflation reduction — mechanically compress concentration metrics into a narrow bandwidth regardless of the underlying distributions. The tight 4-6% range may be partly a property of aggregation, not solely of crime geography.

### Data Validity

Taylor, Ratcliffe, and Perenzin (2024) demonstrated that 311 calls lack discriminant validity — theoretically unrelated categories predict conditions equally well as relevant ones, challenging studies that use calls-for-service or 311 data as disorder proxies. Andresen (2006) showed that the choice of population denominator (residential vs. ambient) substantially affects conclusions about spatial concentration, a concern amplified in cities like New York where daytime and nighttime populations diverge dramatically.

---

## 11. Open Questions

Several fundamental questions remain unresolved:

1. **How much concentration is real versus artifactual?** The sparse-data critique (Chalfin et al., 2021; Bernasco & Steenbeek, 2017; Mohler et al., 2019) and the aggregation artifact question both suggest that standard metrics overstate concentration. But how much?

2. **Is concentration distinctive to crime?** If all spatial phenomena concentrate similarly (Eck et al., 2017), the policy rationale for crime-specific hot spots strategies is weakened.

3. **What mechanisms produce and sustain concentration?** The field has documented the pattern extensively but tested causal mechanisms rarely. More experimental and quasi-experimental work is needed.

4. **How do micro-places and neighborhoods interact?** The emerging consensus is that both levels matter, but the specific transmission mechanisms remain underspecified.

5. **What are the equity implications?** Hot spots policing concentrates enforcement in low-income, minority communities. The corpus contains almost no direct measurement of collateral consequences alongside crime outcomes.
