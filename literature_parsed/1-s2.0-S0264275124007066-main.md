A B S T R A C T

Research suggests that investment in the form of structural home repairs, new construction, and home renovation is associated with crime reductions in residential areas. The current study examines how the relationship between investment and crime on residential street segments is moderated by neighborhood characteristics using longitudinal data from six large U.S. cities to better understand how investment strategies can be targeted to maximize public safety benefits. We estimate spatial Durbin models with street segment and census tract by year fixed effects to examine the effects of one measure of direct investment, building permits, on changes in crime at street segments and the extent to which such effects vary by census tract-level concentrated disadvantage, residential stability, median housing values, percent non-White, percent foreign-born, and population density. Findings reveal a significant negative relationship between permit activity and changes in crime in all cities. With few exceptions, effects were strongest among segments in the densest neighborhoods with the highest percentage of foreign-born residents where concentrated disadvantage is high and residential stability and median home values are low. The moderating influence of the racial and ethnic residential population varied across cities. We discuss implications, including avoiding unintended consequences associated with investment.

1. Introduction

A recent report from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2022) on reducing racial inequality in crime and justice calls for policies and programs to address historic patterns of disinvestment and underinvestment in communities that have produced a range of harmful outcomes, including high rates of crime and victimization. Such investment strategies are promising. They have the potential to improve neighborhood conditions and associated social problems, reduce racial and ethnic disparities with respect to crime and criminal justice system involvement, and produce more sustained reductions in crime over time (Krivo, 2014). Research on housing and the built environment suggests that investment is one potential mechanism for enhancing public safety through place-based improvements (Krivo, 2014; MacDonald, 2015; MacDonald et al., 2019; South et al., 2021). Investment may serve to reverse neighborhood physical decline and the structural antecedents of crime by removing physical disorder that increases perceptions of weak informal social control among potential offenders (Wilson & Kelling, 1982; Skogan, 2012), strengthening social

ties and informal social control among residents (Sampson et al., 1997; Saporu et al., 2011; Velez et al., 2012), and creating incentives for place managers to protect their capital investment by addressing the underlying criminogenic conditions of their properties and the surrounding area (Eck, 2018a, 2018b; Eck et al., 2023; Linning & Eck, 2021; Madensen, 2007). Indeed, research examining various indicators of residential investment – including mortgage lending, house repairs, abandon house remediation, renovations – indicates that such efforts are associated with reductions in crime (e.g., Saporu et al., 2011; South et al., 2021; South, MacDonald, Tam, Ridgeway, & Branas, 2023; Velez et al., 2012; Velez & Richardson, 2012).

Given the growing body of research indicating public safety gains of physical improvements, scholars have begun to examine whether neighborhoods differentially benefit from investment. While evidence is mixed, some studies suggest effects are amplified in the most disadvantaged areas that have historically lacked resources (Boggess & Chamberlain, 2021; Ramey & Shrider, 2014; Saporu et al., 2011; but see Velez et al., 2012). To date, the limited research on how the effects of investment on crime vary across neighborhoods has largely been

Yet the well-established body of work on spatiotemporal crime patterns demonstrates that crime concentrates at relatively few micro-places (e.g., addresses or street segments) within cities, with considerable street-to-street variability in crime within neighborhoods (Groff et al., 2010; O’Brien & Winship, 2017; Walter et al., 2023; Wheeler et al., 2016), thus suggesting that some mechanisms that produce spatiotemporal crime patterns likely operate at smaller spatial scales. In short, while neighborhood characteristics might moderate the effects of residential investment on crime, the empirical reality of spatiotemporal variability in crime calls for a micro-level, longitudinal approach that measures where investment is directed in the neighborhood and the degree to which it is spatially aligned with changes in crime over time, while also accounting for the spatial relationships among micro-places.

Previous research at the micro-level demonstrates building permits are significantly associated with reductions in crime, with spatial diffusion of crime prevention benefits to nearby street segments (Tillyer et al., 2023), and the greatest public safety benefits on segments with high initial crime levels (Walter et al., 2024). However, it is unknown whether neighborhood characteristics amplify or depress the crime prevention benefits of investment. The present study builds on this work to advance our understanding of how the relationship between investment and crime on residential street segments is moderated by neighborhood characteristics using data from six large U.S. cities (Chicago, Los Angeles, New York City, Philadelphia, San Antonio, and Seattle) over an 11-year period (2008–2018). We use building permits as a direct measure of physical investment. Municipal administrative building permit data include new construction, additions, renovations, and demolition – all of which are indicators of physical property investment (O’Brien & Montgomery, 2015). We use longitudinal data to estimate spatial Durbin models with street segment and census tract by year fixed effects to examine the effects of building permits on changes in crime on residential street segments over time and the extent to which such effects vary by neighborhood characteristics, including concentrated disadvantage, residential stability, median housing value, percent nonWhite residential population, percent foreign-born, and population density. This approach allows us to control for potential unmeasured time invariant confounders, thus minimizing sources of bias (Allison, 2005). Findings are instructive for integrating public safety, urban planning, and community development efforts to efficiently allocate scarce resources to optimize community benefits.

2. Investment for safer communities

Place-based initiatives have the potential to alter the physical environment in a way that improves social outcomes without the need for ongoing intervention (MacDonald et al., 2019). Investment represents one possible avenue for sustained place-based improvements that may address the underlying criminogenic conditions of the built environment (MacDonald, 2015). While much of the theorizing in this area has focused on financial investment in the form of mortgage lending, the explicated theoretical mechanisms from this body of work are relevant for understanding how physical investment influences crime in communities more broadly. Velez et al. (2012), for example, argue that mortgage lending may reduce violence by halting neighborhood physical decline processes that produce the structural antecedents of violence, while strengthening the housing and economic infrastructure (see also Velez, 2001, 2009). In other words, these financial investments are expected to reduce criminal activity via improvements to the physical environment. They also posit that the positive framing of the neighborhood promotes social ties and informal social control, with residents more willing to intervene on behalf of the common good (see also Peterson & Krivo, 2010; Sampson et al., 1997; Saporu et al., 2011; Velez & Lyons, 2014).

Conversely, signs of community disinvestment – in the form of

foreclosures, vacancies, withdrawn mortgage lending – can accelerate processes of urban decline, whereby property owners struggle to sell their homes due to limited market demand coupled with buyers’ inability to secure financing. Subsequent property abandonment produces more vacant and dilapidated properties and physical disorder, thus creating conditions favorable for crime (see Skogan, 2012 for review). Broken windows theory (Wilson & Kelling, 1982) suggests physical disorder signals that residents are unwilling or unable to effectively exercise control, thus attracting potential offenders because of perceptions of increased opportunities for crime in an area lacking guardianship. Disorder may also increase fear among residents, leading them to withdraw and avoid one another, which also weakens controls. Hipp et al. (2019), for example, found that older housing was associated with higher crime, positing that housing age can serve as a proxy for physical disorder, in that housing units generally experience dilapidation over time.

There is a growing body of research to suggest that mortgage lending and other forms of investment – including physical property upgrades and improvements, housing investment strategies, abandoned house remediation, and broader urban revitalization – are associated with crime reduction (Alonso et al., 2019; Bunting, 2020; Kreager et al., 2011; Ramey & Shrider, 2014; Shrider & Ramey, 2018; Saporu et al., 2011; South et al., 2021; South, MacDonald, Tam, Ridgeway, & Branas, 2023; Velez et al., 2012; Velez & Richardson, 2012). Velez et al. (2012), for example, report a statistically significant and negative relationship between mortgage lending and violent crime across 118 census tracts in Seattle over 27 years. Similarly, Velez and Richardson (2012) found Chicago census tracts in the 1990s with higher home mortgage loan dollars by banks experienced lower homicide rates. Conversely, there is evidence that indicators of community disinvestment – such as foreclosures, vacancies, and mortgage denials – are associated with increases in crime (Boessen & Chamberlain, 2017; Ellen et al., 2013; Kirk, 2020).

2.1. Heterogeneous effects of investment?

The observed negative relationship between investment and crime raises the question of whether investment is uniformly associated with crime reductions, or if some residential areas differentially benefit from investment. A significant body of literature explores the profound consequences of historical disinvestment in neighborhoods. This phenomenon has resulted in entrenched concentrated disadvantage and segregation within these communities characterized by low property values, residential instability, and high concentrations of households of color and immigrants (e.g., Jargowsky, 1997; Massey & Denton, 1993; Wilson, 1987). Communities with such characteristics often suffer from social disorganization that limits the ability to regulate behavior (Bursik Jr, 1988; Shaw & McKay, 1942); residents lack mutual trust and the willingness to intervene on behalf of the common good, thus undermining social control (Sampson et al., 1997).

Investment, therefore, may be particularly impactful in communities that have historically lacked resources. Velez (2001) has argued that disadvantaged communities have a “low floor” of public social control and connection, so even modest infusion of resources in the form of economic or political investment will produce meaningful change in formal and informal social control (see also Saporu et al., 2011). Conversely, because more affluent communities enjoy a “high ceiling” on access to resources and social control, similar modest investments are unlikely to yield comparable public safety benefits in these communities.

There is some evidence that increased investment is more strongly associated with crime reductions in more disadvantaged neighborhoods and those with larger African American and Latino populations. Seattle’s Neighborhood Matching Fund program, which provides matching funds for neighborhood improvement projects, was more strongly and negatively related to violent crime in poorer neighborhoods (Ramey &

Shrider, 2014). Saporu et al. (2011) examined the relationship between residential loans and crime across 8000 census tracts in 87 U.S. cities. While all neighborhoods enjoyed public safety benefits from residential loans, the effect was stronger in predominantly African American and Latino areas, as well as more disadvantaged communities. Boggess and Chamberlain (2021), in their analysis of home mortgage loans and intimate partner violence in Cleveland, Ohio from 1995 to 2010, found that the number of intimate partner violence incidents in a census tract significantly decreased as the average dollar amount of investment increased. The relationship was strongest for incidents perpetrated against women and incidents in the most disadvantaged areas. However, one study exploring the relationship between investment and crime found no evidence that neighborhood characteristics moderate the relationship. Velez et al. (2012), in their study of mortgage lending and violent crime across 118 census tracts in Seattle over 27 years, report that a series of interaction terms between mortgage lending, racial composition, and economic conditions were not significant, leading the researchers to conclude that “the effects of mortgage investment on violent crime operate similarly across racial, ethnic, and economic regimes in Seattle neighborhoods” (2012, p. 1045).

While the evidence on how neighborhood characteristics impact the effects of investment on crime is somewhat mixed, meso-level analyses do not account for where investments are made within neighborhoods, nor do they measure whether such investments align spatially with subsequent crime reductions within neighborhoods. In the section that follows, we briefly summarize the literature on spatiotemporal crime patterns, including evidence of street-to-street variability in crime within neighborhoods, which suggests the social and physical processes that produce the relationship between investment and crime may operate at smaller spatial scales. Indeed, crime and place scholars have argued that some aspects of collective efficacy likely operate predominately at the street segment level (Groff, 2015; Groff et al., 2010; Weisburd et al., 2014), where a greater degree of familiarity among residents fosters social interaction, cooperation, and shared norms for problem solving. For example, in a recent study of street segments in Baltimore City, Maryland, Weisburd et al. (2020) found that people who live in crime hot spots report much lower levels of collective efficacy relative to those who live on streets that are not hot spots, net of street and community level controls.

2.2. Investment and crime: moving from meso to micro

The study of crime and place over the last 30 years has documented that crime concentrates at small spatial scales (Andresen & Malleson, 2011; Braga et al., 2010, 2011; Curman et al., 2015; Gill et al., 2017; Hipp & Kim, 2017; Lee et al., 2017; O’Brien, 2019; Sherman et al., 1989; Walter et al., 2023; Weisburd, 2011, 2015; Weisburd et al., 2004; Weisburd & Amram, 2014) and such patterns tend to be stable over time (Curman et al., 2015; Groff et al., 2010; Weisburd et al., 2004; Weisburd et al., 2009). Changes in crime at relatively few micro-places can contribute substantially to aggregate city-wide changes in crime (e.g., Braga et al., 2010; Braga et al., 2011; Weisburd et al., 2004), thus signaling a need to sharpen our spatial focus and investigate the sources of micro-level changes in crime. While criminological theory and research around geospatial crime patterns historically existed at the meso-level (e.g., Bursik Jr & Grasmick, 1993; Sampson et al., 1997; Shaw & McKay, 1942), the empirical reality of within-neighborhood crime heterogeneity has shifted calls for intervention targeted at smaller spatial scales, such as hot spots policing (Ariel et al., 2020; Braga et al., 2019). As Weisburd (2011) and others have noted, however, the police are not the only or best source of place-based improvements. Nonpolicing interventions that address the underlying criminogenic conditions of places may produce more sustainable crime reductions (MacDonald et al., 2019).

More recent research has begun to examine the relationship between investment in the form of demolition, structural repairs, renovation, and

new construction and crime at smaller spatial scales. South et al. (2021) examined the association between the City of Philadelphia’s Basic Systems Repair Program (BSRP) and crime. The program offered grants of less than $20,000 to low-income homeowners for structural repairs to address electrical, plumbing, heating, and roofing damage. The researchers used a difference-in-differences design to compare block faces with BSRP-funded homes to block faces with BSRP-eligible homes that were on the waitlist to receive funding. The presence of a BSRP-funded home was associated with a 21.9 % reduction in block face crime, with the magnitude of the observed effect increasing with higher numbers of BSRP-funded homes on the block face (South et al., 2021). Kim and Wo (2021) examined the short-term effects of building demolition on crime on Los Angeles blocks using one-week periods and found the relationship varied depending on the stage of demolition, land use (residential vs. commercial), and crime type. For example, the presence of a permitted demolition (rather than the subsequent completed demolition) was associated with reductions in residential burglary one week later, and effects were significant for residential demolition permits but not commercial permits.

The present study uses building permits as a measure of physical property investment. Physical property investment involves tangible assets that directly influence the built environment. Unlike financial investments, which are intangible, highly liquid, and may be shorterterm, physical property investments are characterized by their longterm nature and less liquidity. As a result, physical property investments are especially relevant when examining the public safety benefits associated with improvements to the built environment. O’Brien and Montgomery (2015) argue that building permits are a measure of proactive investment (see also Lacoe et al., 2018), representing an indicator of a thriving community in contrast to measures of neighborhood disorder that capture the other end of the spectrum. Building permits are required by municipalities for various improvements to the built environment, including new construction, demolition, renovation, and improvements to existing properties, thus going beyond routine maintenance to represent direct physical investment. O’Brien and Montgomery (2015) speculate that individuals who invest in a neighborhood are less likely to create disorder and more likely to take steps to minimize it. Moreover, investment may reflect care for the neighborhood and inspire others to do the same (see Keizer et al., 2013).

Linning and Eck’s (2021) “Neo-Jacobian” perspective is instructive for understanding why building permits may be associated with crime reductions at the micro-scale in particular (see also Eck et al., 2023). They argue that Jane Jacobs’ (1961) influential concept of “eyes on the street” mainly references the eyes of property owners, who establish order through proprietary place management. These place managers, who may be resident homeowners or others who own or manage property, are the primary source of social control whose deliberate actions shape or remove criminal opportunities. Investment, therefore, may directly improve the physical conditions of places, as well as create additional financial incentive for place managers to address criminal opportunities at and immediately surrounding their properties via the organization of space, conduct regulation, access control, and resource acquisition (Eck, 2018a, 2018b; Eck & Madensen, 2018; Madensen, 2007; Sampson et al., 2010), as such actors seek to protect their investment from the disamenity of crime (Alexander, 2021). These scholars argue that Jacobs’s (1961) work is consistent with a bottom-up microspatial explanation of neighborhood crime patterns, whereby specific places (i.e., property parcels and street segments) with clearly defined boundaries and powers of control influence areas around them (Eck et al., 2023; Linning & Eck, 2021). In turn, reductions in crime may attract additional investment that increases the value of their initial investment (Lacoe et al., 2018).

Consistent with this perspective, Tillyer et al. (2023) examined the relationship between building permits and changes in crime on street segments in Chicago, Los Angeles, New York City, Philadelphia, San Antonio, and Seattle, while also measuring spatial spillover to nearby

segments to account for places and place management that may affect nearby locations. Building permits were significantly and negatively associated with changes in crime on street segments in the subsequent year across all six cities. Moreover, nearby segments appeared to benefit from building permit activity, with significant, though smaller, reductions in crime observed on adjacent segments in five of six cities. In a recent study, Walter et al. (2024) report that the public safety benefits associated with building permits are greatest among street segments with the highest initial crime levels; given the availability of crime data in most jurisdictions, cities can use crime levels when developing community economic development plans to maximize public safety benefits. In sum, these studies demonstrated 1) a relationship between building permits and crime at the microscale, 2) the public safety benefits of investment vary in strength.

Informed by the aforementioned meso-level studies reporting some heterogeneous effects of investment across communities, the present study builds on Tillyer et al. (2023) and Walter et al. (2024) by incorporating Census data to investigate the potential sources of variability in the microscale public safety benefits of physical property investment. Specifically, we explore the moderating influence of residential community characteristics on the relationship between investment and crime on street segments. This is consistent with recent theoretical and empirical work highlighting multilevel sources of crime and the conditioning influence of neighborhood characteristics on the effects of place and street segment characteristics on crime (e.g., Deryol & Payne, 2021; Jones & Pridemore, 2019; Lanfear, 2022; Tillyer, Wilcox, & Walter, 2021; Wilcox & Tillyer, 2018).

3. The present study

The present study advances knowledge on the potential moderating influence of neighborhood characteristics on the relationship between investment and crime using both longitudinal data available at small spatial scales and meso-level data. Recent micro-level studies suggest investment at small spatial scales is associated with crime reduction, though there is variability in the observed public safety benefits (Tillyer et al., 2023; Walter et al., 2024); and meso-level work indicates there are reasons to believe that the benefits from an infusion of physical investment may be particularly strong in some communities. Drawing on Velez’s (2001) work, we hypothesize that communities that have experienced historic disinvestment that weakens public social control and connection will benefit the most from investment (see also Saporu et al., 2011). Six economically, topographically, and culturally diverse large cities with different building permit activity and crime levels and patterns were selected to test the hypotheses across contexts. Our focus is on understanding how non-policing interventions in the form of investments that produce physical improvements to the built environment might be strategically targeted to create safer residential areas. Using building permits as an indicator of proactive physical investment that may motivate place management and halt urban decline, we explore whether crime reductive benefits of investment are amplified in neighborhoods with characteristics historically associated with disinvestment and insufficient resources. Specifically, neighborhoods that have experienced historic disinvestment are often marked by concentrated disadvantage, residential instability, low median housing values, nonWhite resident populations, foreign-born residents, and high population density (Jargowsky, 1997; Massey & Denton, 1993; Wilson, 1987), and so we hypothesize that the crime reductive benefits of investment will be strongest in neighborhoods with these characteristics. Informed by the well-established empirical literature on spatiotemporal crime patterns, our analyses account for where in the neighborhood the investment and crime occur by measuring both at the street segment level.

The present study tests the following hypotheses:

H1. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with high levels of concentrated

disadvantage. H2. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with low levels of residential stability. H3. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with low median housing values.

H4. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with a higher percentage of non-White residents.

H5. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with a higher percentage of foreign-born residents.

H6. Building permits are more negatively related to crime on street segments in neighborhoods with high population density.

3.1. Data and measures

We test the hypotheses using data from six large U.S. cities: Chicago, Los Angeles, New York City, Philadelphia, San Antonio, and Seattle. These six diverse municipalities in terms of location, socioeconomic and demographic characteristics, and urban structure allow for cross-city comparisons to determine if the moderating influences of neighborhood characteristics on the relationship between investment and crime on street segments are consistent across cities. The dependent variable is the number of crime incidents, and the independent variable is the number of building permits in the previous year. Crime incident data with geocoordinates and dates were obtained from each city’s police department.1 Building permit data with geocoordinates and dates were obtained from each city’s building department.2 The building permit data include new construction, alterations, demolition, and substantial renovations or repair of buildings. The study period spans from 2008 to 2018.3

The unit of analysis is residential street segments. Street segments are defined as the length of a street until it intersects another street and includes properties and the events that occur on those properties on both sides of the street. Street shapefiles were obtained from the geographic information system (GIS) portals of each municipality and processed into segments with unique identification numbers. The sample inclusion criteria are all segments with only residential properties based on Zillow’s Transaction and Assessment Database (ZTRAX). Furthermore, given the use of street segment fixed effects, any segments that had no changes in crime and building permits over the course of the study period were dropped from the sample because there is no variation on those segments to measure and this study examines changes over time. This resulted in analytical samples ranging from 9266 to 36,483 residential street segments across the different cities. The crime incidents and building permits were aggregated annually to the nearest street segment using a spatial join.

The neighborhood characteristics were collected from the 2006–2010 American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year estimates at the census tract level, capturing the first few years of the study period and establishing a baseline for neighborhood conditions. The means are higher on the neighboring segments since they reflect all neighboring segments (i.e., the average street segment has six neighboring segments).

There is considerable variation across the six cities in median house values, from an average of less than $110,000 in San Antonio to $509,061 in New York City and $587,897 in Los Angeles. 4. Discussion

Amid recent calls to address historic patterns of disinvestment in communities (National Academies of Science, Engineering, and Medicine), and informed by the extensive empirical research documenting the spatial patterning of crime within communities (e.g., Groff, 2015; Sherman et al., 1989), the current study examined the moderating effects of neighborhood characteristics on the relationship between investment and crime on residential street segments using longitudinal building permit and crime incident data from six diverse U.S. cities. This approach revealed how a direct measure of physical investment is spatially aligned with changes in crime over time, and how the relationship varies by neighborhood characteristics, while also accounting for the spatial relationships among segments. The findings suggest opportunities to integrate public safety, urban planning, and community development efforts, which we discuss more fully below.

Consistent with previous work (Tillyer et al., 2023), the results reveal a significant negative relationship between permit activity and yearly changes in crime on residential street segments in Chicago, Los Angeles, New York City, Philadelphia, San Antonio, and Seattle. Moreover, the observed effects of investment on crime along residential street segments appear to be spatially specific, with no evidence of immediate spatial displacement or diffusion of benefits to nearby locations. That is, the relationships between building permits on neighboring segments and changes in crime were negative across all six cities, but none of these effects reached statistical significance. The current study’s findings expand this area of research by investigating the moderating influence of neighborhood characteristics on this relationship. With few exceptions, the effects were strongest among street segments in neighborhoods with the highest level of concentrated disadvantage and where residential stability and median home values are low. They were also generally strongest in the densest neighborhoods with the highest percentage of foreign-born residents. The moderating influence of the racial and ethnic residential population was less consistent across cities. In Los Angeles, Philadelphia, and San Antonio, the effect of building permits on crime was strongest among street segments in census tracts with a higher percentage of non-White residents, whereas the effects were strongest for street segments in areas with a lower percentage of non-White residents in Chicago, New York City, and Seattle. Additional research using more nuanced measures is needed to better understand the varying effects of resident racial and ethnic composition on the public safety benefits of investment. That said, the current study’s findings suggest socioeconomic, housing, and immigration measures are reliable across contexts in identifying locations that are likely to benefit the most from investment.

While the present study was not designed as an explicit test of theory, the findings do offer some implications for theory and suggest areas for future research. First, the significant negative relationship between building permits and crime in all six cities is consistent with theoretical arguments that investment can incentivize property owners to exercise more effective place management and address conditions that create criminal opportunities to protect their investment (Eck, 2018a, 2018b; Linning & Eck, 2021; Madensen, 2007). Future research should test this and other mechanisms to further unpack the negative relationship between building permits and crime. Second, cross-city differences notwithstanding, the overall general pattern of findings is consistent with theoretical arguments that investment is most impactful in historically under-resourced communities, where even small investments can strengthen formal and informal social control in a way not observed in more affluent communities that already enjoy access to resources and have a foundation of social control (Saporu et al., 2011; Velez, 2001). Similar to recent criminological theory and research (e.g., Deryol & Payne, 2021; Jones & Pridemore, 2019; Lanfear, 2022; Tillyer, Wilcox, & Walter, 2021; Wilcox & Tillyer, 2018), these heterogeneous effects of investment across communities affirm the need for multilevel explanations of spatiotemporal crime patterns that consider the moderating

influence of neighborhood characteristics on place and street level effects. Specifically, the lack of spatial spillover effects of building permits to nearby places in our study underscores the importance of a microspatial approach that measures where investments are made in communities when studying public safety benefits that might flow from place-based improvements. Additionally, the significantly different effects of building permits on crime observed across terciles highlight the importance of accounting for the broader meso-level context that can moderate such relationships.

Finally, while there are many benefits to using building permit data to capture direct physical investment (including the availability of longitudinal, microlevel data to measure the location of investments over time within communities), not all of the study cities provide the same level of detail about building permits. Therefore, the current study restricted the measure of investment to permit counts. We did this to prioritize the inclusion of all six cities in the analyses and establish an initial empirical foundation for community context-specific effects of investment on public safety that suggest directions for future inquiry. As such, the building permits measure should be viewed as a baseline indicator of physical property investment that could potentially be masking differential effects. Just as the findings reported above demonstrate that the effects of permits vary by neighborhood conditions, it is also possible that effects vary by permit type (i.e., new construction, alterations, demolitions, and substantial renovations or repair of buildings) and dollar value. Indeed, as noted above, recent work by Kim and Wo (2021) highlights nuanced effects even when examining a single permit type (i.e., demolitions), with variability observed across demolition stage, demolition type, and crime types (see also Wo & Kim, 2021). Additionally, a recent study by Mitre-Becerril and MacDonald (2024) found no evidence of short-term changes in crime in Philadelphia following the enactment of a new zoning code that led to reductions in two specific types of permits - zoning and construction permits in specific council member districts. These authors posit that the null effects could be due to the fact that the properties that did not get permitted due to the new zoning code were largely upmarket residential buildings; had they observed decreases in affordable housing developments, they would have expected negative impacts on crime. While untested, this line of reasoning is consistent with the current study’s findings that the relationship between building permits and crime is strongest among places located in the most disadvantaged communities. In short, the current study contributes to a relatively new line of inquiry that investigates the conditions that produce the greatest public safety returns on investment. To this end, future research, limited to cities where such data are reliably collected, should explore whether the type of permit and dollar value of improvement affect the magnitude of crime reduction associated with investment.

4.1. Implications and conclusions

This research suggests that placed-based initiatives may yield the greatest public safety benefits among residential street segments in the most under-resourced communities within cities. Yet new construction and redevelopment initiatives risk unintended, negative consequences, including the displacement of current residents via gentrification. Increased investment in an area raises property values and rents, which may push long-time residents out and lead to an influx of higher-income residents (Hwang & Lin, 2016). In addition to the obvious negative consequences from gentrification (e.g., perpetuating residential segregation, diminishing affordable housing options in prime locations, increasing risk of housing instability and homelessness for low-income households), research reveals additional unintended effects, such as a reduction in political participation, negative impacts on credit scores, decline in cultural diversity, and poor health outcomes (e.g., Chum, 2015; Ding & Hwang, 2016; Knotts & Haspel, 2006; Richardson et al., 2019; Smith et al., 2020; Smith & Thrope Jr., 2020).

Therefore, given our findings indicate building permit activity in

under-resourced communities produces the greatest public safety benefits, public officials and policymakers should consider increased funding for placed-based initiatives on residential street segments in these areas that support improvement to the built environment for current residents to prevent their displacement. The following development activities are examples of such initiatives that may have the greatest public safety benefits without forced relocation: 1) home repair programs, 2) new development incentives tied to stipulations with community benefit agreements, and 3) physical improvement remediation programs. We discuss each of these in detail below.

Home repair programs provide loans or grants to income-qualified homeowners to make improvements to their homes. Eligible repairs (many of which require a building permit) generally consist of accessibility modifications, kitchen upgrades, electrical or plumbing repairs, foundation replacement, or other structural repairs. Homeowners receive financial assistance in the form of cash grants or low interest, deferred interest, or forgivable loans with generous terms. Local jurisdictions offer these programs with funds that are generated from their own revenue sources such as housing trust funds or with federal funds that are administered at the local level such as the HOME Investment Partnerships Program or Community Development Block Grant Program, of which home repair programs are an allowable activity. The City of Philadelphia’s Basic Systems Repair Program (BSRP) is one example, where a significant reduction in crime was observed with even minor structural repairs (grants for less than $20,000 were provided to BSRPeligible homeowners) (South et al., 2021). In programs with limited funding, like BSRP, income eligibility thresholds dictate how resources are distributed. For the greatest public safety benefit, local governments may want to consider targeting resources based on income and to residential streets in inner-city neighborhoods where concentrated disadvantage is high and residential stability and median home values are low.

To attract new construction and major improvements on underresourced street segments without resident displacement, local governments can use a range of targeted incentives. Examples of incentives that attract residential developers include expedited permitting (i.e., shorter timeframes for construction permits to be issued, which reduces development costs); permitting fee waivers (to reduce or eliminate the costs of permitting as long as certain project criteria are met); or density bonuses (to allow developers to increase the number of dwelling units or size of the dwelling) (Homsy & Kang, 2023; National Multifamily Housing Council, 2018). It is important for these incentives to be tied to requirements that benefit current residents. This can be in the form of eligibility requirements, such as quality affordable housing or renovation activities that preserve housing affordability. Another potential mechanism includes a community benefits agreement, which is a “legally binding contract (or set of related contracts), setting forth a range of community benefits regarding a development project, and resulting from substantial community involvement” (Gross, 2008, p. 37). Designed properly, community benefits agreements direct resources that are responsive to the needs of local residents and prompt public accountability in the redevelopment process (Marantz, 2015; WolfPowers, 2010).

Finally, local governments can support physical improvement remediation programs. Similar to home repair programs, these programs provide cash grants and favorable loan terms to address unsafe conditions created by dilapidated homes, vacant properties, and dangerous structures. Instead of merely issuing code violations when a violation is found, funding along with a remedial team can be provided to the property owner to bring the property back to productive use and in compliance with current building codes and standards. Remediation of vacant lots and abandoned buildings can lead to significant reductions in crime (e.g., Cui et al., 2022; Kondo et al., 2015; Spader et al., 2016).

In short, it is vital that cities integrate public safety, urban planning, and community development efforts to address the underlying criminogenic condition of places through non-policing interventions

(MacDonald et al., 2019; MacDonald & Stokes, 2020). While the aforementioned are promising examples of development initiatives aimed at improving public safety for existing residents, it is important for future research to consider relevant intended and unintended consequences that may affect local residents. To this end, evaluations of place-based initiatives should be comprehensive, measuring a range of potential outcomes, including residential stability, public safety, health, and economic outcomes (Tillyer, Walter, & Acolin, 2021).
