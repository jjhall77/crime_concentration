Abstract

The empirical observation that a small number of micro places generate the bulk of urban crime problems has become a criminological axiom. Explanations for the persistence of high-crime places have traditionally drawn upon opportunity theories of crime. In a new book, Weisburd, Groff, and Yang suggest that social disorganization could also be a powerful explanation for the uneven distribution of crime within neighborhoods. In this article, we explain briefly why their empirical work considerably sharpens knowledge about crime concentrations in the city. We then offer a critique of their conclusions concerning the relative contributions of social and situational variables in explaining crime hot spots and the preventive implications they

Introduction

Explaining the variation of crime within cities has been an enduring area of scientific inquiry in criminology (Bursik and Grasmick 1993; Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls 1997; Shaw and McKay 1942). Social disorganization theory suggests that variations in crime within cities are impacted by community-level structural factors and mediated in important ways by informal social controls (Sampson and Groves 1989). In past volumes of the Journal of Research in Crime and Delinquency, criminologists have examined the potential explanatory power of social disorganization theory in understanding why certain types of crime or different levels of criminality are found in some communities as contrasted with others or how communitylevel variables, such as relative deprivation, low socioeconomic status, or lack of economic opportunity may affect individual criminality (e.g., see Agnew 1999; Gottfredson, McNeil, and Gottfredson 1991; Schuessler and Slatin 1964; Smith and Jarjoura 1988). In most cases, social disorganization research has focused on explaining crime at the ‘‘macro’’ level, often studying cities (Baumer 1994), counties (Bachman 1991), and neighborhoods (Lowenkamp, Cullen, and Pratt 2003).

Against this background, some criminologists had noted that crime is concentrated at specific micro places within neighborhoods (Hawley 1944, 1950; Shaw and McKay 1942). It was only with the advent of powerful computer systems and software packages in the late 1980s, however, that researchers assembled strong evidence that crime is highly concentrated in a relatively small number of crime hot spots (Pierce, Spaar, and Briggs 1988; Sherman, Gartin, and Buerger 1989). These hot spots of crime have been defined as clusters of street addresses, groupings of street blocks, and particular street intersections and street segments (Weisburd, Wim Bernasco, and Bruinsma 2009). Importantly, hot spots have been found in neighborhoods characterized by both low and high levels of social

disadvantage (Sherman, Gartin, and Buerger 1989; Weisburd and Green 1994). More recently, researchers have demonstrated that the concentration of crime at particular places is highly stable over long periods of time (Weisburd et al. 2004).

Criminalopportunitiesatplacesareoftenviewedaskeyfactorsinexplaining persistent clusters of criminal events. In their review of theory-based empirical research, Eck and Weisburd (1995) identified four types of research evidence that illuminate the role of criminal opportunities at crime places. Facilitiessuch as bars, churches, and apartment buildings—have been found to affect crime rates in their immediate environment depending on the type of people attracted,thewaythespaceismanaged,orthepossiblecrimecontrollerspresent such as owners, security, or police. Features such as easy access, lack of guardians, inept or improper management, and the presence of valuable items influence the decisions that offenders make about the places they choose to commit their crimes. Similarly, research on offender mobility, showing that offenders are attracted to areas with many potential targets and move from places with few targets to places with many targets, hasbeen interpretedasevidence of‘‘rationaland deliberate target searching behavior and the influence of personal characteristics and the distribution of crime targets on this behavior’’ (Eck and Weisburd 1995:16). Finally, studies of offender interviews have concluded that their target selection decision-making processes exhibit bounded rationality. For instance, Rengert and Wasilchick’s (2000) research on residential burglars revealed that these offenders seek places with cues that indicate acceptable risks and gains, such

- as homes that are located on the outskirts of affluent neighborhoods. Opportunity theories of crime, such as routine activity (Cohen and Fel-


son 1979), rational choice (Cornish and Clarke 1986), and crime pattern theory (Brantingham and Brantingham 1991), have often been used to understand the place characteristics, situations, and dynamics that cause criminal events to concentrate at particular places. Some authors, however, have suggested that social disorganization and routine activity theories are complementary and should be integrated (Miethe and Meier 1990). Indeed, both theories partially overlap in their treatment of social control as a key element in crime occurrences. In addition, Rice and Smith (2002) show how combining routine activity and social disorganization theories greatly improves the predictive power of spatial analyses to explain variations in auto theft incidents at block faces in a midsized southeastern U.S. city.

In a recent book, Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) build upon these studies not only to refine and strengthen the previous observations, but to

propose an explanation for the observed crime concentrations, which integrates concepts from social disorganization and opportunity theories of crime. Their research represents an important step in criminological research and we anticipate that it will inspire new theory-based empirical research in this area. In this article, we explain briefly why their empirical work considerably sharpens knowledge about crime concentrations in the city. We then present four issues that need to be addressed as research in this important area moves forward. Finally, we suggest new research that could invigorate the debate on the formation and persistence of highcrime places and could support interventions that seek to change microlevel precipitators and facilitators of crime.

Street Segments and Hot Spots of Crime in the City

In their book, Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) extend earlier work in Seattle by examining the concentration and stability of crime at street segments over a period of 16 years. Their longitudinal analysis found that only 1 percent of the street segments accounted for 23 percent of the crime in Seattle during the study time period. The choice of street segments for their analysis is a significant contribution. As Felson has explained, streets have a direct role in the production of crime:

The street belongs to everyone, hence is supervised by no one, except for an occasional policeman who does not know who belongs there anyway. The very system that fosters easy movement and vast opportunity for good experiences also interferes with informal social control of youths and protection of person and property from intruders. And so the street system exposes people to serendipity and calamity. Streets not only provide the means for drawing sustenance from an urban environment, but also constitute its organ of growth. (Felson 1987:917)

In his extension of behavior settings theory to street segments, Taylor

- (1997) suggests that people who frequent particular blocks get to know each other, become familiar with each other’s routines, play particular roles in the life of the block, and develop shared norms about acceptable behavior on the block. These micro-level behavior settings represent the fundamental theoretical justification used by Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) in selecting street segments as their unit of analysis.


Furthermore, streets are tangible entities, and people know what street segments look like, whereas it is much harder to visualize the more abstract

concepts of a cluster of addresses or a grouping of street blocks. Researchers select these latter forms of concentration because they can create them using widely available crime mapping packages. They are also useful for police because they can define where patrols might be focused. It is unclear, however, whether clusters of addresses or a group of street blocks hold much meaning for city officials who might wish to consider other possible interventions, such social services and opportunity provision programs, to reduce crime concentrations. We discuss this further below.

In fact, street segments better serve Weisburd, Groff, and Yang’s (2012) overall purpose of establishing the relative contributions of ‘‘social’’ and ‘‘situational’’ factors to crime concentrations than, for example, clusters of addresses. As we show below there are many situational variables that could help explain concentrations of crime in street segments and, this also the case, but to a lesser to extent, of social variables. Groupings of street blocks, on the other hand, might favor the collection of ‘‘social’’ variables because convenient measures of these can be abstracted from U.S. Census block group records. However, it is unclear which situational measures might be collected for street block groupings and which of these might have relevance to explanatory theory.

We would argue, therefore, that a significant advantage of street segments over the other forms of ‘‘hot spots’’ is that it is possible to collect a great deal of information, within a single study, about both social and situational variables related to crime concentrations. This enables comparative testing of theoretical explanations and consequent development of preventive actions. Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) attempt to do this by using multinomial regression to examine the relative importance of situational opportunities and social characteristics in distinguishing chronic hot streets other locations. They found that variables supporting both opportunity and social disorganization theories of crime were associated with persistent high-crime streets. Their opportunity measures such as increases in motivated offenders (the presence of high-risk juveniles), suitable targets (the presence of employees, residents, and public facilities), and accessibility and urban form (arterial roads and bus stops), were among the best predictors of problem streets.

They also found that some of their measures of social disorganization were associated with these streets. While mixed land use, racial heterogeneity, and urbanization were not found to be significant risk factors, property values and housing assistance measures were associated with persistently high levels of crime on street segments. The ‘‘physical disorder’’ social disorganization measure was strongly related to high-crime street segments.

This variable counted the total number of reported litter, weeds, graffiti, dilapidated homes, junk storage, inoperable cars on the street, and other physical incivilities. The number of truant juveniles residing on street segments, representing a lack regulation on the street, was also a powerful predictor of recurring high-crime street segments. Finally, ‘‘collective efficacy,’’ as measured by the proportion of active voters in the street segment population, was also found to be strongly associated with the persistence of high levels of crime at street segments.

To our knowledge, Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) are the first to use trajectory analysis to identify chronic crime hot spots and then assemble and analyze a wide array of micro-level data to identify risk and protective factors that predict the persistence of crime at these places. They demonstrate that a few streets within both low and high disadvantage neighborhoods consistently account for a bulk of crime in those larger community areas. These persistent high-crime streets have place-level dynamics and characteristics that distinguish them from low crime places in the same neighborhoods. Put simply, the exclusive study of larger community units, such as U.S. Census tracts and block groups, misses much of the reality of urban crime problems that vary from street to street.

Having acknowledged the considerable empirical contributions made by Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012), we now turn to a critique of their conclusions concerning the relative contributions of social and situational variables in explaining the concentration and persistence of crime at a small number of street segments in the city and the preventive implications they draw from these findings, particularly the novel claim that attempts should be made to strengthen ‘‘collective efficacy’’ (Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls 1997)

- at these locations. In summary, we argue it is not clear (1) that the situational variables they used adequately represent the great variety of those that might be implicated, (2) that their measures of social disorganization have established the importance of collective efficacy as an explanation for the concentration of crime on certain street segments, (3) that there are sound theoretical grounds for thinking that collective efficacy could importantly operate at the level of the street, as well as at neighborhood level, and (4) collective efficacy could be manipulated to reduce crime concentrations on street segments. Our critique is presented as a call for further research in four important areas.


The Need for a Broader Selection of Situational Variables

In such a large and complex study, it was perhaps inevitable that Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) would be compelled to rely on readily available

data for making their comparisons of the influence of social and situational variables at persistently high-crime street segments. Within the constraints of this limitation, they showed considerable ingenuity in measuring relevant variables. Like most previous studies of the persistence of high-crime places, they drew principally on routine activity theory (Cohen and Felson 1979) to select the situational variables they included in their analysis. Their measure of motivated offenders was the presence of high-risk juveniles, whereas those of suitable targets and guardians were the presence of employees, residents, and public facilities. These are just a few of the possible measures of routine activity theory that might influence crime on street segments, and they derive from its well-known formula of the three components of a criminal event. However, future research should include possible measures of the convergence of these three basic elements, such as Felson’s

- (1998) concept of juvenile hangouts. These measures would allow an analysis of what is arguably the central preoccupation of routine activity theory—the ebb and flow of these elements at different times of the day, of the week, and of the year. The ratios of ambient to residential populations at different times, especially day and night, and measures of pedestrian flows and vehicular traffic would be measures of these processes.


In fact, routine activity theory is just one of the opportunity theories that have been developed by environmental criminologists. Others that deal in situational variables that could help in explaining the persistence and concentration of crime in some street segments are crime pattern theory (Brantingham and Brantingham 1991), and the two closely linked concepts of the rational choice perspective (Cornish and Clarke 1986, 2006, 2003) and situational crime prevention (Clarke 1980, 1997, 2009; Cornish and Clarke 2003). Important concepts deriving from crime pattern theory include ‘‘crime facilitators’’ (such as street markets and shopping malls) that attract a large number of people of every kind, including offenders, and ‘‘crime attractors’’ (such as drug markets or prostitution strolls) that disproportionately attract those with criminal intent. Crime pattern theory also includes many more variables of urban form and accessibility that could be used in future inquiries. These other variables include street permeability (number of ways in/ways out; Beavon, Brantingham, and Brantingham 1994), street types (e.g., main roads, residential streets, bus routes, business, or retail streets), and street accessibility from major highways. An important concept derived from the rational choice perspective is that of ‘‘risky facilities’’ (poorly managed parking lots, bodegas, fast-food joints, massage parlors, high schools, housing projects, urban parks, motels, and pubs and taverns) that have sometimes been associated with increased risks of crime (Eck,

Clarke, and Guerette 2007; Felson 1987; Roncek 1981). These are frequently ‘‘repeat victims’’ of crime, another concept derived from rational choice (Farrell and Pease 1993).

Given the shared origins of opportunity theories, there is considerable overlap in some of these measures, but they still comprise a considerably longer list of possible situational determinants of crime than those available to Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012). In future investigations, the inclusion of a broader range of these measures seems likely to strengthen the associations that Weisburd, Groff, and Yang found between situational variables and high-crime streets. Admittedly, it would be difficult to collect some of these measures but a well-specified future study should, at least, attempt to collect such data.

The Need for Improved Measures of Social Disorganization and Collective Efficacy

Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) acknowledge some theoretical ambiguity in their measures of social disorganization and collective efficacy at the street segment level. The two key variables that they use to measure social disorganization at the street segment level—the presence of physical incivilities and truant juveniles—could also be conceptualized as measures of opportunity theory. The presence of at least some of the physical incivilities (e.g., litter and graffiti) may reflect high levels of ambient populations. More generally, high levels of physical incivilities might be less an indicator of a lack of social organization than of low levels of capable guardianship and/or low levels of appropriate place management. The number of truant juveniles on street segments could also be characterized as the presence of motivated offenders in the absence of intimate handlers (parents, teachers, and others who can control them). Indeed, the specification of the mechanisms that lead to crime problems suggested by physical disorder and truant juveniles in these micro contexts may be better represented by specific concepts from environmental criminology, routine activity, and rational choice theories than the broad observation that social disorganization undermines the ability of residents themselves to achieve public order through informal means.

Indeed, one might argue that the term ‘‘social disorganization’’ suffers fatally from its sheer lack of clarity. At the outset, Shaw and McKay (1942) used it as a catchall term for ‘‘the other side of the tracks.’’ Far from becoming more definitive over the years, it continued to widen its connotation and lose any consistent denotation. Measures differ from study to study,

often mixing independent and dependent variables, with the social disorganization label changing contents from day to day and journal to journal. In contrast, terms such as hot spots and routine activity, while not immune to definitional issues, are more coherent. Mixing unclear conceptualizations of social disorganization in potentially renders the latter relatively less useful.

Future theoretical investigations of crime and place may want to develop alternative measures of collective efficacy, which is generally defined as ‘‘social cohesion among neighbors combined with their willingness to intervene on behalf of the common good’’ (Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls 1997:918). Building on other inquiries (e.g., Putnam 2000), Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) use the percentage of active voters out of all registered voters on a street segment as their micro-level indicator of collective efficacy on a street segment. They suggest that those who participate in public elections may also be engaged in other aspects of public affairs. However, Sampson (2012) finds that levels of individual voting, while obviously civic, do not seem to define the collective civic engagement character of a neighborhood. Individual voters may or may not be engaged in the social fabric of a neighborhood in a way that leads to more effective monitoring of youth behavior, intervening in acts of truancy, or doing something about people who disturb public spaces.

This may be true of the potential for collective action at the street segment level as well. Other indicators, such as the presence of a block association or a tenant management organization, might be better measures of collective efficacy at specific streets within neighborhoods. Furthermore, there are other reasons why residents of high-crime streets might fail to vote. Many of these residents could be young, short-term renters, often living in apartments above stores, whose lifestyle and circumstances might not favor voting. Many of them might commute to work in nearby cities and might be more likely to dine and relax near their places of work than where they sleep. Consequently, they might find it difficult to get to the polling stations on the appointed day. Indeed, many might not have learned how to find their way to the polling station. None of these reasons need to imply that these residents have no commitment to where they live—only that their everyday lives make it difficult for them to vote.

The Need to Determine the Proper Theoretical Domain of Collective Efficacy

It is unclear whether the community-level concept of collective efficacy can adequately explain why a particular crime spot is persistently hot over time.

It is always dangerous to extend the application of theory (in this case, collective efficacy) beyond its intended domain (in this case, neighborhoods). The presence of community-based organizations, which draw membership from individuals from within and outside specific neighborhoods, predicts collective efficacy and collective civic action (Sampson 2012). These organizations, which include community newspapers, family planning clinics, alcohol/drug treatment centers, counseling or mentoring services (e.g., Big Brother), neighborhood watch groups, and other local agencies, typically act to ensure the well-being of larger community areas. Neighborhoods characterized by high levels of collective efficacy do have lower overall levels of violent crime (Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls 1997). However, neighborhoods that are generally less attractive to criminals can still have specific locations that present compelling crime opportunities and, as we have seen, evidence from Seattle and Boston also suggests that persistent crime hot spots are present in all neighborhoods (Braga, Papachristos, and Hureau 2010; Weisburd, Groff, and Yang 2012). Neighborhoods rich in community-based organizations do have malls where customers are robbed in parking lots, bars where drunken patrons assault each other, indoor drug markets regulated by violence, and other highly localized problems.

Sampson (2013) himself cautioned that smaller units, such as micro places, are not necessarily better than larger units, such as Census block groups, in understanding neighborhood social processes and figuring out how to mobilize collective efficacy. Sampson observes that micro places and conventional neighborhoods are nested within larger communities that are recognized or named by residents, external housing buyers, institutional actors such as real estate agents, and administrative agencies such as the police. He suggests that larger scale perceptual, organizational, and political factors govern how crime control strategies are implemented at micro places:

Razing a drug house or closing a rowdy bar is a complex act, for example, that is about much more than the place itself. It matters what neighborhood the house or bar is in—what ‘‘kinds of people’’ reside there, what the zoning laws are, how vocal the residents are, and how the decision makers view the area. (Sampson 2013:7)

At the micro place level, it may be more appropriate to articulate the potential for informal social control by the more straightforward concepts of guardianship and effective place management drawn from opportunity theories of crime. This is especially true of hot spots located on nonresidential streets. In Boston, only 1 percent of the city’s street segments and

intersections experienced 50 percent of the total number of commercial robberies between 1980 and 2008 (Braga, Hureau, and Papachristos 2011). These chronic commercial robbery hot spots were often nonresidential business strips located on major thoroughfares that facilitated easy access to customers. Business strips may have few residents, if any, but the level of robbery could be powerfully influenced by individual and collective action of business owners on the street. For instance, Business Improvement Districts are local private–public organizations that take monetary assessments from business owners and make investments by place promotion, street cleaning, and public safety. Key crime control activities involve the abatement of physical disorder and improved guardianship of the business area by nonpolice security guards. BIDs have been found to be effective in reducing crime in small areas (Cook and MacDonald 2011).

The Need to Determine Whether Collective Efficacy Can Be Increased at the Street Level

Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) argue that social and health interventions designed to address economic deprivation, unsupervised teens, and low collective efficacy may be more effective and less costly if concentrated at crime hot spots rather than spread across broader communities or neighborhoods. More significantly, they further speculate that formal social control programs, like hot spots policing and particular types of situational measures, may not be enough to alter the long-term trajectories of chronic crime hot spots. Rather, complementary programs to address the underlying social and structural characteristics of street segments may be necessary.

However, the potential crime control payoff of ‘‘lowering the scale’’ of social and health interventions to chronic hot streets remains unproven at present, and it is unclear whether strategies for stimulating collective efficacy in neighborhoods could be implemented at the street segment level. Sampson’s (2011) ideas for promoting stable and longlasting community-based organizations are broad, principle-based recommendations that provide little advice on the actual work of initiating or enhancing community-based groups in identifiable crime hot spots. Even if it proved possible to scale down these strategies to the street, little evidence exists concerning the effectiveness of increasing informal social controls by stimulating collective efficacy in socially disorganized areas, whether these locations are large neighborhoods or small street segments.

Taylor’s (1997) theoretical work on behavioral settings provides some ideas for promoting social control in small places, which he suggests could be achieved by modifying the daily routines followed by residents and business owners in these places. However, he draws primarily upon opportunity theories and situational crime prevention techniques to provide guidance on how crime places might be addressed in practice. For instance, he describes how advances in routine activity theory by Felson (1995) and others suggest promising avenues through which ‘‘intimate handlers’’ (such as relatives, friends, and acquaintances) can discourage likely offenders, guardians can protect specific targets, and managers can control places.

Informal social controls matter in crime prevention and control and there is some conceptual overlap in social disorganization and opportunity theories in the mechanisms through which informal social control can be achieved. For instance, Sampson (2011:226) suggests the key to fostering informal social controls and collective efficacy ‘‘is to increase positive connections among youth and adults in the community’’ and recommends initiatives such as parent surveillance/involvement in after-school and nighttime youth programs, adult youth mentoring systems, and organized supervision of leisure time youth activities. This idea of increasing adultyouth connections is congruent with the routine activities idea of increasing the number of intimate handlers of potential offenders (Felson 1986). However, in contrast to collective efficacy, situational prevention provides clear guidance to policymakers on how to achieve reductions in crime through the application of 25 techniques designed to increase the difficulties of crime, increase its risks, reduce its rewards, remove excuse for crime, and reduce temptations and provocations (Cornish and Clarke 2003). A recent systematic review of 206 tests of situational crime prevention measures found that 75 percent of the evaluated interventions were effective in reducing targeted crime problems (Guerette 2009). There are now nearly 250 evaluated applications of these techniques (see www.popcenter.org).

A next logical step would be to conduct a detailed analysis of high-crime street segments to uncover the specific social and situational characteristics that underlie the crime problems that cause these places to be hot spots. The analyses can be specifically tailored to understanding how informal social control (or the lack of it) is related to these underlying criminogenic conditions. The prospects of increasing collective efficacy to control crime at the street level, whether via increasing the connections of residents to local community-based organizations or some other strategy designed to induce collective efficacy, can then be considered.

A New Research Agenda for the Study of Crime Concentrations at Specific Places within Communities

We believe it is time social scientists began to develop appropriate data collection systems and measurements to capture the characteristics and dynamics of small areas such as street segments and intersections. One reason why the bulk of criminological inquiry on the distribution of crime across cities remains focused on larger areas such as neighborhoods is that very little formal data collection occurs at small places. Consequently, criminologists use readily available data collected by the U.S. Census Bureau at macro units of analysis—namely, tracts and block groups. If we fail to expand our community data collection efforts beyond what is readily available, criminologists will not meaningfully advance our understanding of urban crime problems.

It is beyond the scope of this brief article to specify the particulars of a new place-focused research agenda. However, we envisage that it would involve using a wider range of data sources than heretofore, collecting much more microdata and perhaps making use of a wider variety of research designs. Criminologists could make more use of the data that are already available to them. For instance, Google Street View can be used to create an inventory of the various structures, and their condition, on city streets (Fujita 2011). Computerized mapping software companies offer geocoded data files that document the addresses of schools, hospitals, bars, restaurants, subways, bus stops, banks, and a myriad of other place characteristics. Digitized Yellow Pages and Lotinfo (http://www.lotinfo.com/) hold similar possibilities. Much more detailed time use information that can be linked to victimization data is now available than few years ago (Lemieux and Felson 2012). CCTV cameras are in routine use in many streets and intersections in cities and these sometimes yield pictures of actual criminal events. Data produced by automatic number plate readers in use by police (Lum et al. 2011) could provide counts of cars on streets and some estimates of the numbers of these reported stolen. Police incident data capture systems, such as the U.S. Federal Bureau of Investigation’s National Incident-Based Reporting System (Maxfield 1999), routinely capture much more detailed information about crime events than the Uniform Crime Reports data used by most criminologists. Businesses, such as retail organizations, now often collect highly detailed information about shoplifting and other crimes affecting their operations and in some cases have proved willing to share this information with researchers (Smith 2013).

With the increasingly availability of huge data files, systematic subsetting is now possible, for example, very specific risks of different forms of auto theft, affecting different kinds of vehicles is possible. It is also possible for researchers to compare the effects of nighttime and daytime activities on crime. Finally, social scientists could attempt to acquire funds from federal funding agencies to support a large-scale data collection enterprise (e.g., a Project on Human Development in Chicago Neighborhoods focused on places). As for research designs, criminologists could make more use of matched case controlled designs to compare high- and low-crime street segments and intersections, and they could make greater use of randomized controlled designs to evaluate modifications made to streets and intersections. These designs would be much easier to implement than when the units of analysis comprise entire neighborhoods. In short, these suggestions should enrich place-level analyses of the risk and protective factors associated with crime.

Conclusion

Weisburd, Groff, and Yang’s (2012) research attempts to use collective efficacy to complement situational explanations to explain high-risk concentrations of crime in the city. Indeed, their work has the attraction of reconciling two disparate strands of criminological theory. Their findings open up the possibility of developing a new and potentially powerful research agenda focused on streets and intersections, not neighborhoods. The available empirical evidence seems to suggest that opportunity theory may be more fruitful than social disorganization when trying to explain crime at these micro places. We believe that future research in this area need to address the issues raised in this article to determine whether research on high-risk crime places should be broadened to integrate key concepts from social disorganization theory or whether this represents a less productive diversion that results in the neglect of some alternative strategies to address criminal opportunities at places.

Author Biographies

Anthony A. Braga is the Don M. Gottfredson Professor of Evidence-Based Criminology in the School of Criminal Justice at Rutgers University and Senior Research Fellow in the Program in Criminal Justice Policy and Management at Harvard University. His research focuses on working with criminal justice agencies to develop crime prevention strategies to deal with gang violence, illegal firearms markets, and violent crime hot spots.

Ronald V. Clarke is University Professor in the School of Criminal Justice at Rutgers University. Clarke worked for 15 years in the British government’s criminological research department, where he jointly developed the rational choice perspective on crime and helped to launch the British Crime Survey. He is also the associate director of the Center for Problem-Oriented Policing (www.popcen ter.org), an applied crime prevention institute funded by the U.S. Office of Community Oriented Policing Services.
