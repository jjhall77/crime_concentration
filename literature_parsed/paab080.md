# Does a Large Crime Decline Mean That Hot Spots of Crime Are No Longer ‘Hot’?: Evidence from a Study of New York City Street Segments

## David Weisburd* and Taryn Zastrow**

Abstract The law of crime concentration at places predicts that hot spot streets in a city will maintain very high crime levels even when there are strong crime drops in a city overall. We use New York City as a case study focusing on crime at street segments to illustrate this outcome. New York City experienced very large crime declines over the last quarter-century. Nonetheless, looking at the hot spot street segments that produce 25% and 50% of crime in 2010, 2015, and 2020, we find that many New York City streets continue to have very high levels of crime. In 2020, for example, over 1,100 street segments in the city evidenced more than 39 crime reports. These data suggest that the argument that a city can disengage from policing when overall crime rates are low, belies the reality that hot spots of crime are likely to continue to be ‘hot’ during such periods.

Researchers, policymakers, and the public ordinarily look to crime rates across cities to assess how safe citizens are in their daily lives. Accordingly, a ‘crime drop’ focuses on declines in overall city crime trends, just as a ‘crime wave’ is assessed by examining increasing city-wide crime trends. But the focus on city-wide trends masks a tremendous degree of diversity in localized, microgeographic levels of crime. Over the last two decades scholars have identified significant variability of crime within communities or neighbourhoods (e.g. see

Groff et al., 2010; Tita and Radil, 2010; Weisburd et al., 2012; Taylor, 2015; Steenbeek and Weisburd, 2016; Schnell et al., 2017). Often focusing on street segments (including both block faces of a street, intersection to intersection), scholars have shown that there is significant variability in crime street to street in cities (Groff et al., 2010; Weisburd et al., 2012; Steenbeek and Weisburd, 2016; Schnell et al., 2017).1

Indeed, they found that the crime decline was accounted for by just 14% of city streets (see also Weisburd et al., 2014). Most streets in the city experienced little change in crime, and indeed, about 2% of the street segments (representing more than 500 streets) experienced crime waves with average increases of almost 50%. Accordingly, in a period that is described by many as representing a ‘great American crime decline’ (Zimring, 2006; Sharkey, 2018), looking at localized crime trends at the street segment level suggests that the crime decline was limited to a relatively small number of streets in the city, and that, on many streets, crime increased significantly.

These findings illustrate the importance of digging deeper into localized trends if we want to understand how people who live in a city experience crime. They are reinforced by another important observation about crime in the city. Weisburd (2015), in his Sutherland Lecture to the American Society of Criminology in 2014, showed that there is tremendous consistency in the degree to which crime is concentrated at hot spots across cities (and across time in cities). Indeed, he argued that this consistency was so strong that it suggested a ‘law of crime concentration at places’, where in larger cities about 50% of crime was found at about 5% of streets, and about 25% of crime at about 1% of streets (see also Weisburd et al., 2012). This finding of strong concentrations of crime at microgeographic hot spots has become one of the key regularities of research on crime at

place (Telep and Weisburd, 2018).2 It suggests that the crime problem is experienced with the greatest intensity at a very small number of places in the city, and that in addressing crime problems we need to focus in particular on the top 1% (or 5%) of streets that produce about one-quarter (and one-half) of the crime problem.

We focus on these hot spot streets in New York City in 2010, 2015, and 2020. Over the last 30years, crime has declined to such a degree in New York City that it is easy to reach the conclusion that crime has become a marginal problem, or at least that it has declined to levels such that we need not place too much emphasis on crime control. In the early 1990s, there were more than 700,000 index crimes3 reported in the city each year (NYC Division of Criminal Justice Services). By 2010 that number had declined to less than 200,000 crimes. Between 2010 and 2019, New York City had an additional 11% decline in index crimes. Looking at these overall numbers provides a very encouraging view of the crime problem in New York City. Focusing however on the high crime hot spots in New York City that produce 25% and 50% of crime we find that there are still a large number of city streets with very high crime and violent crime levels. This suggests that even in cities that have very large overall crime declines, many streets will continue to have crime problems that need to be addressed by police and other agents of city government.

### Crime concentrations

The first observation we make from these data are that they follow closely the bandwidths for the law of crime concentration observed in prior studies (e.g. see Andresen and Linning, 2012; Weisburd and Amram, 2014; de Melo et al., 2015; Weisburd 2015; Jaitman and Ajzenman, 2016; Steenbeek and Weisburd, 2016; Andresen et al., 2017; Gill et al., 2017; Chalfin et al., 2019; Park, 2019). Looking at the proportion of streets that produce 25% and

Between 1.27% and 1.38% of city streets produce 25% of crime. This illustrates that crime is highly concentrated at crime hot spots in New York City.8 Moreover, despite an 11% decline in crime reports at street segments over the decade, the concentration of crime remains fairly stable (for similar findings, see Weisburd et al., 2004; Weisburd et al., 2012; Curman et al., 2015; Wheeler et al., 2016). Indeed, there is little difference in crime concentrations between the highest crime year (2010) and the lowest crime year (2020). For the 50% concentration criterion, 5.78% and 5.71% of streets produce 50% of crime in those years, and for the 25% level of concentration, for each year, about 1.4% of streets produce 25% of crime.

While our article is not focused on the stability of crime hot spots over time, the hot spots in our study follow patterns of stability noted in earlier studies (e.g. see Weisburd et al., 2004). We examined the streets that were in the highest hot spot group (that produced 25% of crime in the city) in 2010 and asked whether they continued to be crime hot spots in 2020. Of streets that were high crime hot spots in 2010 (top 25% of crime), 53% were high crime hot spots in 2020. An additional 37% of streets moved from the highest hot spot category to the second category (which produced from 25% to 50% of crime). This suggests that most of the streets that were hot spots as we have defined them in 2010 were also hot spots in 2020. There are many reasons for crime remaining relatively high at hot spots, including environmental factors such as population density or shopping areas that are strongly correlated with crime at place (Weisburd et al., 2014). These findings point to the importance of maintaining crime prevention efforts at places that have opportunities that make them continually vulnerable to crime.

It is notable that while many streets had high levels of crime during these years the large majority of street segments had relatively low levels of crime. The lowest quartile, producing the remaining 25% of crime in the city, included between 33,096 and 35,112 street segments over the three observation years. These streets averaged between 2.6 and 2.8 crimes per year. If we add to this group the street segments with no crime, we gain a total of over 68,000 streets in 2010 and over 72,000 streets in 2020. ### Discussion

The New York City crime decline has become a model for the ability of cities to successfully address crime problems. While in the 1990s, New York City was perceived as one of America’s most dangerous cities, today it is seen as one of America’s safest cities. But looking deeper at crime at street segments in New York City, we have shown that despite the substantial crime decline in New York City over the last 30 years, a large number of city streets still have very high levels of crime. In 2020, just over 1,100 street segments in the city evidenced more than 39 crime reports. These streets had average crime levels of more than 70 crimes. 858 streets had 10 or more violent crime reports that year. Clearly, there are still many streets in the city suffering from crime levels that need efficient, effective responses by city government. Looking at overall crime rates masks the extent to which New York City still has serious crime hot spots. The crime found on the 1%, or 5% of streets, that produce about 25% and 50% of the crime problem, are still at very high levels. We have no reason given the law of crime concentration to believe that this would not occur as well in other larger cities.

Given New York City’s crime declines over the last few decades, and that found in many other cities, it is tempting to view a radical reduction in policing as a reasonable policy outcome. While we think there is wide agreement on the necessity of police reform that would provide greater justice in the application of policing, including reducing unwarranted disparities in the application of policing to disadvantaged minority communities, our data suggest that the rush to defund the police does not recognize that many city streets have very high levels of crime and that policing remains an essential service for people who live in these crime hot spots.

Lum et al. (2021, p. 4) after analysing emergency calls to the police across nine police agencies conclude that the vast majority of requests for police service cannot be handled efficiently or effectively by other city agencies:

Our findings indicate that the amount and types of incidents for which people call the police are voluminous. Many seem to fall in categories not obviously transferable to other organizations or government sectors without significant resource expenditures, adjustments, and commitments by other entities. If such responsibilities were transferred, those calling for defunding need to consider possible costs to safety, community satisfaction, and the potential for disparity that might arise. However, if the police retain these responsibilities, they will also need to reconsider how to more effectively address many concerns for which people call the police.

Before concluding we want to note some caution in drawing inferences from our research in New York City. First, it is important to recognize that geographically coded crime data may represent social as well as objective realities. While citizens are the primary generator of crime data through emergency calls to the police (Lum et al., 2021; Weisburd and Majmundar, 2021), the final designation of crime incidents requires that police conclude a crime was committed. Citizen crime reporting may be affected by attitudes towards the police, or social and economic circumstances (Leibovitch, 2021). Police may influence crime data through their decisions to ‘found’ a crime call, or in their proactive efforts to respond to crimes. These biases are common to geographic studies of crime using official crime data, but we think it important to note it before concluding. At the same time, given the highly skewed nature of the distribution of crime across place, streets identified as hot spots are likely to have serious crime problems even if there are significant reporting biases (Weisburd and Majmundar, 2021).

Second, smaller cities or cities with lower crime rates may evidence much lower levels of crime on hot spot streets during a crime decline. Nonetheless, given evidence of a law of crime concentration, we think it likely that our data reflect in broad terms what would occur in other larger cities. Because crime is so concentrated, it means that even when overall crime rates decline, a large number of streets will continue to have high numbers of crimes.

### Conclusions

New York City provides a useful case study for examining crime problems on hot spot streets during a period of large crime declines. We find that despite precipitous declines in the overall crime rate in New York City, crime hot spots retain extremely high levels of crime and violent crime. This finding is predicted by the law of crime concentration (Weisburd, 2015) which posits that substantial proportions of crime are found at a relatively small number of streets. Given this reality, crime hot spots will continue to have worrying levels of crime even when overall crime rates are substantially reduced. In this context, the proposition that a city can disengage from policing when overall crime rates are low belies the reality that hot spots of crime are likely to continue to be ‘hot’ during such periods.
