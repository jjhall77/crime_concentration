#### Abstract

Objectives The tendency of businesses and residents to gravitate toward safer streets complicates efforts to assess the inﬂuence of informal surveillance (or eyes on the street) as a crime deterrent, as it is often intertwined with other factors such as target availability and neighborhood dynamics. We leverage a quasi-experimental setting of itinerant street markets to assess the impact of concentrated retail activity on property crime.

Methods We combine ofﬁcial records with user-generated trafﬁc data from Waze (available via Waze Discuss) to identify street market locations in Montevideo, Uruguay, and link them to over 15 years of georeferenced police reports. Using an imputation difference-indifferences design, we exploit temporal and spatial variation in street markets to estimate the causal effect of retail-driven activity on thefts and robberies.

Results Our ﬁndings show that while street markets lead to a signiﬁcant increase in the number of reported thefts, they have no measurable impact on robberies—a crime type that involves the use or threat of violence. The documented effects are concentrated in small geographic units with no substantial spillover into adjacent areas, indicating no crime risk diffusion. These effects are also more pronounced in larger markets, suggesting a mechanism tied to the absence of familiar strangers. Moreover, the effects on thefts are stronger in neighborhoods with shared socioeconomic vulnerabilities, offering insights for geographic proﬁling.

Conclusions Overall, our results underscore the interplay between urban mobility patterns and crime, pointing to the need for balanced urban policies that integrate formal surveillance with community-driven strategies.

Keywords Urban crime · Eyes on the street · Urban mobility · Spatial spillovers

This empirical regularity has been consistently documented across cities worldwide, spanning the Americas, Europe, Africa, and Asia (Amemiya and Ohyama 2019; Cummings et al. 2024; Schnell et al. 2017; Steenbeek and Weisburd 2016; Umar et al. 2021). Moreover, it holds across various metrics—such as prevalence, frequency, incident counts, and service calls—and across spatial units of analysis ranging from individual addresses and street segments to census tracts (Lee et al. 2017; O’Brien 2019).

However, the literature offers no consensus on its explanation. ## Urban Concentration and Crime

### Informal Control and Natural Surveillance

Urban concentration enhances informal social control and natural surveillance, thereby deterring street crime. According to Cohen and Felson’s (1979) routine activities theory, three conditions must be present for a crime to occur: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of capable guardians. In this framework, social control is closely tied to the presence of such guardians, as it can be exercised formally by the police or informally by community members who monitor public spaces and mitigate the risks associated with increased target availability (Cohen and Felson 1979; Felson and Eckert 2018).

Jacobs’ (1961) seminal work on urban planning highlights the critical role of active street surveillance as a natural mechanism for maintaining public order. Urban areas with a wellintegrated mix of residential and commercial areas foster eyes on the street, enhancing safety through informal social control. However, in contrast to routine activities theory, the eyes on the street perspective emphasizes that the most relevant informal guardians are not residents or passersby but property owners, managers, and shopkeepers who actively oversee and manage urban spaces. Unlike the incidental byproducts of pedestrian activity, these forms of informal control are more deliberate and goal-oriented, involving not only surveillance but also land use decisions, property maintenance and ownership (Linning and Eck 2021; Linning et al. 2022).

Empirical evidence from diverse settings underscores the protective role of informal guardians in public spaces. Browning and Jackson (2013) found a negative association between active streets and violence exposure in Chicago, provided street activity surpassed a certain threshold. Similarly, studies on the closure of recreational marijuana dispensaries and restaurants in Los Angeles (Chang and Jacobson 2017), the implementation of curfews that conﬁned minors and their caregivers indoors in Washington (Carr and Doleac 2018), or the deployment of a program of civil guardians along students’ routes to school in Chicago (McMillen et al. 2019) documented that visible, regular presence in public spaces plays a protective role against both violent and property crimes. More recently, Rosenthal and Urrego (2023) showed that increased retail agglomeration enhanced street activity, ampliﬁed surveillance, and reduced both crime and police stops in New York City.

Milgram’s(1972)notionoffamiliarstrangersextendedtheconceptofeyesonthestreetby emphasizing that repeated encounters among individuals without personal ties can nonetheless foster self-monitoring and a willingness to intervene, driven by mutual awareness and a shared sense of responsibility. In Brisbane, Zahnow (2023) found that train stations with more regular passengers had lower theft and property damage. This crime reduction can be

due to increased self-monitoring (Newton 2014; Newton et al. 2014) and a greater inclination to assist familiar individuals (Milgram 1972). However, the relationship between familiarity and crime reduction can be moderated by contextual factors such as neighborhood sociodemographics (Zahnow et al. 2023).

Neighborhood characteristics and their guardianship role have been used to explain the uneven geographical distribution of crime (Nagin et al. 2023). Low-crime areas are typically marked by higher levels of social control (Shaw and McKay 1942) and collective efﬁcacythat is, shared norms, mutual trust, and a willingness among residents to intervene for the common good (Sampson 2012). This relationship is supported by evidence from studies conducted in Chicago (Sampson et al. 1997), Stockholm (Sampson and Wikström 2008), and Brisbane (Mazerolle et al. 2010). More recently, Jones and Pridemore (2019) found that crime concentration in Lexington, Kentucky, was shaped by social disorganization and crime opportunities, and moderated by formal and informal guardianship.

### Crime Opportunities

Urban concentration can also expand criminal opportunities and lower the chances of detection and punishment. In line with Cohen and Felson’s (1979) routine activity approach, more active streets may promote criminal activity by facilitating the convergence of motivated offenders and suitable targets in the absence of capable guardians. Jarrell and Howsen’s (1990) extension of Becker’s (1968) model also posits that property crime increases with the number of strangers in an area, as the expected beneﬁts of offending grow: perpetrators are harder to apprehend when they can blend into larger crowds, and more potential victims are present. Freeman et al. (1996) further argue that urban agglomerations may attract a higher concentration of offenders, thereby reducing the likelihood of detection and arrest.

This alternative perspective also aligns with evidence that crime tends to be highly concentrated in small geographical units (Lee et al. 2017; Weisburd 2015). These areas foster criminal activity through two distinct mechanisms: crime generators—places such as parks, transit hubs, and commercial streets where non-criminal gatherings inadvertently create crime opportunities—and crime attractors—places such as drug markets, alcohol outlets, and nightlife areas, which draw motivated offenders due to speciﬁc criminal opportunities (Brantingham and Brantingham 1995).

Several studies have documented a positive link between street activity and crime. Jarrell and Howsen (1990) found the presence of outsiders (e.g., tourists, shoppers) was associated with higher rates of property crime in Kentucky, but not with violent offenses. Harries (2006) observed in Maryland that property crime increased with population density, while violent crime decreased, likely due to enhanced informal surveillance. Bernasco and Block (2011) showed that robbery rates in Chicago were higher in areas with ecological disadvantages like crime generators and transit access, with spillovers to adjacent areas. Wilcox and Eck (2011) argued that trafﬁc volume, rather than type, drives crime risk around neighborhood facilities. Supporting this, Tillyer and Walter (2019) and Zahnow and Corcoran (2021) found that businesses and bus stops with greater trafﬁc in San Antonio and Brisbane, respectively, experienced higher theft and crime rates, particularly when combined with environmental factors. The COVID-19 pandemic further illustrated these dynamics, as global mobility restrictions led to sharp declines in crimes such as theft, burglary, and robbery (Abrams 2021; Felson et al. 2020; Gerell et al. 2020; Koppel et al. 2023; Nivette et al. 2021).

## Research Design

Theoretical arguments and empirical evidence suggest that the relationship between land use, pedestrian activity, and crime is uncertain. While active streets are often associated with informal surveillance and reduced crime rates, the increased opportunities for criminal behavior in active urban areas complicates the identiﬁcation of potential causal effects. Our paper addresses this empirical challenge and contributes to different strands of the literature by leveraging the quasi-experimental setting of itinerant street markets.

### Street Markets in Montevideo

Traditional open-air street markets, locally known as “ferias vecinales” (Spanish for “neighborhood markets”), have been operating in Montevideo since 1878. Initially, these markets combined commerce and entertainment, with auctioneers selling unsold goods and designated areas for farmers to purchase supplies. In 1933, in response to food shortages, the government established 11 street markets to supply essential goods like potatoes, meat, and bread. By 1947, the National Subsistence Commission had created most of the markets that remain active today, run by both public and private vendors. These markets continue to play a key role in capping the prices of staple foods, including dairy, ﬁsh, fruits, and vegetables.

### Market Location, Rotation, and Size

The location of street markets in Montevideo is formally regulated by both national and local governments, which authorize each market’s operation through renewable permits and periodic evaluations that consider logistical feasibility and neighborhood input. While the framework allows for changes, in practice, several of these markets have remained in the same locations for decades, becoming integral features of their neighborhoods. On average, street markets regulated by the Ministry of Economy remain in the same location for an average of 18 years, while those overseen by the municipal government stay for an average of 30 years (Cuberos 2023).1 A notable example is the iconic Tristán Narvaja market, held every Sunday since the early 20th century.

Street vendors typically engage in this activity as a full-time occupation and rotate across differentmarketsthroughouttheweek,followingaﬁxedscheduleauthorizedbytheirpermits. Most vendors work six days a week, attending a different market each day and resting on Mondays. As a result, the same vendors tend to operate in the same set of markets each week, creating stable patterns of vendor presence over time. The speciﬁc day and location assigned to each vendor are determined by municipal or national authorities during the permitting process, and vendors are allocated ﬁxed stalls within each market layout.

Market size is primarily determined by the available street space, zoning regulations, and logistical constraints, such as trafﬁc circulation and accessibility. This leads to considerable variation in the size of markets: some occupy only a few blocks, while others extend over several hundred meters and host a larger number of vendors. The institutional setup described above results in a recurring but mobile structure of retail activity that is predictable both in time and space.

### A Quasi-Experimental Setting

We identiﬁed 151 open-air street markets operating today across most of the 62 neighborhoods of Montevideo, the capital and largest city of Uruguay.2 These markets cover diverse locations, including neighborhoods with low, middle, and high socioeconomic contexts, and vary signiﬁcantly in size, ranging from small setups spanning a single block to larger ones extending over two or more kilometers. Markets run every day except Mondays, usually opening at 7 a.m. and closing between 2 p.m. and 3 p.m., depending on their location and the variety of goods offered. Most markets operate only once a week in the same location, generating a signiﬁcant increase in pedestrian activity and restricting vehicle trafﬁc as they occupy the entire road, as illustrated in Fig. 1.

This setup leads to an increase in the number of eyes on the street, fostering informal guardianship during market hours. It also encourages the presence of familiar strangers. In smaller markets, the routine of weekly shopping creates opportunities for repeated interactions, gradually building mutual awareness and reinforcing informal surveillance. On the other hand, these markets also serve as crime attractors. Transactions at street markets have

### Street Markets Location

To determine the exact location of street markets, we combined two data sources: (i) ofﬁcial records provided by the regulatory authority and (ii) user-generated information from the navigation and trafﬁc application Waze.3

In a ﬁrst step, we identiﬁed the location of the street markets using information from the Consumer Defense Unit of the Ministry of Economy and Finance, together with data from the Municipality of Montevideo.4 However, ofﬁcial market boundaries do not always align with their actual boundaries. In practice, informal vendors often occupy the periphery of the ofﬁcially deﬁned areas, sometimes signiﬁcantly expanding the market’s footprint. For the purposes of our study, we do not distinguish between the ofﬁcially deﬁned market area and its informal periphery; instead, we treat both as part of the same intervention. To address this issue, we supplemented the ofﬁcial deﬁnition of market locations with data from Waze.5 Speciﬁcally, we incorporated information on road closures regularly reported by Waze users and published on the platform’s community website (i.e., Waze Discuss).6

It is important to mention that we do not have data on potential shifts in the markets’ locations during our study period. This represents a limitation of the available data and, consequently, of our study. However, if such shifts occurred, they would likely result in some geographic units being erroneously classiﬁed as treated when they were not. This type of misclassiﬁcation would bias our results towards the null hypothesis of no effect, effectively reducing the size of our point estimates. Taking this into account, our estimates should be interpreted as a lower bound of the true effects. Nonetheless, as previously noted, street markets tend to remain in the same locations over time (Cuberos 2023).

### Estimator

Our goal is to evaluate how the number of police reports per square kilometer for theft or robbery (the outcome variables of interest) changes in response to the increased activity on the streets of Montevideo due to open-air urban markets.

Let yjt represent the number of crime reports (theft or robbery) per square kilometer in a given buffer of street market j on date t, and Djt be the treatment dummy variable, equal to 1 if street market j operates at time t, and 0 otherwise. Since street markets (mostly) operate only one day of the week, we have a treatment that switches on (on the day the market operates) and off (the rest of the week). Figure C.1 in the Online Appendix illustrates the staggered nature of the treatment and its intermittent dynamics.

In such scenarios, the standard TWFE estimator can produce biased estimates of the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) due to the inappropriate weighting of comparison groups. The two-stage estimator mitigates this bias by ﬁrst residualizing both the outcome and treatment variables with respect to group and time ﬁxed effects, and then estimating the treatment effect using the residualized variables.


While alternative approaches are available (Callaway and Sant’Anna 2021; GoodmanBacon 2021; Sun and Abraham 2021), the choice of the estimator proposed by Gardner et al. (2025) is grounded in the speciﬁc features of our empirical setting, which make this method particularly well suited to the challenges at hand. As an imputation-based method (Borusyak et al. 2024; Liu et al. 2024), it not only addresses the negative-weight problem under heterogeneous treatment effects—by imputing counterfactuals for each treated unit before averaging (“impute-then-average”)—but also offers several additional advantages that are particularly relevant in our case (Chiu et al. 2025). First, it accommodates treatment reversals, a key feature given that street markets are itinerant, moving in and out of each location within hours. In contrast, staggered adoption estimators (Callaway and Sant’Anna 2021;Goodman-Bacon2021;SunandAbraham2021)assumemonotonictreatmentadoption and may yield biased ATT estimates in the presence of such reversals. In addition, under homoskedasticity, imputation-based estimators achieve the semiparametric efﬁciency bound (Borusyak et al. 2024).

### Placebo Tests

We ﬁrst replicated our estimations for periods when the treatment was not active. Figures 6 and 7 display null effects across all buffers when the same estimations are conducted for the same time and units, but focusing on police reports outside market hours (i.e., before 7:00 a.m. and after 3:00 p.m.).

These ﬁndings suggest that there are no spatial spillovers into areas surrounding the markets and that the estimated effects are strictly conﬁned to market hours. One plausible concern is that street markets might attract offenders who linger in the vicinity beyond operating hours, thereby affecting crime rates in nearby areas after closure. However, our results suggest otherwise: once an itinerant market exits a geographic unit, the treatment effect disappears. Our empirical results underscore the spatially and temporally concentrated nature of crime patterns associated with pedestrian agglomerations driven by retail activity.

## Discussion

Our ﬁndings provide novel insights into the relationship between retail-driven street activity and urban crime, challenging several assumptions in the existing literature. Contrary to interpretations of Jacobs’ (1961) eyes on the street that emphasize pedestrian presence as informal guardianship, Wilcox and Eck’s (2011) ecological structuring of criminal opportunities in busy areas, and subsequent empirical studies (Browning and Jackson 2013; Carr and Doleac 2018; Chang and Jacobson 2017; Rosenthal and Urrego 2023; Zahnow 2023), our results suggest that informal guardianship associated with street markets fails to offset the increased availability of theft targets. Instead, we observe a signiﬁcant and highly localized surge in thefts during market hours, concentrated within the ﬁrst two 100-meter rings around the markets. This spatial clustering, combined with the lack of spillover into adjacent areas, aligns with Weisburd’s (2015) framework on the geographic concentration of crime.

However,Jacobs’originalconceptextendsbeyondtheunintentionalsurveillancebypedestrians to include the deliberate, place-based oversight of property owners and shopkeepers,

whose presence and long-term investment foster enduring urban order (Linning and Eck

- 2021; Linning et al. 2022). Itinerant vendors represent a different type of actor. They are temporary users of space without ownership, institutional authority, or highly embedded stakes. Their connection to the urban environment is intermittent, and their capacity to exert informal control is limited. While these merchants may increase crowd density, visibility, and surveillance, their mobility limits the development of local knowledge, durable ties, and embedded guardianship—key elements of effective place management. Thus, our ﬁndings suggest that the quality of eyes on the street matters: not all forms of informal surveillance, nor all types of place users, contribute equally to crime control.


Street markets also reshape the urban landscape, revitalizing local economies and reshaping crime patterns. The presence of cash economies characterizing street itinerant markets in Montevideo creates ecological conditions that attract and generate crime (Brantingham and Brantingham 1995). One of the most striking aspects of our ﬁndings, however, is the divergence in effects between property crimes with and without violence. Previous research suggests that while assaults are generally unaffected, the concentration of businesses or passengers has been linked to increases in ﬁnancially motivated crimes, such as robberies (Ceccato et al. 2022; Tillyer and Walter 2019). In contrast, our results show that although thefts exhibit a signiﬁcant and localized increase on market days, robberies remain unaffected across all buffers and market sizes. This distinction highlights the role of speciﬁc property crime dynamics in shaping the outcomes of urban retail activities.

One possible explanation lies in the differing nature of these two crimes, which inﬂuences offenders’ decision-making and the role of situational factors. Theft typically involves covertly exploiting distracted victims who leave their belongings unattended, whereas robbery requires direct confrontation using force or threats. In essence, robbery depends not only on target availability but also on an offender’s willingness to use violence. Crime opportunities are highly speciﬁc and vary signiﬁcantly between types of property crimes (Clarke and Felson 2017). Street markets provide an ideal environment for pickpockets due to the anonymity, noise, and confusion, which create ample opportunities to exploit victims unnoticed, rendering the eyes of the street ineffective against theft.

Instead, the null results for robberies document a suppressing effect of informal surveillance. Crimes involving direct confrontation are harder to conceal, increasing the risks of detection and potential retaliation from victims or bystanders. A critical part of offenders’ cost-beneﬁt analysis includes assessing informal costs, such as the risk of retaliation or “role reversal” (Jacobs and Cherbonneau 2016; Wright et al. 1996). Moreover, when property crimes involve violence, passive guardianship often shifts to reactive guardianship (Nagin et al. 2023) or “guardianship in action” (Reynald 2016). In street markets, bystanders may intervene during robberies due to moral intolerance for violence, particularly when the presence of other capable bystanders mitigates fears for personal safety (Barnum et al. 2024; Liebst et al. 2019). Additionally, market vendors have a strong economic incentive to intervene in robberies. Violence at their stalls—especially if left unpunished—can ultimately harm their livelihoods.

These results align with previous studies showing that violent property crimes are less responsive than non-violent property crimes when drastic changes in criminal opportunities takeplace.Forinstance,duringtheCOVID-19pandemic,evidenceshowsthatneighborhoods with a higher proportion of residents working from home experienced a greater reduction in reported thefts compared to neighborhoods with fewer remote workers, while both groups exhibited a similar decrease in the number of robberies reported to local police (Díaz et al.

- 2022). This provides further evidence that changes in opportunity structures have a more limited impact on property crimes involving violence compared to those that do not. Taken


together, our results suggest that both mechanisms are at work: increased target availability facilitates thefts, while informal surveillance is probably suppressing robberies by increasing costs through bystander and vendor intervention.

Consistent with other studies evaluating eyes on the street, our ﬁndings are predominantly localized, with minimal to no evidence of spatial spillover effects (McMillen et al.

- 2019). However, we did identify impacts beyond the operational zones of street marketsspeciﬁcallywithinthesecondring,between100and200metersfromthemarket.Aportionof the observed increase in thefts within this buffer zone may be occurring in vehicles and homes that are not situated directly on the market’s street blocks but are nonetheless inﬂuenced by changes in mobility dynamics generated by these retail agglomerations. For instance, residents’ weekly routines of visiting the market often leave their vehicles and homes unattended, rendering goods and money not only attractive to offenders but also visible, accessible, and vulnerable. This vulnerability is exacerbated by inertia—items remain unprotected and easy targets while local residents are away (Clarke and Felson 2017).


The heterogeneity analysis also reveals important nuances. In particular, the effects on thefts are statistically signiﬁcant only in large street markets. In smaller markets, the effects arenotonlyweakerbutclosetozero,suggestingthatmarketsizemaymediatetheftdynamics. Theseﬁndingsare consistentwith alower availability of targets in smallmarkets butalso with Milgram’s (1972) concept of familiar strangers, and are supported by empirical studies on transportstations(Zahnowetal.2021;Zahnow2023).Smallermarketsmaymitigatetherisks associated with increased street activity by attracting repeated visitors, fostering familiarity or a sense of temporary community, reducing anonymity, and strengthening the moral obligations necessary for effective surveillance. The spatial heterogeneity of our results also carries implications for geographic proﬁling, as the ﬁndings suggest that offenders may be more likely to reside in socially vulnerable neighborhoods, regardless of population density levels (Ackerman and Rossmo 2015; Costello and Wiles 2001; Ratcliffe 2006; Rengert et al. 1999).

We also observe heterogeneity across socioeconomic contexts, although the differences are statistically less clear. A difference in effects favoring street markets located in the most vulnerable neighborhoods of Montevideo is consistent with research on journey-to-crime distances, which shows that many offenders commit crimes relatively close to home due to lower costs, greater familiarity with the area, and the integration of offending into their everyday routines (Bernasco 2014; Daele and Beken 2011; Wiles and Costello 2000). Recent work using mobile phone data conﬁrms that offenders typically travel shorter distances than the general public, highlighting the inﬂuence of social and environmental constraints on offender mobility (Zhou et al. 2025).

A poor socioeconomic context may also be linked to neighborhood-level social disadvantage and social disorganization. Prior research has shown that such conditions are associated with higher crime rates, even when controlling for opportunities (Jones and Pridemore 2019). Street markets situated in disadvantaged areas are more likely to experience weaker social controls and a reduced capacity for collective intervention when crimes occur. There is also evidence that offenders actively consider these factors, targeting neighborhoods characterized by low cohesion, poor social organization, and diminished collective efﬁcacy (Bernasco and Block 2009; Chamberlain and Boggess 2016; Johnson and Summers 2015).

Our robustness checks strongly reinforce the credibility of these ﬁndings. The null effects observed in placebo tests—both on days surrounding market activity and during non-market hours, as well as by replacing the outcome variable with a crime type unlikely to be affected by crowding around markets, such as domestic violence—validate the assumption of parallel trends and conﬁrm that unobserved confounding factors do not drive the results. These tests, combined with the consistent spatial and temporal patterns identiﬁed, underscore the

robustness of our two-stage difference-in-differences design and the reliability of our dataset. Future research should aim to replicate these ﬁndings in similar contexts, further testing our identiﬁcation strategy to evaluate whether the concentration of people indeed increases thefts or if, under certain conditions, this relationship does not hold.

While our design allows for assessing the robustness of our results from multiple perspectives, further effort is needed to uncover all the potential mechanisms underlying these ﬁndings. In particular, there is signiﬁcant scope to integrate spatial dimensions with the drivers of our results. Prior studies have demonstrated that crime concentration in speciﬁc urban areas can be shaped by factors such as physical environmental design (Ceccato et al.

- 2022;Reynald2011),neighborhood-levelsocialdisorganization(JonesandPridemore2019), residents’ collective efﬁcacy (Mazerolle et al. 2010), and the proximity of offender clusters and anchor points (Bernasco and Block 2011). Future research should incorporate these contextual factors to provide a deeper understanding of the spatial dynamics at play.


The functional form linking retail-driven street activity to thefts also remains uncertain. It is unclear whether increases in crowd density follow a continuous pattern or whether there is threshold at which the concentration of potential targets outweighs the deterrent effect of informal surveillance. This hypothesis aligns with theoretical models of collective behavior (Granovetter 1978; Schelling 1972) and with Browning and Jackson’s (2013) work on active streets, which ﬁnds a criminogenic effect at lower crowd densities and a protective effect only beyond a certain threshold. Similarly, studies on transit hubs reveal curvilinear patterns: higher crowd densities initially reduce crime through informal surveillance but eventually reach a tipping point where anonymity dilutes guardianship, facilitating theft (Zahnow 2023). Future research that more precisely segments market sizes and pedestrian congestion could test for the existence of a tipping point where crowds cease to deter and begin to enable crime.

Theresults documentedheregenerateinsights thatinform broaderdebatesonurbanpolicy and planning. In Montevideo, street markets present negative externalities related to neighborhood crime. While they play a vital role in promoting local commerce and community engagement, their potential crime attractors and generators highlight the need for a careful balance between fostering economic activity and ensuring public safety. Strategies aimed at developing collective efﬁcacy, empowerment, and informal social bonds (Gearhart 2023; Ohmer et al. 2016)—such as organizing community events around smaller markets—could amplify the protective effects of eyes on the street.

The study also highlights the need to strengthen formal surveillance. Deploying police ofﬁcersincoordinationwithenhancedinformalsocialcontrols(Weisburd2015)orintegrating environmental design strategies—such as installing security cameras during market hours or designing spaces to enhance natural surveillance (Cozens and Van der Linde 2015)—could help reduce the risks associated with increased target availability. Effective policies should promote collaboration among public and private stakeholders, including municipalities, law enforcement, market organizers, vendors, local businesses, and community organizations. This approach could build on successful strategies from transit safety research (Ceccato et al. 2022).

Despite its contributions, this study has limitations that future research should address. First, our reliance on police-reported crime data may underrepresent the true incidence of crime, as not all offenses are reported to authorities. This is a standard and well-known limitation in empirical criminological studies. One might argue that street markets increase the likelihood of reporting due to factors such as the presence of witnesses. However, evidence suggests that this bias in reporting is more relevant for violent crimes or offenses that draw attention from bystanders (West et al. 2023), which limits its impact on our ﬁndings. Moreover, police data may show geographical imprecision when compared to alternative

data sources (e.g., emergency services), potentially affecting our results (Gerell 2018). Thus, future research should aim to triangulate police reports with complementary data sources, such as victimization surveys and health services records to validate our ﬁndings. This would also allow us to investigate whether the victims are primarily customers or vendors in the markets, something that our current data do not allow us to disentangle and that represents another limitation of our study.

Second, the inability to account for potential shifts in the location or size of street markets duringthestudyperiodcouldintroducemeasurementerrors,potentiallybiasingourestimates toward the null. Since this limitation would involve treating areas that are no longer near a market, it likely results in an underestimation of the true effects. However, Montevideo’s street markets tend to remain in their established locations more consistently than residents might prefer, particularly the larger markets where the observed effects are most pronounced. The robustness tests, where we replicated our estimates for different ﬁve-year sub-periods, show results similar to our core estimates and, therefore, are consistent with the assumption that the blocks served by street markets remained stable over time.

Third, while we leverage the spatial and temporal dynamics of Montevideo’s markets to address endogeneity concerns, the ﬁndings may not generalize to other urban contexts with different socioeconomic, cultural, or institutional settings, particularly where key urban factors—such as social disorganization, collective efﬁcacy, proximity to crime-generating or attracting areas—differ. These factors may shape the dynamics of crime concentration in ways that could either amplify or mitigate the effects observed in this study. Additionally, street markets represent a speciﬁc type of retail activity that may limit the applicability of these results to other forms of businesses that also generate street-level concentration and activity. Lastly, but also related to this point, our analysis does not fully capture the potential role of formal surveillance, such as police patrols or private security, which could interact with informal guardianship to shape crime dynamics.

Fourth, while our ﬁndings—particularly the differential effects on theft and robbery—are consistent with both increased target availability and informal surveillance mechanisms, our datadonotallowustoformallytesttheintensityofinformalsurveillanceusingmeasuressuch

- as vendor sector concentration. Indicators capturing heterogeneity in vendor concentration across markets could offer valuable insights into the strength of social ties and the presence of eyes on the street. We see this as a promising direction for future research, particularly in settingswherevendor-levelinformationisavailableandmarketdynamicsmayfosterstronger forms of informal guardianship.


In sum, our study provides compelling evidence that urban street markets, while enriching localcommunities,alsoposeuniquechallengestocrimeprevention.Byempiricallyassessing therelativestrengthofcompetingmechanisms—informalsurveillanceandincreasedcriminal opportunities—this research contributes to a deeper understanding of the spatial dynamics of urban crime and offers actionable insights for policymakers seeking to design safer and more inclusive urban environments.

## Conclusion

This study sheds light on the nuanced relationship between retail-driven street activity and urban crime, using the unique case of itinerant street markets in Montevideo. Our ﬁndings reveal that these markets signiﬁcantly increase thefts—by 43% relative to the average crime rate observed in Montevideo during the study period—during market hours. However, the effects are highly localized, do not show spillovers, and are only signiﬁcant for thefts, high-

lighting the differentiated impact of opportunity structures on various types of crime. By leveraging the spatial and temporal dynamics of street markets and incorporating recent advances in the econometric literature on staggered differences-in-differences methods, this research provides a robust framework for understanding how urban activity inﬂuences crime patterns

These results challenge simplistic assumptions about the protective effects of increased pedestrian activity in environments like street markets, demonstrating that informal guardianship alone may be insufﬁcient to counteract the heightened concentration and availability of targets. These insights call for a more balanced approach to urban policy—one that integrates formal surveillance with strategies aimed at strengthening community ties and mitigating crime risks in high-activity areas. Rather than assuming the universal applicability of Jacobs’ (1961) principles, researchers should consider how contextual factors—such as the stability and embeddedness of local actors—shape the quality of eyes on the street and ultimately determine their effectiveness in controlling crime.

While the context of Montevideo’s street markets provides a compelling empirical setting, the broader applicability of these ﬁndings will require further research in diverse urban environments and retail settings. Despite these limitations, the study contributes to the literature by offering actionable insights for designing safer and more inclusive cities. Ultimately, the interplay between urban dynamics and crime calls for policies that recognize both the opportunities and challenges of vibrant street life, ensuring that public spaces remain accessible, engaging, and secure for all.
