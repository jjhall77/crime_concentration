# ABSTRACT

ARTICLE HISTORY  Received 12 October 2021   Accepted 30 November 2022 

Statement of the problem. 311 call data are replacing on-site assessments as a popular alternative metric to gauge urban streetblock conditions, including physical incivilities like litter, trash and rubbish. Work  to date, however, has not yet established the ecological construct validity, and thus the meaning, of streetblock 311 call counts for specific  physical incivilities. The current work gauges this validity over time.  Procedures.  Philadelphia  open  source  geolocated  311  data  (35,055  streetblocks within all of Philadelphia’s 45 neighborhoods) were combined with streetblock litter scores from two open source on-site assessments made by trained city raters. Following the Hawley/Bursik change  framework, this work examined connections between ecological discontinuities in 311 streetblock litter call counts and later ecological discontinuities in on-site litter assessments. Results/implications. Earlier 311  litter call count shifts connected positively albeit modestly to later  assessed litter shifts. Nevertheless, so too did earlier call count shifts in  a theoretically unrelated category. For this physical incivility, and perhaps others, category-specific streetblock call count shifts have demonstrated  some  modest  convergent  predictive  validity,  but  not  discriminant predictive validity. This is the first theoretically aligned,  streetblock-level ecological change analysis linking 311 calls about  a specific physical incivility to a specific corresponding on-site condition.  Neighborhood spatiotemporal inequalities surfaced.

KEYWORDS  Community criminology;  ecological construct  validation; physical  incivilities; litter

At the site and streetblock level, litter, trash and rubbish – hereafter just termed litter – may be  the most widespread physical incivility in urban residential contexts (Perkins, Meeks, and Taylor   1992: Table 2). Streetblock on-site litter assessments link significantly to residents’ perceptions of  physical and social incivilities, and to on-block crime problems (Perkins, Meeks, and Taylor 1992).  In some studies, but not others, litter on corners or streetblocks connect to crime changes  (Brown, Perkins, and Brown  2004; O’Brien and Sampson  2015; Wheeler  2018). Litter/crime  connections seem likely given that the demographic, land use, and street network covariates  associated with more widespread litter are similar in many respects to crime covariates for the  same geographies (Lockwood, Wyant, and Grunwald 2021). More broadly, litter on urban streets  have psychological, economic, and socioecological impacts (Lockwood, Wyant, and Grunwald   2021). Their presence and consequences bother urban residents and may signal broader community concerns. Streetblock litter, as a particular physical incivility, merits attention from policy  makers and practitioners. It is a local condition that concerns residents, has local public health  implications, falls clearly within the purviews of both specific city agencies and local residents  individually and collectively, and can be remedied in relatively short time frames (Lockwood,  Wyant, and Grunwald 2021).

Data are not simply natural and essential elements that are abstracted from the world in neutral and objective  ways and can be accepted at face value.                                                                             Kitchin (2014a), 4-5).

The  key  question  considered  here  is whether  earlier  shifts  in  streetblock  litter  concerns,  reflected in changing 311 call counts, can predict later on-site streetblock litter changes. The  examination will provide insight into the ecological construct validity of 311 calls for litter,  which, as explained above, includes trash and rubbish calls as well. Is the overlap between  the  variation  in  the  call  shifts,  and  the  variation  in  later  on-site  litter  assessment  shifts,  sufficient for the former to serve as conceptually and empirically acceptable alternate indicators of the latter?

This  article  provides  an  empirical  example  of  how  to  investigate  this  type  of  validity,  focusing on the connection between streetblock 311 litter calls and 311 streetblock on-site  litter assessments. As such, it provides a working example of how to investigate the meaning  of  an  ecological  indicator  like  streetblock-level,  category-specific  311  call  counts.  From  the  perspective of urban informatics, this work asks: Is it wise to rely on changing streetblock 311  calls  counts  about  a  specific  problem  as  an  acceptable  proxy  for  soon-to-appear  on-site  changes in that problem? Given increasing interest among criminologists and urban researchers  in  ‘big  data’  sources  as  proxy  indicators  (Chan  and  Bennett  Moses  2016;  O’Brien  2018;  Taylor  in press), this is a timely investigation.

# Framing: community criminology and ecological construct validation

Community criminology (Taylor  2015) is an organizing meta-theoretical framework bringing  together related theoretical frames and research streams in neighborhoods and crime (Bursik  and Grasmick  1993; Sampson  2012), communities and crime (Reiss  1986; Wilcox, Cullen, and  Feldmeyer  2018),  crime  and  place  (Weisburd,  Groff,  and  Yang  2012;  Weisburd  et  al.  2016),  environmental  criminology  (Andresen  2014;  Brantingham  and  Brantingham  1981;  Taylor   2018), street crime (St. Jean  2007) including links between crime and facilities (Askey et al.   2018;  Haberman,  Groff,  and  Taylor  2013;  McCord  and  Ratcliffe  2009;  Ratcliffe  2012;  Taylor,  Haberman, and Groff 2019), criminal circumstance (Miethe and Meier 1994; Wilcox, Land, and  Hunt 2003), situational action theory (Wikstrom et al. 2012), and the streetblock microecology  of  crime  and  disorder  (Taylor  1997).  Community  criminology  is  meta-theoretical  because  it  focuses on the ‘grammar’ of theories (Hutten 1956, 81); how constructs and linking dynamics  are  each  defined,  and  the  ways  the  different  pieces  of  theories  are  put  together.  Its  focal  interests  are  crime,  delinquency,  victimization,  and  related  events  that  include  social  and  physical incivilities. It is interested in these events as causes or consequences, or both. The  scope of community criminology ranges from situated individuals and single addresses up to  sub-jurisdictional  domains,  such  as  neighborhoods  within  a  city  or  suburb.  Of  particular  interest in the grammatical inquiry is not only the constructs and dynamics taking place at  a particular level, like the streetblock (Taylor 1997), but also the dynamics taking place across  different  spatial  scales;  a  streetblock  attribute  might  affect  an  individual,  or  an  individual’s  actions might affect a streetblock. In short, it can assume a stance of either methodological  holism or methodological individualism (Kincaid 1986, 1996). Integrated models (Bursik 2009;  Short  1985,  1998) emerge from this latter vantage, but prove hard to test comprehensively  and empirically (Bursik  2009).

Ecological construct validation is probably the least well understood and least examined meta-  theoretical challenge in community criminology (Taylor 2015, 205). It deserves additional empirical  attention. ‘Construct validation is a process of establishing that designated empirical indicators  largely or solely reflect one particular underlying construct chosen by the researcher, rather than  alternative constructs. The process helps the researcher establish the meaning of the indicator in  question’ (Taylor 2015, 211). The work here examines one accepted indicator reflecting the intended  construct: on-site litter assessments by trained raters reflecting this physical incivility. The selected  indicator investigated is 311 streetblock litter calls. The selected indicator should correlate strongly  with the accepted indicator, suggesting convergent predictive validity. Further, the selected indicator should correlate more strongly with the accepted indicator than does a theoretically irrelevant  indicator, like 311 information request calls. If the selected indicator performs better than the  theoretically irrelevant one, then the selected indicator demonstrates a type of discriminant validity  (Taylor 2015, 213). Substantively, the validation effort also informs about spatiotemporal inequality  dynamics. Neighborhood level assessed litter shifts, separate from streetblock shifts, can reveal  whether deteriorating conditions are afflicting particular parts of the city.

# Longitudinal thought example

Imagine you have streetblock litter ratings completed by trained raters across a city at two  points in time. Imagine further that you have available 311 calls, including complaints about  litter. In addition, imagine you want to learn whether you can save the effort and expense of  completing on-site litter assessments by learning whether preceding increases in geolocated  311 calls from residents complaining about litter link to later, on-site, assessed increases in  litter. As will be elaborated upon below, a particular slice of changes is of interest. In essence,  you are  inquiring into  the construct validity of changing streetblock 311  call counts about  litter by gauging how strongly these changes link to shifts in a particular criterion variable:  on-site litter assessments.

Note  the  following  two  features  of  this  analysis.  First,  longitudinal  data  are  needed  for  both  the  key  indicator  and  the  criterion  variable.  This  is  mandated  by  the  Hawley/Bursik  perspective on community change (Taylor  2015, 149–157, 198). This view requires longitudinal work capable of separating two types of changes: (1) those reflecting ongoing ecological  continuities where, despite overall shifts, communities retain their same relative ordering vis  a  vis  one  another  on  the  attribute  of  interest;  and  (2)  those  reflecting  ecological  discontinuities,  where  communities  shift  their  relative  ordering  on  the  attribute  of  interest  (Taylor   2015,  147–154;  Taylor,  Ratcliffe,  and  Perenzin  2015).  Cross-sectional  analyses  are  far  less  helpful because at any one point in time how a locale scores on an attribute is bound up  with so many other features of locale (Taylor  2015, 161–162).

Second, the investigation also requires a theoretically irrelevant variable. In other words,  within  the  current  theoretical  frame,  the  incivilities  thesis,  this  variable  would  have  no  rationale  suggesting  a  connection  with  the  accepted  indicator  of  the  intended  construct,  i.e., on-site litter assessments. Considering the links demonstrated by this theoretically irrelevant indicator is not an exact test of the discriminant validity of 311 litter calls. Nevertheless,  it is relevant to it. If 311 call shifts in an unrelated category link as strongly to assessed litter  shifts as do 311 litter call shifts, both categories of 311 call shifts have the same amount of  variance theoretically relevant to assessed litter shifts (Messick  1995). This would raise questions about both the meaning and utility of 311 litter calls per se.

This exact example will unspool empirically here. Two separate citywide assessments made  by trained raters of streetblock litter in Philadelphia provided the criterion variable, changes  in assessed litter. Changing geolocated 311 streetblock call counts from residents about litter  in their environs, cumulated across two different periods, provided the key ecological indicator whose construct validity was gauged. Changing 311 streetblock call counts for information requests provided the theoretically unrelated alternate indicator. The work improves on  previous investigations linking assessed physical incivilities and corresponding 311 calls by:  (1) focusing on longitudinal dynamics reflecting only ecological discontinuities; (2) comparing  the  performance  of  a  theoretically  relevant  311  call  pattern  with  the  performance  of  a  theoretically irrelevant 311  call pattern; (3) controlling  for  spatially  proximate  changes in  the  criterion  variable;  and  (4)  completely  removing  neighborhood  level  outcome  variation,  thereby  focusing  only  on  dynamics  at  one  spatial  scale,  the  streetblock.  The  most  closely  related earlier work is described in the next section.

# Earlier work on 311 calling

Several studies have investigated 311 calling behavior at the individual level (O’Brien 2018; Wei-Ning   2020), and 311 call patterns at various sub-city community levels (Minkoff 2016; O’Brien 2018; White  and Trump 2018). Work also has considered individual differences associated with different types of  311 callers (O’Brien 2015, 2016b; O’Brien et al. 2017; O’Brien 2018; Wei-Ning 2020). For example,  ‘technology acceptance has a positive influence on frequent use of 311 system’ (Wei-Ning 2020, 7).

At various geographies, 311 information is useful. These ‘naturally occurring administrative data’  can serve ‘as a means of learning more about the social life of urban areas’ (Liu et al. 2019: abstract).  They can reflect the ‘pulse of the city’ (O’Brien 2018, 9). If and as they reflect quality-of-life concerns,  such as physical and social incivilities tracked by residents of and users of urban spaces, one would  expect call patterns to be geographically clustered, as are the various social and physical problems  these calls reference (Blasko, Roman, and Taylor 2015; Brown, Perkins, and Brown 2004; Taylor et al.   2011). Indeed, 311 calls associated with physical incivilities do prove to be geographically patterned  (Wheeler 2018). Studies considering whether 311 call patterns referencing physical incivilities link to  later crime shifts have generated varying results (O’Brien and Sampson 2015; Wheeler 2018).

Questions persist about how to interpret geographic variations in call levels and call patterns.  Driving such variations are geographic differences in: (a) problematic extant conditions, (b) civic or  (c) political engagement, (d) communication channel preferences, and (e) views of government.  Some scholars doubt whether 311 call volumes should be interpreted as indicators of civic or  political engagement (White and Trump  2018). Others argue for such an interpretation (Minkoff   2016), albeit sometimes through a highly localized or spatially graded lens (O’Brien et al.  2017;  O’Brien 2018).

Two  main  points  emerge for  the  current analyses  given  preceding  discussions.  First,  311  call  indicators controlling for geographic variation in individual level or group level call-motivating factors  are desirable. That concern leads to the longitudinal design used here. It will control completely for  initial  geographic  variation  in  this  complex  of  call-motivating  factors.  Second,  given  geographic  clustering of problematic urban conditions and the 311 calls referencing them, spatial autocorrelation  controls are likely desirable to isolate what is happening on specific streetblocks.

O’Brien and colleagues have conducted extensive Boston-based work examining determinants,  correlates, and consequences of 311 (or equivalent hotline) calls from residents about physical  incivilities in their surrounds (O’Brien 2016a, 2016b, 2016c, 2018, 2019; O’Brien, Farrell, and Welsh   2019; O’Brien, Gordon, and Baldwin 2014; O’Brien and Montgomery 2015; O’Brien, Sampson, and  Winship 2015). Their work includes initial assessments of the ecological construct validity of various  place-based 311 indicators; see especially O’Brien, Sampson, and Winship (2015). Of most interest  here, given current purposes, is their work gauging how well 311 calls summed across a group of call  categories predict specific, assessed physical incivilities. Their data sources included, in addition to  311 calls, surveys of subsets of registered callers, and two sources of on-site information: on-site  assessments of garbage and streetlight outages done by student raters over a summer, and  information about the quality of all sidewalks on all residential blocks in the city collected by  a third party. The current authors are not aware of research examining category-specific physical  incivility 311 streetblock call rates as predictors of those same physical incivilities, using either cross-  sectional or longitudinal designs. Nonetheless, empirical cross-sectional work  is  available at the  community level.

For a subset of census block groups, O’Brien, Robert, and Winship (2015: Table 4) conducted five  analyses predicting on-site assessed garbage levels. After controlling for a moderating impact of civic  response rate (311 trash calls X civic response rate), trash calls significantly predicted assessed  garbage levels. 311 trash calls included (their Table 1) calls for ‘abandoned bicycles’, to ‘empty litter  basket[s]’, and to report ‘illegal dumping’, ‘improperly stored trash barrels,’ or ‘rodent activity.’ This  finding speaks in favor of interpreting 311 trash calls as reflective of on-site litter levels.

# Open questions regarding work to date

Important though the above finding is, several questions remain. First, the analysis neither reported  the level of spatial autocorrelation of the outcome variable, nor controlled for potential spatial  adjacency impacts (Spencer and Ratcliffe 2005). If the condition of interest, litter assessments at the  Census Block Group (CBG) level, was spatially autocorrelated, the reported results might have looked  different had spatially adjacent outcomes been controlled. Second, the links examined were at one  spatial scale, census block groups. It is not clear if the same connections operate at another spatial  scale, like streetblocks (Taylor 2015, 97–101). Third, since these were cross-sectional data, the links  may not apply to changes over time (Lieberson 1985). From both policy and theoretical perspectives  (Taylor 2015, 216–223), understanding community shifts proves pivotal. Fourth, with cross-sectional  data and a random effects analysis, impacts of geographically varying unobserved covariates were  not controlled (Allison 2009). Finally, what was the discriminant validity of the 311 calls in the key,  theoretically relevant category, 311 litter calls, compared to the connection with a theoretically  irrelevant category of 311 calls? The theoretically relevant call shifts, compared to the theoretically  irrelevant call shifts, should overlap more with shifts in assessed litter (Messick 1995). The current  investigation addresses all these points.

Details  on  categories appear  below  in  the  methods  section.  Geolocated  streetblock  calls  for  a conceptually unrelated category of 311 calls, information requests, were summed for the same  period as well. Note that there is no temporal overlap between the period for 311 calls and the period  for the 2017 on-site litter assessments. The rationale appears below in the methods section. 311 pre  2018 assessment calls reflect the same streetblock call counts for the same period, February through  June, but a year later. Again, call counts were summed for litter, and for a conceptually unrelated  category, information requests. When 2018 311 litter streetblock call count totals are regressed on  corresponding 2017 streetblock call count totals, Path A, reflecting an expected positive correlation, is  created. Conceptually, the model-generated parameters of Path A, and the associated predicted 2018  311 litter call counts, reflect cross-year ecological continuity in 311 streetblock litter call volumes. This  continuity reflects different communities remaining, over time, in the same relative litter call volume  niche vis a vis one another (Taylor 2015, 147–150). What is left over from this regression is labeled  Quantity  B  and  reflects  ecological  discontinuity  (Bursik  1986b;  Hawley  1950).  This  is the  residual  (observed – expected) variation in 2018 streetblock litter call counts not predictable from corresponding 2017 streetblock call counts. Since residual scores should correlate zero with predictor scores, this  ecological discontinuity, or unexpected change in litter streetblock call counts, is completely independent of initial call counts. Initial conditions, including streetblock variations in sensitivity to conditions  (Ward, Link, and Taylor 2017), willingness to make 311 calls (Wei-Ning 2020), and in government views,  cannot explain these unexpected shifts (Taylor, Ratcliffe, and Perenzin  2015). The same operations  were completed for streetblock call count totals for the category information requests.

Of central interest for gauging ecological construct validity of streetblock 311 call counts  are two patterns. First, how strongly do the unexpected shifts in theoretically relevant 311  streetblock  litter  call  counts  affect  later  unexpected  shifts  in  assessed  litter?  Following  the  urban informatics perspective on 311 calls as acceptable proxies for on-site conditions, as 311  litter  calls  unexpectedly  increase,  so  too  should  on-site  streetblock  litter  ratings.  A  strong  positive  link  suggests  convergent  predictive  validity.  Path  E  reflects  this  first  crucial  test.  Second,  is  the  above  connection  with  assessed  litter  change  much  stronger  than  that  observed  for  311  changes  in  a  theoretically  irrelevant  category  of  calls?  Following  the  urban informatics interpretation of 311 calls and specific 311 call categories, streetblock shifts  in theoretically unrelated call categories would not be expected to link up with on-site shifts  in  litter  assessments.  A  much  stronger  link  for  the  theoretically  relevant  call  category,  compared to the theoretically irrelevant call category, would suggest a type of discriminant  validity for the relevant category of 311 calls.

The city of Philadelphia classified 311 calls into several dozen mutually exclusive and exhaustive  categories. A full list of all the codes appears in APPENDIX A. 2,445,634 311 calls for the years 2016–  2019 (yearly count ranging between 589,152 and 623,706) were downloaded from the Open Data  Philly website (City of Philadelphia 2016). Geolocatable calls with latitudes and longitudes totaled  903,974 for the period, with yearly counts ranging from 189,371 (2016) to 273,060 (2019). Of the non-  geolocatable calls 1,541,660 (82.6%) were ‘information request’, 104,143 (6.8%), were ‘miscellaneous’, 92,042 (5.9%) were ‘revenue escalation’, and 38,511 (2.5%), were ‘directory assistance.’

# Methods

## Data and sources

### 311 Data

Individual geolocatable calls had their x,y coordinates geolocated to the nearest streetblock  (streetline segment layer) centroid. Effectively, calls at corners were pulled to the center of the  shortest radiating streetblock. After attaching calls to their respective streetblocks, call counts in  specific categories were cumulated, by month, February through June inclusive, separately for the  years 2017 and 2018.

Litter is defined as ‘things lying about in disorder; rubbish’ (Webster 1983).  The following 311 categories directly correspond:

#### Counting litter calls.

- (1) ‘Illegal dumping’
- (2) ‘Rubbish/recyclable material collection’
- (3) ‘Sanitation/dumpster violation’
- (4) ‘Vacant lot clean-up’


Summing all the calls from these categories in a streetblock generated a litter call count for each  month.

One specific, clearly theoretically unrelated category was ‘information request.’ Geolocatable calls in this category were counted up  separately for each streetblock, for each month.

## On-site assessments

Trained raters who were City of Philadelphia employees conducted on-site assessments of litter in  streetblocks across Philadelphia between August and December 2017 and again between August  and December 2018 (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste Litter Cabinet 2019).1 Trained city employees  from multiple departments completed assessments. One group of assessors, from the Community  Life Improvement Program (CLIP), continued 2018 assessments of vacant lots until the end of  March 2019.

Although minor changes were made to the assessment protocols between the two ratings, in  both periods trained raters used a scoring described as follows:

“Surveyor rating of the state of litter at the site, which is adapted from Keep America Beautiful’s  rating system:

“1 - Little to no litter

“2 - Litter in the amount that can be picked up by one person

“3 - Litter in the amount that would need a team to clean up

“4 - Litter that would require a large cleanup effort and/or heavy machinery to remove debris”2

Philadelphia connected point-level observations to streetblocks, providing 2017 and 2018 streetblock mean litter scores. Details on their methodology appear in APPENDIX B.

To gauge streetblock changes, and determine whether analyses of changes in litter at the  streetblock level were feasible, the 2017–2018 streetblock litter survey file was separated into two  sets of records: (1) ratings for 2017 (n = 39,754), and (2) ratings for 2018 (n = 39,849). Each file  contained only streetblocks with an available rating for that year.

Each file was scanned for duplicate street IDs, indicating that more than one streetblock rating  was available in one year for that streetblock. For 2017, nine duplicate records were removed, leaving  n = 39,745. For 2018, there were no duplicate records.

The two streetblock files were then merged using a 1:1 merge and a streetblock ID variable.  39,634 of the 39,849 2018 streetblock records were matched to 2017 for a streetblock match rate  of 99.5%.

Graphical inspection verified that ratings, for each year, more closely approximated a slightly  positively skewed roughly normal distribution rather than a Poisson distribution.

About five percent of the streetblocks in each year had a code of 0 (see Appendix B). We removed  streetblocks scored 0 in either assessment (n = 3,187). This left 36,447 streetblocks with litter scores  ranging from 1 to 4 at both time points. Of these, 35,055 (96.2%) were geolocatable. This is the final  set of streetblocks used in analyses.

## Dependent variable

To build the variable capturing ecological discontinuity in streetblock assessed litter (Quantity D) we  started with robust regression (Hamilton 1992) to minimize the influence of outlying observations. The  model was based on 36,447 streetblocks with mean litter assessment scores between 1.33 and 4 at  both assessment points. Path C (ecological streetblock continuity) was reflected in a b weight of .538  for the impact of 2017 assessed litter scores on 2018 assessed litter scores (R2 = .256). A constant of .836  reflected a citywide increase in assessed litter ratings. Using the b weight, 2017 litter scores, and the  constant created predicted 2018 assessed litter scores. Then, subtracting the predicted 2018 streetblock litter score from the observed 2018 streetblock litter score generated the ecological discontinuity  in assessed litter variable (Quantity D). Descriptive statistics appear in Table 1. The variable proved  roughly normal with a slightly extended positive tail and a more peaked than expected distribution.

To capture ecological discontinuity we used a mixed effects negative binomial model. 2018  streetblock litter call counts were predicted using corresponding 2017 litter call counts, and  a dummy variable (see below) controlling for fuzziness in streetblock assignment to neighborhood.  Since the same count variable was used for the predictor and the outcome, just for different years, no  exposure variable was needed. As expected, and reflecting ongoing ecological continuity, higher  streetblock call counts in 2017 predicted higher call counts in 2018 (IRR = 1.39, p < .001) (Path A).  From this model, the deviance residuals (McCullagh and Nelder 1989, 39) were retained as the most  normally distributed (Pierce and Schafer 1986) indicator of longitudinal ecological discontinuity in  litter calls (Quantity B). These residuals were completely uncorrelated (r = .003) with initial 2017  streetblock litter call counts, and with ambiguity about streetblock assignment to neighborhood (r =  .001). Therefore, the shifts captured by these residuals were independent of initial conditions.

## Predictor variables

### Ecological discontinuity in preceding 311 litter calls

Counts of calls across all of the litter 311 call categories were summed at the monthly level and  combined across the months February through June for each streetblock, for each year. Winsorizing  (Tukey and McLaughlin 1963) is often used to reduce undue influence of extreme count values (e.g.,  Newcomb et al. (2014), or Wyant and Taylor (2007)). The 2017 five months of streetblock litter call  counts ranged from 0 to 17 after Winsorizing 13 values back to the last consecutive high count value.  The 2018 five months of call counts ranged from 0 to 18 after Winsorizing 7 values back to the last  consecutive high count value.

Graphical inspection using the Stata add-on nbvargr verified that the 2018 streetblock litter call  counts followed a negative binomial distribution. A null mixed effects negative binomial model with  streetblocks (level 1, n = 35,055) nested within Philadelphia Health Management Corporation  (PHMC) neighborhoods (level 2, n = 45) confirmed significant variation in outcome counts across  neighborhoods. These neighborhoods have been used extensively in previous research (e.g., Garcia,  Taylor, and Lawton (2007)).

## Ecological discontinuity in preceding 311 information request calls

For 311 streetblock call counts from 2017, two values were Winsorized back to the highest  consecutive count value of ten; for 311 streetblock call counts from 2018, five values were  Winsorized back to the highest consecutive count value of ten. After Winsorizing, the same  procedure  used  to  construct  ecological  discontinuities  in  streetblock  litter  call  counts  was  used to generate discontinuities in streetblock information request call counts.

February to June 2018 total streetblock call counts were predicted using corresponding 2017 call  counts, including a control for streetblock-to-neighborhood assignment fuzziness. A mixed effects  negative binomial count model with streetblocks at level 1 and neighborhoods at level 2 was used  after confirming the negative binomial distribution of the 2018 311 information request streetblock  call counts, and significant neighborhood-level variation in streetblock information request call  counts. Again, as expected, and reflecting ecological continuity, 2017 streetblock information  request call counts strongly predicted corresponding 2018 counts (IRR = 1.47; p < .001) (Path A).  Again, retained deviance residuals captured streetblock longitudinal ecological discontinuities in  information request call volumes (Quantity B). These discontinuities also proved unrelated to initial  conditions; the correlation of the information request deviance residuals with 2017 call counts was  −.03, and with streetblock-to-neighborhood assignment fuzziness −.02.

## Fuzziness in streetblock assignment to neighborhood

Creating streetblock-level call counts required assigning point data to line segments, and then  matching those line segments to PHMC neighborhoods. Aggregating counts from corners, after  streetblock points were assigned to the nearest streetblock centroid, or from streetblocks astride  neighborhood  boundaries,  sometimes  created  assigned  average  neighborhood  numbers  for  a streetblock that were not whole numbers (n = 1,186; 3.38% of 35,055 streetblocks) For these  streetblocks there was fuzziness about streetblock-to-neighborhood assignment. This is understandable because sometimes an address was on one side of a street and in one neighborhood, sometimes on the other side and in another neighborhood, but both were on the same streetblock.  Streetblocks were assigned to the nearest whole number if the neighborhood number did not end in  .50. If it ended in .50, it was randomly assigned to the adjoining lower or higher whole number.  Adjoining neighborhoods sometimes did not have consecutive neighborhood numbers (e.g., 17, 34).  This ambiguity in streetblock assignment to neighborhood was recognized by including a dummy  variable capturing this uncertainty. It was entered in both the computations of longitudinal ecological discontinuities, as described above, and, later, in the main models as well (Path E).

## Controls for spatial autocorrelation of streetblock assessed litter shifts

Ecological discontinuities in streetblock assessed litter scores proved globally spatially autocorrelated:  Global  Moran’s  I  =  .12,  pseudo  significance  p  <  .001,  using  first  order  Queen  contiguity. Local clustering also was apparent (Anselin  1995). Therefore, a first order Queen  spatially lagged outcome variable was introduced as a predictor. Other spatially lagged forms  were investigated as well, including different numbers of nearest neighbors and different lag  orders. They all correlated about the same, .11 – .13, with the outcome variable. BIC values  from  frequentist  mixed  models  indicated  models  with  the  first  order  Queen  contiguity  lag  variable were preferred (Raftery 1995a, 1995b), so this was the spatial lag variable used in the  full Bayes models.

As explained above when describing tests for ecological construct  validation (see also Taylor (2015, 220)), alternate 311 indicators of other ongoing streetblock changes  outside the incivilities thesis theoretical frame are also needed. The net overlap between 311 call  variation and litter assessment variation should be much stronger in the case of litter calls compared  to information request calls. There is no theoretical rationale leading one to expect shared construct  variance between changing information request calls and changing litter conditions.

Earlier unexpected changes in 311 streetblock information request call counts also  linked significantly (mean b = .015; p = .012) to later assessed litter shifts. Streetblocks where 311 information  request calls were increasing unexpectedly also experienced later unexpected increases in litter  assessments. This suggests that not only do ecological discontinuities in specific, theoretically  relevant categories of 311 call counts link to later assessed litter shifts, but so too do discontinuities  in a theoretically irrelevant category of streetblock call counts.

This pattern leads naturally to the following question. If the theoretically relevant and irrelevant  classes of streetblock 311 call count changes both enter in the same model, does the former have  a markedly stronger impact than the latter? In Messick’s terminology (1995), does the pertinent 311  proxy indicator contain more construct-relevant unique variance than the not pertinent 311  indicator?

On one hand, this result is encouraging, and demonstrative of the multimethod predictive  convergent validity of theoretically relevant 311 category-specific indicators; earlier litter call count  shifts still proved predictive of later assessed litter shifts, even after controlling for the other category  of streetblock call shifts. Further, the evidence for discriminant validity is very slightly in the right  direction; the beta weight for litter calls is bigger than the beta weight for information request calls  (.015 versus .010) when both call shifts are entered. Nevertheless, on the other hand, two features of  results are discouraging. Despite the net impacts of earlier call shifts proving significant, each is  modest. Further, both categories of calls, the theoretically related category and the theoretically  unrelated category, overlap roughly the same amount with construct-relevant outcome variation,  assessed litter shifts. Each taps into the key accepted indicator of the construct, on-site assessed litter  shifts, to approximately the same degree. In short, the evidence does not support the discriminant  validity of category-specific, theoretically relevant 311 litter calls.

# Discussion

The goal identified at the outset was to test the ecological construct validity of streetblock changes  in 311 call counts relevant to one specific physical incivility, litter, which here subsumes trash and  rubbish as well. Can these call count shifts predict later changes in on-site-assessed streetblock litter?  Throughout, shifts reference changes reflecting ecological discontinuities, communities changing  their ordering relative to one another (Bursik 1986a; Hawley 1950; Taylor 2015; Taylor, Ratcliffe, and  Perenzin 2015). Compared to previous work on this question, advances in the current investigation  included (1) a longitudinal approach, (2) an over-time design aligning with the Hawley/Bursik  framework on ecological change, (3) controls for nearby outcome shifts, and (4) the removal of  between-neighborhood  outcome  variation  in  order  to  focus  specifically  on  streetblock-level  dynamics. The current examination provides crucial information at a time when many social  scientists are moving away from capturing streetblock life with on-site assessments, substituting  instead ‘big data’ indicators like 311 calls (Taylor in press).3

When information request calls are considered alongside litter calls, two points also support the  half full idea. The predictor-outcome overlap amount may be less for information request calls  compared to litter calls. The Bayesian DIC value (Table 3) was 4 lower for litter calls versus information calls. This is ‘positive’ but not ‘strong’ evidence that the litter calls model was better (Long 1997,  112). Further, the beta weight for information request calls (.010) was lower, descriptively, than the  corresponding value for litter calls (.015).

The half empty interpretation rests on two points. First, unexpected shifts in theoretically  irrelevant counts of 311 streetblock calls for information also significantly affected the outcome.  Therefore, a theoretically irrelevant 311 call category also contained a notable degree of construct-  relevant variance. Since variation in both theoretically relevant and theoretically irrelevant unexpected 311 call count shifts overlapped with the litter construct as captured by the outcome (Messick   1995), the discriminant validity of 311 litter calls has not been established.

Second, the size of the impact of changing streetblock litter 311 call counts was modest. The  standardized beta weights based on  Table 4  results showed a standard deviation increase in  unexpected litter call count shifts linking to just a 1.5% of a standard deviation increase in unexpected assessed litter condition shifts. There is significant overlap, but it is far from convincing.

Where to go from here? The half empty view means that questions persist about the construct  validity of changes in physical incivility streetblock 311 call counts. ‘Should links from outside the  [theoretical] domain prove equally strong or stronger, it suggests the researcher needs to extend the  theory . . . testing these alternative links provides a potential guide for further needed theoretical  elaboration’ (Taylor 2015, 221). An additional streetblock longitudinal dynamic driving assessed litter  changes, a dynamic outside of the current theoretical frame, appears at work. What that might be  remains open to investigation.

Two important next steps may further understanding of links between changes in assessed litter  and changes in 311 litter calls. First, does the link depend on the temporal gap between the data  windows for earlier calls and later assessments? Category-specific 311 streetblock call counts might  demonstrate both convincing convergent and discriminant validity if the temporal lag between the  two types of assessments was shorter. Second, Lockwood, Wyant, and Grunwald’s (2021) work on  census block group patterning of cross-sectional assessed litter ratings observed that land use  patterns interacted with racial and socioeconomic indicators to mutually shape the influence of  each on litter. Something analogous could be happening here over time. At the streetblock level, do  impacts of earlier 311 litter call count shifts on later assessed litter shifts depend in part on location  within the city? Since the fixed effects model here removed all between-neighborhood outcome  variation, pursuing this question requires a different type of analysis than reported here.

Broader theoretical implications include the following. First, assessed litter shifts on adjoining  streetblocks powerfully influenced assessed litter shifts on target streetblocks. This supports microecological principle 3, ‘Block life is conditioned by features of adjoining blocks’ (Taylor 1997, 134).  Because the focus here was on ecological discontinuities, the current finding underscores how this  conditioning principle applies to longitudinal shifts, even when those shifts are independent of initial  conditions. Second, the significant impact of a streetblock being located on a neighborhood boundary street, captured by the streetblock-to-neighborhood assignment fuzziness dummy variable,  speaks to the importance of the street network not just as an influence on litter (Lockwood,  Wyant, and Grunwald 2021), but also as an accelerant of increases in this physical incivility. These  boundary streets in general were more likely to be major arteries. Street network influences on  jurisdiction-wide crime changes have been observed previously (Groff et al.  2014). The current  finding extends this over-time impact to a smaller spatial scale and to a crime-related outcome.  Third, the pattern of results supportive of convergent but not (a type of) discriminant validity speaks  to an ongoing question. To what extent can changes in category-specific 311 call patterns serve as  acceptable stand-ins for changes in assessed on-site conditions (Taylor in press)? It speaks to this  question, but at the same time leaves it wide open. Fourth, the neighborhood level spatiotemporal  dynamics, showing unexpectedly deteriorating litter conditions in many predominantly (> = 70%)  African-American neighborhoods, and unexpectedly improving conditions in several socioeconomically advantaged neighborhoods (Figure 2), underscores the broader macro-level ethnoracial and  socioeconomic dynamics (Peterson and Krivo  2010) operating dynamically at the neighborhood  level.

## Implications

Since policies are about making changes, from a policy perspective the connections between  changes examined here are centrally relevant. Are 311 streetblock call count shifts useful for  estimating later changes on-site conditions? Maybe, but just a little bit. In the case of litter calls,  the standardized net impacts observed with beta weights of between one and two percent are  sobering. Only close examination, using both 311 call data, and separate data sources about on-site  conditions, over different change periods, will help policy makers and practitioners better understand when, how, and where they can wisely use 311 shifts in call patterns as helpful and cost-  effective stand-ins for emerging shifts streetblock conditions. Hopefully, and in line with the Hawley/  Bursik perspective, this future work will distinguish changes reflective of ongoing ecological continuities (Path A, Path C) from those reflecting ecological discontinuities (Quantity B, Quantity D).

## Limitations and strengths

A third potential limitation arises from the large fraction of 311 information request calls that  lacked geographic coordinates in the original data source. As one reviewer asked, is it plausible that  the geolocated information request calls were more theoretically relevant to streetblock conditions  than the non-geolocated information request calls? Perhaps, but not necessarily. Two elements are  relevant. First, in Philadelphia, cell phone or web submitted 311 inputs require an address, but  landline calls do not. Second, in Philadelphia, if the call has the service name ‘information request,’ it  means that ultimately no work order for a city agency was generated based on that input. The reason  could be that the topic was just an information request, for example, where do I go to register to  vote? Alternatively, the reason could be that a streetblock issue was identified, but the issue fell to  a non-city agency. For example, the response to a call about a problem with trolley tracks would  inform the caller how to contact the local regional transportation authority, SEPTA, which is not a city  agency. Thus, no work order for a city agency would be generated from that call and it would be  classified as an information request. A voting registration location question and a trolley tracks  complaint would both end up as geolocated information requests if input through cell phone or web  browser. Both would end up as non-geolocated information requests if called in using a landline.  These points aside, at least a case can be made that geolocated 311 information requests were  numerous, permitting constructing streetblock call counts, and, further, that, among the 311 call  types available, this was the one whose theoretical connection to incivility dynamics was weakest.

Two study limitations arise from the temporal structure embedded in the analyses. First, and as  depicted in Figure 1, ecological discontinuities for both the 311 call shifts and the assessed litter  shifts each referenced a particular elapsed time during which changes accumulated. A year passed  between the initial and follow-up 311 call windows. Depending on when trained raters scored up  specific streetblocks, well over a year could have passed between the initial and follow-up assessment windows. The connections observed here reflect this specific temporal structure of the data.  There is no theoretical guidance on whether these time horizons (Taylor  2015, 154–157) for  accumulating changes in both these data sources are appropriate. Work to date on streetblock  incivility changes does not provide any guidance here. Second, because the initial and follow-up  periods for the assessment data were each several months in duration, these assessment data embed  aoristic uncertainty (Ratcliffe  2002). For example, one streetblock may have been assessed in  July 2017 the first time around and December 2018 the second time around, with litter changes  accumulating  over  almost  1.5  years.  Another  streetblock  could  have  been  assessed  in  December 2017 and again in August 2018, with litter changes accumulating only over .75 years.  Does this matter? Note that both data sources are time and date stamped, so future researchers  could shed light on this question.

These limitations should be considered in the context of study strengths. First, changes over time  were considered. In addition, changes were operationalized in line with the Hawley/Bursik perspective on unpacking longitudinal ecological shifts (Bursik 1986b; Hawley 1950; Taylor, Ratcliffe, and  Perenzin 2015). Thus, changes in both streetblock 311 call counts and assessed litter ratings reflected  ecological discontinuity. ‘In the Hawley/Bursik frame, unexpected changes, if sizable, capture ecological discontinuity and ecological niche redefinition; such shifts are usually operationalized so they  are independent of ongoing ecological continuity’ (Taylor 2015, 198). In short, the changes examined  here may have unfolded from initial setting conditions, but represented longitudinal dynamics  independent of those starting conditions (Taylor, Ratcliffe, and Perenzin 2015). Second, and in line  with suggested microecological principles (Taylor 1997), spatially adjacent ecological discontinuities  in litter conditions were taken into account. In some significant earlier works (O’Brien 2018; O’Brien,  Sampson, and Winship  2015), such controls have  not  been applied. Adjacent changes proved  important at the streetblock level, suggesting they should be included in future models as well.  Third, the current work analytically isolated streetblock dynamics by controlling fully and completely  for between-neighborhood outcome variation. Allison’s (2009) recommended approach accomplished this with n-1 (44) dummy predictor variables for the neighborhoods (n = 45). Some earlier  works with neighborhood random effects models may have limitations because they failed to  separate unobserved between-neighborhood covariation from other model parameters. Finally, as  explained above, mono-method bias was avoided.

Using data from two city-wide standardized litter streetblock assessments in the city of Philadelphia  in 2017 and 2018, the current work considered how earlier streetblock shifts in 311 calls complaining  about litter, trash, and rubbish connected with later shifts in streetblock litter ratings carried out by  trained raters. Scholars championing spatially and temporally precise 311 category-specific call data  argue these indicators provide timely, accurate insights into how conditions are changing on the  ground for residents in different communities. The Hawley/Bursik framework to separate changes  reflecting longitudinal ecological continuities from ecological discontinuities, for both 311 calls and  on-site assessments, revealed that earlier 311 streetblock litter call count shifts linked positively, as  expected, to later assessed streetblock litter shifts. Nevertheless, so too did theoretically irrelevant  shifts in 311 information request call counts. In short, 311 streetblock call counts for the specific  category of physical incivility considered here, litter, demonstrate some convergent validity but not  discriminant validity. Moreover, the modest degree of construct-relevant overlap between 311 litter  call changes and litter rating changes leaves open the question of whether category-specific streetblock call count shifts can serve as acceptable proxy indicators for on-site shifts in crime-related  physical condition, and perhaps for other conditions as well. Despite the costs of acquiring on-site  assessments for gauging community conditions, at this time, at the very least for this physical  incivility, collecting such assessments seems crucial for gauging shifts in crime-related community  qualities. Perhaps as importantly, researchers, policy makers, and stewards of urban communities  need on-site assessment data so they can better understand the outputs of 311 systems. Finally, the  finding that multiple categories of 311 call count shifts independently predict later shifts in assessed  litter conditions suggests theoretical revisions of streetblock incivility dynamics are needed. With  ecological construct validation, as with construct validation efforts more broadly (Cronbach 1970),  empirical results spur theoretical reflection and revision, in turn inducing further empirical investigation of this type of validity. From a theory vantage, this type of validity assessment is perhaps the  most crucial benchmark of scientific quality since it provides insight into the conceptual meaning of  empirical indicators.

# APPENDIX B. City Methodology to Create Mean Streetblock Litter Ratings

The explanation of “mean” streetblock ratings is as follows. Raters from different departments assessed different types  of sites. For example, Department of Licensing and Inspections personnel rated vacant lots. “Ninety-five percent of  blocks were surveyed by the Streets department while just under a third of blocks were surveyed by at least one other  agency” (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet  2018, 4). Rectangular buffers were created for each  streetblock so that site-level ratings could be applied to streetblocks (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter  Cabinet 2018, 5). Then an overall (“mean”) streetblock rating was generated via a weighted sum of the relevant ratings.  Overall streetblock ratings, conducted by Department of Streets raters for the entire length of the streetblock, counted  for 2/3 of the overall scores, and the average of streetblock-associated site level ratings, counted for 1/3 of the overall  scores (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet 2018, 6). More specifically, “using the surveys aggregated to  that block . . . the following formula . . . calculate[d] the block score” (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet   2018, 6)

2/3*S block + 1/3*k*L block

Where

S block = “average of all surveys conducted by the Streets department on that block. These surveys represent the full  length of the block from street to right-of-way and predominate the experience of walking down that block” (City of  Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet 2018, 6).

L block = “the average of all surveys scored 2 or higher conducted by other city agencies on that block. Scores that  rated an area of the block as 1 (little to no litter) were excluded from this average because it is functionally the same as  an unsurveyed area of the block” (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet 2018, 6).

k = 2 is a “calibration factor” that “adjusts for the fact that 80% of blocks do not contain vacant lots, schools, parks, or  SEPTA stations that scored 2 or higher, such that any block with an open area that is littered is already among the top  20% for litter outside the street right-of-way. This also ensures that blocks that were only surveyed by the Streets  department do not receive an inappropriately reduced score” (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet   2018, 6).

“For blocks that were surveyed by the Streets department but not by any other agency a minimum value of 1” was  used for L block (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet 2018, 6).

“For blocks that were not surveyed by the Streets department but were surveyed by another agency . . . the average  Streets survey score within that neighborhood” was used for S block (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter Cabinet   2018, 6).

“If the block was not surveyed by any department, it received a score of 0” (City of Philadelphia - Zero Waste and Litter  Cabinet 2018). Streetblocks coded zero in either year (n=3,187) were dropped from analyses.
