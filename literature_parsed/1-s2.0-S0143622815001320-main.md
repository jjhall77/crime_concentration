Research within the geography of crime and spatial criminology literature most often show that crime is highly concentrated in particular places. Moreover, a subset of this literature has shown that the spatial patterns of these concentrations are different across crime types. This raises questions regarding the appropriateness of aggregating crime types (property and violent crime, for example) when the underlying spatial pattern is of interest. In this paper, using crime data from Campinas, Brazil, we investigate the crime concentrations and the similarities among different crime types across space. Similar to some recent research in another context, we ﬁnd that crime is highly concentrated in Campinas but the ability to aggregate similar crime types at the street segment level is not generalizability when compared to a North American context.

1. Introduction

Research in spatial criminology over the past 25 years has consistently shown that crime is highly concentrated in very few places (Sherman, Gartin, & Buerger 1989; Weisburd & Amram, 2014). In some of the more recent studies that consider disaggregated crime types (assault and robbery versus violent crime, for example), the spatial concentrations of these crime types have been so great that there appear to be concentrations within concentrations (Braga, Hureau, & Papachristos, 2010, 2011). Because of these concentrations, David Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) have put forth the “law of crime concentrations at places”. This is a geographical twist on the known behavior of the few chronic offenders who commit the vast majority of criminal events (Wolfgang, Figlio, & Sellin, 1972). Consequently, Weisburd and his colleagues posit that we not only need to investigate chronic offenders, but chronic places.

Once the question of how concentrated criminal events has been answered, this is discussed in the review below, one may also ask if these concentrations occur in the same places.

Intuitively, we know that this cannot be the case for some crime types. Commercial burglary can only occur where there is commercial land use and similarly for residential burglary; as such, there will only be a similarity in these respective spatial patterns where there is mixed land use in a city. But for other crime type comparisons this may not be so obvious. There are theoretical considerations that may be at play here: understanding the role of criminal opportunities, for example, but also for criminal justice policy. As outlined by Andresen and Linning (2012), a criminal justice policy prescription for one crime type will most often be different than for another crime type, so we must be careful basing any (criminal justice) policies on analyses using aggregated data.

As discussed below, there is a growing body of literature in this research area in the context of the United States, but very little that is international in scope. In this paper, we contribute to this literature by asking the following two research questions: 1) is crime concentrated in a Latin American context similar to the concentrations in North America?, and 2) if so, are different crime types concentrated in the same places? We investigate these two research questions using criminal event data from Campinas, Sao~ Paulo State, Brazil considering sixteen different types of crime from 2010 to 2013. We also used a spatial point pattern test that allows for the nonparametric statistical identiﬁcation of the similarity in

spatial point patterns and extended the methods for sensitivity analyses in this literature by using only the spatial units of analysis that have their respective crime types present.

2. Crime concentrations at places and their spatial patterns

Since at least the early 19th century, we have known that crime exhibits particular patterns such that one is not equally likely to be a victim of criminal victimization in different locations (Quetelet, 1842). Despite these varying spatial patterns of crime, the units of analysis used in these earlier studies were rather coarse, exhibiting spatial heterogeneity when analyzed at a smaller geographical scale (Andresen & Malleson, 2013a; Groff, Weisburd, & Yang, 2010). As such, it should be no surprise that the trajectory of research within the geography of crime has been to smaller units of analysis (Weisburd, Bruinsma, & Bernasco, 2009a). As reviewed below, this movement of research to smaller units of analysis, currently referred to as the micro-spatial unit of analysis using the street segment, has been rather instructive; however, performing analyses at multiple different scales is important to undertake whenever possible to assess any potential impact from the modiﬁable areal unit problem (MAUP), whether the MAUP is an explicit research question or not (Fotheringham & Wong, 1991; Openshaw, 1984).

Though micro-spatial units of analysis have been used in previous research, Sherman et al. (1989) published the ﬁrst such analysis considering an entire city to measure the concentrations of crime at places. In their research in the context of predatory crime in Minneapolis, Minnesota they found that 50 percent of the calls for police service were generated from only 3 percent of addresses and intersections: all robbery calls came from 2.2 percent of addresses and intersections, all motor vehicle thefts came from 2.7 percent of addresses and intersections, and all rape calls came from 1.2 percent of addresses and intersections, for example. This is an incredibly high degree of concentration for any activity, let alone criminal activity. However, it must be acknowledged that because of the relatively rare nature of criminal events, a high degree of concentration is not surprising when there is a large number of spatial units under analysis such as addresses, street segments, and street intersections. For example, if there are 1000 spatial units of analysis and 100 criminal events, the degree of spatial concentration must be 10 percent, or less, with each spatial unit of analysis having no more than one criminal event.

It was another 15 years before another research study emerged with a similar statistic, but in Seattle, Washington (Weisburd et al., 2004). These researchers found that approximately 5 percent of street segments accounted for 50 percent of all calls for police service over a 14-year time period, and that all calls for police service could be accounted for in approximately 50 percent of street segments in Seattledfurther research in Seattle found similar concentrations of other contexts, such as juvenile delinquency (Groff et al., 2010; Weisburd, Morris, & Groff, 2009b). Braga et al. (2010) found that 50 percent of gun violence in Boston, Massachusetts was concentrated in less than 5 percent of street segments and intersections, and in subsequent research on Boston they found that 50 and 66 percent of commercial street robberies, respectively, could be accounted for in 1e8 percent of street segments and intersections (Braga et al., 2011).

Though repeatedly showing that these concentrations are a phenomenon in various cities across the United States, it is also important to replicate these results in different contexts around the globe. The ﬁrst, and closest neighbor to these cities in the United States, is from research by Andresen and Malleson (2011) and Andresen and Linning (2012) in Canada: Vancouver and Ottawa, respectively. Considering a variety of crime types (assault, burglary,

robbery, sexual assault, theft, theft of vehicle, and theft from vehicle), Vancouver calls for police service are also highly concentrated: 5 percent of street segments account for 50 percent of the calls for service for these crime types. Perhaps more instructive, is the variation in these concentrations across crime types: assault (1.62%), burglary (7.61%), robbery (0.84%), sexual assault (1.12%), theft (2.58%), theft of vehicle (5.97%), and theft fromvehicle (2.64%). Overall, just over 60 percent of street segments in Vancouver accounted for all of these calls for service in the context of these crime types. Moreover, only considering the street segments that had any crime, Andresen and Malleson (2011) found evidence for concentrations within concentrations, particularly for assault, robbery, theft, and theft from vehicle. In Ottawa, Andresen and Linning (2012), a smaller set of crime types were analyzed: burglary (commercial and residential), robbery (commercial, individual, and other), and theft of vehicle. Though this is a restricted set of crime typescomparedtootherresearchinthisarea,theconcentrationsare incredibly high: total burglary (1.67 percent of street segments), total robbery (0.38 percent of street segments), and theft of vehicle (0.99 percent of street segments). Overall, 50 percent of these crime types occur within 1.70 percent of street segments in Ottawa, but more telling are the comparisons with the speciﬁc crime types in Vancouver and Boston. Lastly, Weisburd and Amram (2014) have investigated the concentrations of crime at places in Tel Aviv-Jaffa, Israel. Similar to other research in Canada and the United States, these authors found that 50 percent of criminal incidents occurred at 4.5 percent of street segments, and that all criminal incidents could be accounted for considering only 36.8 percent of street segments, in 2010. This bodes well for the generalizability of the law of crime concentrations at places (Weisburd et al., 2012). However, at thistimethelatterstudyistheonlyoneundertakenoutsideofNorth America. In this paper, we contribute to this literature through an analysis of Brazilian crime patterns.

In addition to the crime and place literature, we investigate the similarity of spatial point patterns for disaggregated crime types in a Brazilian context. To the best of our knowledge, Andresen and Linning (2012) is the only research paper that has investigated this phenomenon. They found that it was generally inappropriate to aggregate detailed crime types to form categories such as “property crime” and “violent crime” if one is interested in the spatial patterning of these crime types. The only exception they found was in the context of highly concentrated crime types measured at the level of the street segment. However, their study is not without its limitations. Though the authors acknowledged the importance of the places that have no criminal events in their analyses (40 percent of Vancouver and 90 percent of Ottawa), they only removed the street segments in their sensitivity analyses that had zero of any crime type. As such, for those crime types that are highly concentrated, they may still appear to be more similar than they really are because many of the street segments will be free from the two crime types under analysis. Consequently, we are able to contribute to this literature by replicating their study in a Brazilian context, but also by extending their analyses to use street segments that matter speciﬁcally for the two crime types under analysis when investigating similarity.

3.2. Scale of analysis

The spatial scales of analysis used in this research are: ponderation areas,4 census tracts, and street segments. Fig. 3 depicts these different scales and shows the descriptive statistics for these three spatial units of analysis in Campinas municipality. The geographically largest spatial unit of analysis is the ponderation area. This is the ofﬁcial geographic unit inside the municipality territory that assembles several census tracts. Its purpose is to apply procedures to calibrate the estimation with other socioeconomic data from the population. Both census tract and ponderation area map ﬁles and the relevant technical information were downloaded from the IBGE site (http://www.ibge.gov.br/home/). There are 36 ponderation areas in Campinas.

The census tract can be rural or urban and it is the smallest ofﬁcial territorial unit for cadastral control to government data collection. Its boundaries are deﬁned based on two criteria: it needs to be an area contained within the municipality territory and its size is limited such that an enumerator can survey the area in a period of one month. Usually, the census tract has an average of 300 households in the urban areas, similar in size to census tracts in the United States. There are 1749 census tracts in Campinas.

And lastly, the geographically smallest spatial unit of analysis is the street segment. The street segment is not an ofﬁcial data unit in the census. Each street segment is a line with its distance deﬁned between street intersections. Usually, the street segments contain the addresses of both sides of the block. There are 49,173 street segments in Campinas.

On average, there are 48 census tracts within each ponderation area and 28 street segments within each census tract. Using this variety of spatial scales, our analysis will be able to ascertain varying spatial patterns at different scales. This has been shown to be instructive within the crime and place literature, as reviewed above. Additionally, we undertake sensitivity analyses the only use spatial units of analysis that have their respective crime types present. For example, total robbery is present in only 13.26 percent of street segments in Campinas, whereas total crime is present in 28.69 percent of the street segments in Campinas. As such, when we compare the spatial patterns of two robbery crime types (commerce robbery and residence robbery, for example) we only use the street segments that have any criminal events relating to those two crime types (a maximum of 13.26 percent) rather than any crime type (a maximum of 28.69 percent).

4.2. Spatial patterns of crime types and their similarities

Turning to the results of the spatial point pattern test, Table 6 shows the results for ponderation areas (lower-left triangle) and census tracts (upper-right triangle) considering all spatial units of analysis. With regard to the ponderation areas, the S-Index values are all quite low, ranging from 0.00 to 0.30. This is a clear indication that aggregation of crime types is not appropriate for this scale of analysis. Census tracts, on the other hand, do present more promising results, but in most cases do not achieve the value of 0.80, used in previous research to indicate that two spatial patterns are similar. The only exceptions to these results are the S-Index values comparing public transportation robbery, public transportation theft, and vehicle robbery. The various combinations of tests for these crime types result in S-Index values ranging from 0.89 to 0.93, indicating that these crime types exhibit similar spatial patterns. In the context of public transportation crime types, this should not be too surprising because public transportation does generally only move through particular places and Campinas does not deviate from this generality.

Turning to the results for the street segments, Table 7, the SIndex values are now all quite high indicating spatial similarity in most comparisons of crime types. The only crime type that does not meet the threshold of 0.80 in most cases is total crime except when compared to other theft, total robbery, and total theft. However, all of the S-Index values for the other comparisons with total crime are greater than 0.70 indicating that the spatial patterns of all crime types appear to be similar. If a researcher wished to be conservative, any aggregation of crime types here would be suitable aside from total crime if the spatial unit of analysis used was the street segment. We need to recall, however, that the majority of the street segments, and some census tracts, in Campinas did not have any crime. As such, these S-Index values may be artiﬁcially high because of all the zero-values.

5. Discussion

In this paper we have investigated the importance of crime concentrations and the similarity of spatial patterns for a number of crime types in Campinas, Brazil. We have found that similar to the crime and place literature that developed the law of crime concentrations at places, this law is generalizable to Campinas, Brazil. In fact, it appears as though this law is “stronger” in this Latin American context than found in other contexts. This is important for the further development of this law of crime concentrations at places because it implies that any explanations for this law cannot be restricted to Israeli and North American phenomena.

We have also found that the similarities of spatial patterns are rather low in Campinas, Brazil. Though there were some initial results that indicated similarity, particularly at the street segment level of analysis, further investigations revealed that this was not the case. This is similar to the results found by Andresen and Linning (2012), but extends their work by using non-zero street segments that are speciﬁc to each pair of crime types being compared in the spatial point pattern test. Of particular interest from this latter portion of the paper is that there were essentially only three broad crime types under analysis: rape, robbery, and theft. Though the latter two crime types were separated into rather detailed forms of robbery and theft, they were still robbery and theft. With this level of detail separating the crime types into 16 different classiﬁcations, our analysis is actually strengthened. This level of detail is most often not available in the literature. And despite the, apparently, minute details separating these crime types they still were found to not be spatially similar. In the cases comparing public transportation theft/robbery to other forms of theft and robbery this result may not be surprising, but in the other cases it shows the importance of disaggregating crime types if one is concerned with the spatial patterning of these phenomena. This result shows that the result found by Andresen and Linning (2012) in Ottawa, Canada that highly concentrated and similar crime types may be aggregated at the street segment level of analysis without incident is not generalizable to a Latin American context. The potential for generalizability may be present elsewhere, speciﬁcally in North America, but deﬁnitely not in all places. In fact, it would be interesting to know the results of more speciﬁc non-zero street segments in this North American context.

With regard to Andresen's (2009) test and the corresponding index, the above analysis raises some methodological issues in the context of (many) zero values in the spatial units of analysis. When

analyzing the entire study area (municipality, in the current analysis), the presence of many zero values may make the spatial pattern appear to be very stable, when there is a lot of spatial movement in the areas that actual have crime, or the event data that are being analyzed. For example, an S-Index value of 0.90 may be present when one crime type is concentrated in 5 percent of street segments and another crime type is concentrating in another 5 percent of street segments. In this extreme example, not present in the current analysis, there is zero similarity between these two crime types when considering where they actually occur. As such, removing zero value spatial units of analysis is a critical step in the analysis to ensure improper inference is not being made, especially in the context of spatially-speciﬁc crime prevention interventions. However, it must be remembered that the analysis of all the street segments (the entire study area) is still instructive because it shows the entire study area is similar (i.e. no crime) for 90 percent of the study area. This is important information. Consequently, it is important when using Andresen's (2009) test to undertake a complete analysis of the spatial units available as well as a sensitivity analysis that only includes non-zero value, if these are present in the data.

Overall, this research shows the importance of understanding space and place in the context of spatial crime patterns. What is known to exist in one context cannot be assumed to be present in another.
