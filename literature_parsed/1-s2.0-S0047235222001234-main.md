A B S T R A C T    

Purpose: The law of crime concentration suggests cities have almost no impact on the spatial distribution of  crime. This study reinvestigates the relationship between cities and the distribution of crime across the various  micro-places and neighborhoods which compose these locations. 

Methods: We observed robbery incidents reported to police departments across eight U.S. cities from 2015 to  2019. We calculated the spatial variability of the distribution of robberies attributed to census blocks (i.e., micro-  places), census tracts (i.e., neighborhoods), and cities (i.e., macro-places) using variance partitioning techniques  with multi-level negative binomial regression models. 

Results: Our findings are mixed on the relationship between cities and the spatial distribution of robbery in-

cidents. The descriptive analyses suggest a moderate influence of cities on measures of crime concentration. One  of our modeling strategies estimates a larger impact while another strategy observes almost no contribution of  cities to the total spatial variability. 

Conclusions: This study supports previous research which demonstrates there are overwhelming similarities in the  distribution of crime between cities and micro-places account for most of the spatial variability of patterns. While  cities do not appear to have a major influence on distributions, future research should continue to clarify these  mixed findings and provide a more compelling theoretical account of this relationship.   

1. Introduction 

Cities are generally considered distinct ecological spaces (see Park &  Burgess, 1925) but there is very little understanding of how these locations could influence the spatial distribution of crime.  Weisburd  (2015)  proposed  the  law  of  crime  concentration  after  observing  remarkable consistency between cities regarding the concentration of  crime at micro-places (also see Weisburd, Groff, & Yang, 2012). The  cities he observed had disparate social and physical characteristics but  exhibited an almost identical degree of distributional concentration of  crime. In other words, the law of crime concentration suggests there is  almost no difference between Portland, OR and Portland, ME in the  extent to which a large amount of these city’s crime occurs at a small  number of places. Outside of this study, there is almost no research  which provides a systematic assessment of the impact of cities on the  distribution of crime at the places within these locations. The criminology of place instead emphasizes the opposite, which is scaling-down  from macro- or meso- units to understand more about where the “action” 

of crime occurs, which is between micro-places in cities (Steenbeek &  Weisburd, 2016). 

Recent studies have indicated micro-places provide a larger contribution to the total spatial variability of crime patterns compared to  neighborhoods within cities. These analyses use a hierarchical framework which examines crime across at least two nested spatial units to  understand how crime varies between spatial scales (see Hipp, Kim, &  Wo, 2021;  O’Brien, 2019;  O’Brien  &  Winship, 2017;  Quick, 2019;  Schnell, Braga, & Piza, 2017). These analyses provide concise estimates  of variability which represents a quantification of the unique contribution of a spatial unit of analysis in telling the story of where crimes occur  between places. These estimates permit a more comprehensive assessment of the distribution of crime and rely on more sophisticated analytic  techniques compared to previous tests of crime concentration. To provide a more detailed examination of the potential influence of cities, the  spatial variability of crime instead of its distributional concentration  should be considered across the places which compose cities. Our research design builds upon these previous hierarchical analyses  to examine the distribution of crime within and between cities. We estimate a three-level hierarchical model which contrasts the influence of  cities to neighborhoods and micro-places on the spatial variability of  robbery  incidents.  This  study  provides  a  unique  contribution  by  expanding upon this research methodology by scaling-up to cities to  reconsider a key question: do cities influence the distribution of crime?  Our motivation is providing a new systematic assessment of this relationship and further elaborating upon why these similarities or differences do exist between cities. This research question matters for place-  based criminological theory because there is very little comprehension  of the characteristics from these locations and the mechanisms by which  these aspects of cities could produce differences in where crime happens.  In addition, this relationship is critical to help refine place-based crime  prevention strategies which are often championed as “one-sized fits all”  solutions that can be applied across disparate jurisdictional contexts. We then present the  research design and findings before conducting an extended discussion  section of how our findings advance understanding of this topic for  place-based criminological theory and crime prevention strategies. 

2. Cities, crime, and spatial scales 

There are demonstrable differences in the characteristics of cities  across the world which reinforce how each could be considered a unique  place. The premise of Charles Dickens’s book  A Tale of Two Cities  (Dickens, 1859) and its iconic opening sentence is built upon contrasting  the conditions of London and Paris at the end of the 18th century (“It  was the best of times, it was the worst of times…). The Dickensian  example highlights the starkest contrast based upon political conditions  of the era. Today, around 80% of people in the United States live in  urban or suburban metropolitan areas (U.S. Census Bureau, 2010) and  these locations present vibrant economic hubs for populations. There are  major cultural differences between cities which can be observed from  preferences associated with everything from food and recreation to  public transportation and weather. These distinctions could manifest as  tangible behavioral differences between people (e.g., are residents of  city X more violent?) or political differences which regulate behavior (e.  g., this law in City Y impacts residents). Certain cities’ municipal services might do a better job of repairing streetlights; others’ might be  more  proactive  installing  close-circuit  televisions.  Criminal  justice  agencies within cities such as police departments have a substantial  impact on the application of criminal justice policy. In general, important city-level characteristics could be social (e.g., racial demographics,  unemployment rate, etc.) or physical (e.g., square milage, waterways,  etc.) representations of these differences between cities. 

Although the law of crime concentration suggests cities might not  influence the spatial distribution of crime, there is much more research  which suggests cities could matter. One of the many enduring contributions of the Chicago School was the focus on cities as a critical spatial  unit of analysis and the collective goal to understand the organization of  places within cities (Burgess, 1925). The role of city-specific characteristics is potentially impactful because these units of analysis can  inherently shape everything found within them (see Kim & Hipp, 2018).  Specifically, historical differences between cities have influenced drastically  different  policies,  police  practices,  urban  layouts,  and  demographic  distributions  (Hipp  &  Williams,  2021).  Through  this  downward influence, city-wide characteristics could affect processes at  smaller geographic levels, providing for unique differences between  cities regarding the spatial distribution of crime. Most research on 

micro-places is often bounded to observations of crime data from a  single city (e.g., Weisburd et al., 2012). City to city comparisons are  rarely conducted, or studies are just conducted in a few high-profile  cities in the United States (e.g., Chicago). Previous scholarship has  argued for the importance of understanding local processes while  recognizing the broader geographic context they are found within  (Andresen & Malleson, 2013). There are only a few examples of research  focused on micro-places that expand the spatial scope beyond a single  city to consider differences between cities (see Hipp & Kim, 2017; Hipp  & Williams, 2021). 

The law of crime concentration is a seminal contribution to the study  of crime and place. The law is constructed around Weisburd (2015)  observations on the distribution of crime across eight cities. The law was  influenced by the previous findings on crime hot spots and the Pareto  Principle or the 80/20 rule (Pareto, 1909). These cities were located  across different regions of the United States, and one was in Israel with  populations ranging from around 70,000 (e.g., Redlands, CA) to over  8,000,000 residents (e.g., New York, NY). In turn, these cities had  disparate social and physical characteristics. For example, the poverty  rate in Cincinnati, OH was more than two times higher than in Seattle,  WA and Tel-Aviv, Israel has only 4.2% Black residents while Brooklyn  Park, MN has 24.0%. The physical characteristics differed regarding the  square milage of the cities and length of street segments. In addition, the  number of crime incidents and crime rates varied drastically between  cities. Despite these differences, across all cities 2–6% of street segments  accounted for 50% of crime incidents. The range of results or “bandwidth” (per Weisburd, 2015) are even closer when separating between  small (i.e., 2–4%) and large (i.e., 4–6%) cities. For example, Ventura, CA  had 3.5% of micro-places account for 50% of incidents while Cincinnati,  OH had 6.0%. In general, the law of crime concentration and the possibility that there are almost no differences in the distribution of crime  across disparate urban environments is a striking proposition to consider  even a few years after its proposal. Tests of the law of crime concentration often explored distributions from a new, unstudied city (e.g.,  Gill, Wooditch, &  Weisburd, 2017) or examine measurements across  varying temporal periods (e.g.,  Haberman, Sorg,  &  Ratcliffe, 2017).  Today, there is a substantial amount of empirical support to demonstrate  the spatial concentration of crime at micro-places (Lee, Eck, & Martinez,  2017). Despite the proposal of this range of values, there is still a lack of  clarity surrounding the applicability of the law of crime concentration. 

Crime and place research has increasingly investigated spatial variability across multiple place-based units of analysis within cities (Deryol  & Payne, 2021; Duru & Kim, 2021; Lee et al., 2017; Umar, Johnson, &  Cheshire, 2021). This is important because the modifiable aerial unit  problem (MAUP) is a foundational consideration for any spatial analysis.  Due to the MAUP, researchers must consider the benefits and costs to  prioritizing any single spatial unit of analysis over the wide range of  alternatives (Openshaw, 1984). Examining multiple spatial levels is  critical because meso- and macro-units can provide important context to  localized processes (Baumer, Wolff, & Arnio, 2012; Hipp & Williams,  2021; Jones & Pridemore, 2019; Lyons, V´elez, & Santoro, 2013). For  example, by studying neighborhoods researchers could miss out on  micro-variability within these units or by studying micro-places researchers ignore the higher-order clustering of patterns within neighborhoods (Ramos, Silva, Clarke,  &  Prates, 2021). To the authors’  knowledge, we present the first study to consider cities as a spatial scale  and to standardized crime variability measures for comparisons at  multiple levels within and between cities. Cities present unique environments and the landmark finding the spatial distribution of crime  between locations is similar should be investigated in much greater  depth. The study of a city’s influence addresses whether the generalizability of key components of research on crime and place might not  extend beyond the boundary of the city examined.

3. Data and methods 

3.1. Units of analysis 

Three spatial units of analysis are observed for our study: census  blocks (i.e., micro-places), census tracts (i.e., neighborhoods), and city  boundaries (i.e., macro-places). Crime and place researchers have relied  upon census boundaries to represent places for almost one hundred  years (see  Lee et al., 2017;  Sampson, Morenoff,  &  Gannon-Rowley,  2002;  Walker  &  Drawve, 2018;  Weisburd, Bernasco,  &  Bruinsma,  2009). These three units of analysis were selected because they are  already nested by-design. This was the most important criteria for the  selection of our units of analysis to facilitate comparisons between  spatial levels. Census blocks are generally representative of street blocks  which grounds these units of analysis within street grids that are often  used for representations of micro-places and capture how people navigate cities (see Grannis, 1998).1 Census blocks are in-turn designed to be  nested within census tracts which are often used as proxies for neighborhoods (Sampson et al., 2002). The size of these units of analysis is  often standardized across cities in the U.S. which permits their comparison between these distinct locations (see Peterson & Krivo, 2010). A  base map for each spatial level was created by obtaining the appropriate  boundary data from the tidycensus and tigris packages in R provided by  the U.S. Census Bureau. 

This study used a systematic approach to identify a convenience  sample of cities. This provides a contrast to Weisburd (2015) study on  the law of crime concentration which created a convenience sample  without much discussion of why the cities were selected for comparison.  The increased availability of public data provides numerous options of  cities that could be considered for inclusion in our analysis. The construction of our sample was guided by several selection criteria. First,  the sample was restricted to U.S. cities with populations  >100,000  residents (U.S. Census Bureau, 2019). Although Weisburd (2015) does  examine two cities with populations under 100,000 and one international city, we decided to restrict our eligibility. Larger cities minimize  concerns about statistical power by providing more potential crime incidents and units of analysis to study, which helps the observation of  variance. Second, we identified only cities that provided all crime incidents reported to the police publicly through an online data portal (see  Appendix Table 1A). Several pre-existing sources were referenced to  determine which cities had public data and a hand-search of each of the  remaining cities was conducted.2  Third, the available crime incident  report data had to span five years. The use of multiple years helped  minimize the influence of any potential outlier years within the distribution. We selected from 2015 to 2019 which covers the last five full  years before the COVID-19 pandemic started began (i.e., March 2020).  Fourth, the crime incident data had to include XY coordinates to minimize geocoding concerns across these disparate jurisdictions.

3.3. Analytic strategy 

This study used a multi-stage analytic strategy. The first stage  involved observing summary descriptive statistics of the distribution of  robberies between cities. Despite the contribution of the law of crime  concentration, there were several noteworthy critiques over recent years  which have helped refine research on measuring the distribution of  crime at places. The use of distributional thresholds for the law (e.g.,  50% of incidents) was critiqued for offering arbitrary cutoff points to  measure concentration (see Mohler, Short, & Brantingham, 2017). Gini  coefficients have become a common replacement which are often used  across varying disciplines to provide a more comprehensive assessment  of distributional concentration (see Gini, 1912). In general, these coefficients are a numerical summary of statical concentration varying  between 0 and 1. They are calculated as the proportion of space between  the line of perfect equality (i.e., 1% of units, 1% of observations) and the  Lorenz curve compared to the total space below the line (Lorenz, 1905).  These coefficients can also be adjusted for samples where the number of  micro-places  is  larger  than  the  number  of  crimes  which  presents

The distribution of robbery incidents  is  observed  at  both  micro-places  and  neighborhoods  then  compared between cities to offer the first portrait of the influence of  these locations. 

In the second stage of analysis, we conducted hierarchical variance  partitioning or decomposition to determine the unique contribution of  each unit of analysis to the total spatial variability of robbery patterns.  This analytic framework expands upon recent studies which have conducted variance partitioning for only within-city units of analysis  despite the framework being malleable to account for any spatial units  (see Steenbeek &  Weisburd, 2016). Effectively, our methodology integrates research on crime concentration and hierarchical spatial variance. Both concentration and spatial variability together are helpful  tools to understand the broader distribution of crime. We define spatial  variability as an analysis of crime variance across different hierarchical  spatial scales for which the measurements could change based upon the  aggregations of place (Spencer, 2022). Multi-level negative binomial  models were estimated to determine the variability attributed to blocks,  tracts, and cities. The use of this model is an improvement upon prior  research that often ignores the count nature of crime incident data. The  overarching goal of this study is to quantify the city-level effect (i.e.,  level-3) on estimates of the spatial variability of robbery. This study  examines the research question: what is the contribution of cities to  understanding the spatial distribution of crime? In the simplest words,  do cities matter? We use the 4% range (i.e., 2–6% of locations) from the  law of crime concentration established to categorize differences between cities in the percent of micro-places which have 50% of incidents  as a point of comparison to determine if there is a similar range of estimates for spatial variability.6

The designation of hierarchical nesting implies a spatial relationship of level-1 units within level-2 units  since they are treated as connected. 4. Results 

4.1. Descriptive statistics 

Table 3 presents the summary descriptive statistics for the distribution of robbery across cities. 5. Discussion 

This study provided a novel analysis and unique insight into the role  of cities for understanding the spatial distribution of robbery patterns.  Our results highlight the role of higher-order spatial units such as  neighborhoods and cities when estimating proportions of robbery variability. Like other research that has explored spatial variability, our  work further supports findings of nested crime variability with micro-  units accounting for most of the total variability and with neighborhoods accounting for a small but essential share of the variability  (Quick, 2019; Schnell et al., 2017; Steenbeek & Weisburd, 2016). Our  results are the first to systematically examine between-city differences of  the spatial variability of robbery. 

Overall, our findings were mixed on the influence of cities on the  distribution of robbery. Our main modeling strategy found almost no  impact of cities. This is despite the vast differences between the cities  such as geographic size, number of robbery incidents, and the number of  spatial units. An alternate strategy and our descriptive analyses noted  more moderate differences of VPC estimates and concentration between  the cities. We believe these mixed findings lead to two major conclusions  which presents a much murkier portrait of the relationship between  cities and the spatial distribution of crime than the law of crime concentration first offered. 

First,  our  findings  support  the  general  conclusion  of  previous  research which notes remarkable similarities between cities in the  concentration and distribution of crime incidents. The distribution of  crime between cities does appear to fit some ironclad statistical properties like those suggested by the law of crime concertation. In general,  these  findings  still  support  the  general  conclusion  from  previous  research which is the distribution of crime between cities is overwhelmingly similar and the largest contribution to the total spatial  variability of crime is from micro-places. Even the analyses which  denote a moderate impact of cities on the distribution of robbery see this  effect as much less salient than the differences between micro-places.  While the matter of quantifying this variance is unresolved or even  open for debate regarding its utility there does appear to be an emerging  foundation of evidence which generally suggests the difference between  cities are not large. On one hand, the differences are more substantial  than first indicated by the law of crime concentration. On the other

Second, the mixed findings suggest continued research  to clarify these results and provide a more sophisticated theoretical  justification for why cities or other macro-units of analysis might matter  in shaping the distribution of crime. There is still a gap in conceptual  understanding of this issue because decades of scholarship suggest cities  are distinct ecological behavior settings, which could influence social  outcomes. Our results present a seemingly dissonant contrast between  two realities where we suggest there are many similarities between  cities, but future research needs to continue investigating the differences  for the sake of refining place-based criminological theory and crime  prevention strategies. 

There is not much theoretical development on how city-level char-

acteristics are connected to the distribution of crime at varying spatial  scales within these locations. In addition, what is the effect of other  macro-places such as counties, states, and countries? The shifting of  these explanations to the city-level presents some conceptual challenges.  This is critical to further understand because the variation of these  physical and social characteristics within cities is critical to explaining  crime variation (Weisburd et al., 2012). There are robust literatures on  both micro and neighborhood spatial scales but little consideration of  how they both could be integrated together to address city-variation (see  Wilcox & Tillyer, 2017). Both units of analysis explore the impact of the  varying social and physical characteristics of locations (Weisburd et al.,  2012). For example, the varying of physical characteristics of cities  could address how the locations expansion was limited by geography  such as a body of water or differences in the population, number of units  of analysis, and even the street grids between cities. For instance, “Rust  belt” cities and “sun belt” cities are entirely different in the time periods  their populations shifted. These theories are at the center of increased  efforts to integrate neighborhood and micro-based explanations within  cities (see Jones & Pridemore, 2019). In general, this creates a paradox  because how can these social and physical characteristics matter so  much for explaining crime variation within cities but not between cities? 

The use of a specific crime such as robbery allows for a more  grounded construction of a theory to consider the spatial variability of  crime across macro-scales. Motivated offenders make decisions based on  characteristics of small places and there are social characteristics of  neighborhoods that explain variation in patterns (Bernasco &  Block,  2011; Sampson & Raudenbush, 1999; Wright & Decker, 1997). Based on  our assessment these key differences between cities could include the  number of locations that experience crime, the degree of distributional  concentration of these incidents, and extent of variability across key  spatial units of analysis. In simpler terms, the spatial footprint of where  robberies occur within cities is seemingly connected in some capacity to  the social and physical characteristics of the place. Some cities could  experience more diffuse distributions of robbery indicating more places  are exposed to risk. These physical characteristics could facilitate the  availability of more criminal opportunities due to the layout of locations  or the social characteristics could suggest there is more economic  inequality in a location which makes more residents susceptible to  committing robbery. This would suggest there are differences between  large places, but these are because of the impact of the distribution of  these characteristics. The consideration of macro-level variation requires an entirely new perspective on crime variation at places but also  presents a clear domain to extend previous theories. 

Our findings provide reinforcement for the continued adoption of  place-based crime prevention strategies. Spatial variability reflects there  are more differences in the distribution between units of analysis  compared to the other units of analysis examined. For micro-places,  there are more unit-to-unit differences between locations than neighborhoods. Interventions such as problem-oriented policing or crime  prevention through environmental design (CPTED) strike a balance of  being  generally  adaptable  across  a  wide-range  of  cities  but  also  malleable enough to account for unique characteristics of very specific 

locations that facilitate crime (Goldstein, 1979, 1990; Hinkle, Weisburd,  Telep, & Peterson, 2020). Our findings indicate there could be something smaller but unique about spatial robbery patterns between cities  policymakers should consider. While we do find some evidence of between city effects, these estimates are not large enough to present much  pause for the continued use of  “one size fits all”  place-based crime  prevention strategies between cities. These strategies provide some  room for modification based on jurisdictions but are built on the foundation they are widely generalizable. Our finding of one outlier city (i.e.,  Philadelphia) regarding the spatial distribution does leave the door open  for the possibility some cities could see potentially important differences. We believe these findings provide support for policy or program  interventions to address higher-order places such as neighborhoods that  provide a small but critical influence on spatial crime patterns. For a few  cities in our study, tract-level variance was higher than expected. Much  variability occurs at micro-places but to unlock the next wave of crime  reduction these larger units of analysis are critical to understand. 

There were several limitations to this study. First, the number of  cities observed is only a small fraction of the locations with populations  that exceed 100,000 residents. We attempted a systematic approach, but  our sample would still be classified as a convenience sample and is not  generalizable to every city in the United States. That is our results  explain the spatial variability of robbery in these cities only. However,  the differences between these eight cities are great in many ways and  can at least provide a basic understanding of how spatial variability  differs between cities with little similarities other than providing open  data. On the later point though, it is possible that certain cities are more  or less likely to provide incident data, and this could affect our results.  We also could not account for the fact that while growing, the number of  cities that provided public data is still small. Future research should  continue to explore additional cities. 

There are inherent concerns when micro-units far exceed the number  of crimes being analyzed (see Andresen & Linning, 2012; Bernasco &  Steenbeek, 2017). Although we address this issue for our measurement  of Gini coefficients the broader concern permeates all analyses on spatial  variability, which create a bias towards micro-places. Our findings are  limited to variance estimates of robbery, particularly all robbery types.  We  also  recognize  there  are  likely  different  patterns  of  robbery  depending on the type, however, we did not incorporate these differences into our analyses (see Andresen & Linning, 2012; Harper, Khey, &  Nolan, 2013). Other crime types were not examined which could in-

fluence our observed estimates of variance. This is important because of  the law of crime concentration, which we use as a comparison examines  total crime instead of robbery. Furthermore, future research will need to  incorporate different spatial weighting processes to better understand  the proximal influences within and between units. Opportunities also  exist to incorporate models which can provide a more sophisticated  treatment of these complex spatial-temporal processes. Replicating our  approach, future research should incorporate more variables such as  different spatial lags and or city-level characteristics to the models. The  latter could help in establishing a specific theoretical explanation for this  variance. 

In conclusion, our study finds mixed evidence of differences between  cities in the spatial variability of robbery patterns. We reexamine one of  the most compelling findings from research on crime and place over the  past few years. In general, these results suggest cities are much more  similar than they are different regarding the distribution of crime. More  so, our approach to examine city-to-city differences has found that  despite the discernable differences between two cities such as Los  Angeles and Orlando, estimates of the spatial variability of robbery  between cities are not drastically different. Essentially, there is only a  small amount of evidence there are unique spatial patterns of robbery  incidents between cities. Our estimates were both larger and aligned  with the first attempt to observe this phenomenon from the law of crime  concentration. This study expanded upon the law of crime concentration  to present a new framework which incorporates new concepts and

methodologies. The contribution of micro-places is well-defined within  crime and place research. Most of the total spatial variability is attributed to micro-places and there still is a large degree of similarity between cities. This presents a nuanced reality where cities are relatively  comparable but the differences along the margins are still potentially  worth exploration. There is much more room to explore how these 

micro-places interface with the higher-order spatial scales they are  nested within which plays a key role to help understanding crime  variation. Neighborhoods and cities are two of the more salient ones  considered. Our study complements prior crime and place research  while offering avenues for future research to build upon.
