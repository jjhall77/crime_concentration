## 1. Introduction

The crime and place literature now spans over 35 years with the  original work stemming from that of  Sherman, Gartin, and Buerger  (1989) and Eck and Weisburd (1995). This research, has developed over  the  past  35  years  identifying  a  number  of  empirical  regularities,  including the law of crime concentration at places that states “for a  defined measure of crime at a specific microgeographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages  for a defined cumulative proportion of crime” (Weisburd, 2015, p. 138).  Moreover, in a recent review, Weisburd, Zastrow, Kuen, and Andresen  (2024) found that crime concentrations at place are consistent across  many countries finding that an average of 4.5 % of street segments account for 50 % of crime.

In this paper, we consider the baseline distribution for statistically  testing the (generalized) Gini coefficient in order to test the law of crime  concentration at places. As such, we build on the work of  Chalfin,  Kaplan, and Cuellar (2021) who show that crime concentrations may  occur simply at random. We discuss why a random distribution is the  appropriate null hypothesis to consider, as well as use locally-based  testing to identify the number and percentage of locations that are  driving any statistically significant differences. This is a novel approach  to the use of the Gini coefficient because the Gini coefficient is a global  metric  describing  concentration  across  the  entire  study  area.  The  approach put forth here identifies the places driving the global concentration, providing a local approach. Consequently, we show that  though crime incidents are statistically significantly more concentrated  than a random distribution and the percentage of places that are driving  that result is quite small.

### 2.1. Concentration metrics

There are a number of concentration metrics that have been used in  the crime and place literature since the work of Sherman et al. (1989).  The most often used metric calculates the percentage of places that are  necessary to account for 50 % of crime (Weisburd et al., 2024) and relates to the law of crime concentration at places that states:  “for a  defined measure of crime at a specific microgeographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages  for a defined cumulative proportion of crime” (Weisburd, 2015, p. 138).  This metric has the benefit of a simple and easy interpretation (Chalfin  et al., 2021), but is also relatively easy to calculate, using only a  spreadsheet and simple equations.

Other metrics have addressed the issue of having fewer events than  places, such as only counting the number of places that have any crime  in  the  denominator  (Andresen  &  Malleson,  2011).  This  metric  is  imperfect, however, because it does not account for the total possible  spread of crime, only where it occurs—similar to the previous metric, it  also only considers 50 % of the crime distribution. The Andresen and  Weisburd metric (Andresen & Weisburd, in press) resolves this issue by  using the total number of crime as the denominator, thereby accounting  for the maximum dispersion of crime. Hipp and Kim (2017) inverted the  original concentration metric to calculate the percentage of crime that  the top 5 % of street segments contain. However, as noted by Weisburd  et al. (2024), this inverted metric inherently imposes unnecessary  variation because of the inversion.

The Gini coefficient calculated based on the values along the Lorenz  curve. The Lorenz curve, in the context of the crime and place literature,  plots the cumulative percentage of streets segments (x-axis) and the  cumulative percentage of crime (y-axis), see Fig. 1. The Gini coefficient,  see Fig. 1, is the ratio of (Area A)/(Area A + Area B); this captures the  amount of deviation from the line of equality in a distribution: the  greater the value (closer to 1) the more concentration. Considering the  Lorenz curve as a whole, this provides researchers with a more complete  visualization of crime concentrations. However, two study areas, or the  same area at different times, with the same Gini coefficient do not  necessarily have the same spatial crime distribution.

The marginal crime concentration index has been put forth that  considers the level of concentration that occurs randomly—this also  accounts for the issue of having fewer crimes than places (Chalfin et al.,  2021). The marginal crime concentration index recognizes that concentration may emerge simply at random, especially when there are  fewer events than places and even when there are notably more events  than places. This is an important empirical fact that needs to be  considered when considering crime concentrations. Built on Weisburd’s  (2015) metric, Chalfin et al. (2021) calculate the marginal crime concentration index using the following equation:  Δcc = E(cc)� cc* (1)  where  Δcc represents the marginal crime concentration. E(cc) is the  expected crime concentration assuming that crime incidents occur  randomly across all places and cc* is the observed crime concentration in  the study area. Both E(cc) and cc*are measured using Weisburd’s (2015) metric (e.g., the percentage of places necessary to account for 50 % of  crime). In this metric, a lower value indicates a higher degree of crime  concentration (i.e., few places are needed to account for 50 % of crime).  Therefore, when crime is more concentrated than one would expect  under a random distribution, cc* will be lower than E(cc). As a result, a  larger value of Δcc indicates a greater degree of observed crime concentration.  One  merit  of  the  marginal  crime  concentration  is  its  straightforward interpretation. According to  Chalfin et al. (2021), it  represents the excess share of places needed to account for a defined  percentage of crime (e.g., 25 or 50 %) relative to the null (random)  distribution.

In a criminological context, O’Brien (2019) suggests that a  Gini coefficient of over 0.50 reflects strong concentration. But because  the Gini coefficient is based on areas under curves, it may be interpreted  as approximately the percentage of events (crime in the current context)  that need to be redistributed in order to have equality in its distribution,  or a Gini coefficient value of zero (Rogerson, 2013). As such, a value of  0.50 suggests that 50 % of the events need to be redistributed in order to  have a Gini coefficient equal to zero, or no concentration. As an extreme  example, consider 1000 criminal events distributed across 100 street  segments. If all criminal events are located on one street segment,  complete concentration (the common unit of analysis in the crime and  place literature), this would lead to a Gini coefficient of 1, whereas if  each street segment has 10 criminal events the Gini coefficient is 0,  resulting in a uniform distribution. As such, in order to achieve a uniform distribution, the traditional baseline of comparison for the Gini  coefficient, 990 of the 1000 criminal events must be redistributed to  have the Gini coefficient go from 1 to 0: 990/1000 = 0.99, or 99 %. This  is clearly not 100 % at the interpretation above implies, but this is why  approximately is an important aspect of this interpretation of the Gini  coefficient. As the number of events decreases relative to the number of  places, this approximation becomes less precise so it should be used with  caution.

In this paper, we contribute to this literature in three dimensions.  First, we consider the (generalized) Gini coefficient as an appropriate  metric to measure crime concentrations, or any type of event that is  expected to concentrate in space and/or time. From this we compare our  calculated Gini coefficients with an expected value and simulated distribution for statistical testing. Should it be zero? Second, we introduce  the marginal (generalized) Gini coefficient as an extension of the marginal crime concentration index (Chalfin et al., 2021). Third, we use a  locally-based point pattern test, discussed below, to identify where any  concentrations are present and how many areas contribute to this concentration. The first two dimensions synthesize different strands of  research within the crime and place literature; we are concerned with  proper testing of the law of crime concentration at places (Weisburd,  2015), and do so by integrating  Bernasco and Steenbeek’s (2017) (generalized) Gini coefficient with Chalfin et al.’s (2021) marginal crime  concentration index. The third dimension identifies the places where  events concentrate using Andresen’s (2009, 2016) spatial point pattern  test and has practical implications in the context of prevention in the  context  of  crime  through  the  identification  of  hots  spots  (Braga,  Turchan, Papachristos, & Hureau, 2019; Braga & Weisburd, 2022). Does  a value Gini coefficient (close to unity) imply many places or just many  events? This provides a specific null hypothesis to test at the local level  to identify where concentration occurs, compared to randomness, not  just identifying if it occurs. We use data from Vancouver, Canada as an  illustrative example regarding the importance of this methodological  concern.

Another important consideration for the Gini coefficient is that it  generates a biased representation of concentration when the number of  events is less than the number of places (Bernasco & Steenbeek, 2017).  In other words, the Gini coefficient overestimates the degree of concentration(i.e. crime concentration in the current context) when there  are fewer events than units of analysis. ## 4. Data and methods

### 4.1. Data

For our analyses below, we use open data from the Vancouver Police  department,  https://vpd.ca/crime-statistics/. These open crime data  account for all property-related crime types reported to the police. These  data are available at the point level. We assign each point its appropriate  unit of analysis and then aggregate them to obtain a total count for each  unit. We use both street segments (n = 13,102) and dissemination areas  (n = 1015) for our analyses below—dissemination areas are equivalent  to the census block groups in the US Census, containing 400–700 persons. The crime types available include all property crime separated into  commercial burglary, residential burglary, mischief, theft, theft from  vehicle, theft of bicycle, and theft of vehicle. Though these data are  available for 2003–2024, we only analyze 2024 data here.

Because of its long-standing presence and ability to address the small  sample problem in crime concentration measurement (fewer events than  places), the Gini coefficient has been used by a number of authors.  Though it has not been used in as widespread of a manner as the original  50 % of crime being accounted for by some percentage of places, it is  becoming  increasingly  common  in  the  crime  and  place  literature  (Weisburd et al., 2024).

It is important to note here that Vancouver’s crime had been falling  since the late 1990s, consistent with the international crime drop  research (Farrell, Hodgkinson,  &  Andresen, 2018;  Farrell, Tilley,  &  Tseloni, 2014). However, from 2012 to 2019, crime in Vancouver had  been increasing slowly. In 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, crime  property crime in Vancouver, and around the world, decreased substantially (Andresen  &  Hodgkinson, 2023;  Hodgkinson  &  Andresen,  2020; Nivette et al., 2021). During the last 5 years, property crime reported to the police has included approximately 30,000 events each year  in Vancouver. The counts for each crime type in 2024 are shown in  Table 1.

The most notable research in the context of our analyses here has  considered alternative baseline comparisons for the Gini coefficient. In  other words, the researchers working in this area argue that using a  uniform distribution (Gini coefficient = 0) is not appropriate for testing  for the presence of concentrations of crime (Mohler, Brantingham,  Carter, & Short, 2019; O’Brien, 2019; O’Brien et al., 2022; Prieto Curiel,  Collignon Delmar, & Bishop, 2018). These researchers have used some  form of a count-based distribution as the baseline to test for the presence  of concentrations.

### 4.2. Analyses

In order to conduct this investigation, we first need to consider a  baseline distribution to test against. Though intuitively enticing because  of the common finding that crime is spatially concentrated and appears  to follow a count-based (Poisson or negative binomial) distribution,  there are at least four separate reasons why this is not an advisable  approach—our list here is not exhaustive. These reasons are based on  the data generating processes assumed in count-based distributions  (Poisson and negative binomial) that assume independence, alternative  hypotheses that are (paradoxically) less precise, and implications for  subsequent investigations into the (spatial) patterns of these events.

As we argue below, though a count-based distribution is an intuitive  baseline to test against, this is not advisable. We show below why this is  not the best null hypothesis for a test for concentrations. We also provide  a method for moving forward and subsequent analyses for making  contributions to the crime and place literature.

## 3. Current study

And fourth, subsequent testing is not well-informed from this first  distributional test. For example, if a researcher is interested in understanding where those differences occur (the focus of our analyses)  imposing a Poisson distribution at the global level will make local level  testing incongruent, from a theoretical perspective. If a simulation is  used at the global level to generate a non-rejection envelope for testing  the null hypothesis of a specific distribution, each iteration will create a  value of a statistic, Gini coefficient for example, that is based on that  distribution. By the Central Limit Theorem, the mean of that distribution  is expected to be the same as the mean of the population and follow a  normal distribution that can be used for testing. So far, so good. If the  researcher then wanted to perform local level testing to identify where  the two distributions differed, they would effectively be using a random  distribution. Why? Because the data generating process for a Poisson, or  negative binomial, distribution assumes independent observations. As  such, the “few places” that have a disproportionate amount of crime are  going to be in different places each iteration. After a large number of  iterations in a simulation, this will all get washed out, leading to what  will appear to be a random distribution at the local level.

For these reasons, we use a random sampling with replacement  technique to identify statistically significant differences from no concentration and the Gini coefficient. As such, we are using Monte Carlo  simulation-based approach. We use random sampling to simulate possibilities where each crime occurs on a pure random basis. When we  sample those street segments, we sample them  without the observed  number of crimes for each street segment. Once the simulation/sampling is complete, we obtain a non-rejection envelope around the  theoretical value when we assume the null hypothesis is true. This envelope provides a distance for the (expected) number of crime incidents  per street segment under the null hypothesis. Lastly, we compare the  observed value with the non-rejection envelope. A bootstrap approach  as a common practice for hypothesis testing of (generalized) Gini Coefficients (Steenbeek & Bernasco, 2018), on the other hand, is a confidence interval around the observed value obtained by resampling street  segments  with  the observed values, not a simulated random data-  generating  process  that  compares  the  observed  value  to  a  non-  rejection envelope constructed under the null hypothesis. These two  different results may appear similar because we compare an interval  with a number, but the logits behind these two approaches are notably  different.

First, the data generating process for these distributions does not  follow what we have known about crime distributions for almost 20  years: crime is concentrated, such that its observations are not independent (Weisburd, Bruinsma, & Bernasco, 2009). As a specific example  that may occur at the same location, the phenomena of repeat and near-  repeat victimization generate crime patterns that are not spatially independent (de Melo, Andresen,  &  Matias, 2018;  Farrell, Phillips,  &  Pease, 1995; Johnson, 2008). A Poisson process, for example, can produce a distribution with a greater number of events at a few places that  when analyzed at the level of a city can be interpreted as concentrated—a small number of places account for a greater percentage of  crime than expected. But this would be fundamentally incorrect because  these few places are unlikely to be spatially concentrated.

Second, if one uses a Poisson distribution, again for example, with a  mean that matches the number of events in a crime distribution as the  null hypothesis, then the alternative hypothesis for any testing is not that  Poisson distribution—there are many Poisson distributions. As such,  rejecting a null hypothesis related to any specific distribution can only  tell us that the distribution we observe is very unlikely to be that specific  distribution and may actually be any other distribution. Though unlikely, the actual distribution may be random or uniform. More likely,  the crime distribution will follow what looks like another count-based  distribution, even a different Poisson distribution that has a mean  other than what was specified for statistical testing. Additionally, it is  important to note that this is likely just appearance because the data  generating process for most crime distributions is not independent  because of spatial concentrations. In short, setting the null hypothesis  for a specific distribution within a family of distributions cannot be  expected to provide much insight into its distribution: rejecting that null  hypothesis can mean random, uniform, concentrated but less so than  expected, or concentrated but more so than expected.

To  facilitate  simple  interpretation,  we  introduce  the  marginal  generalized Gini coefficient as an extension of the marginal crime concentration index (Chalfin et al., 2021). This coefficient can be calculated  using the following equation:  ΔGʹ = Gʹ * � E(Gʹ) (3)  where ΔGʹ represents the marginal generalized Gini coefficient. Gʹ *is the  observed generalized Gini coefficient in the study area and E(Gʹ) is the  expected value assuming that crime incidents occur randomly across all  places. The marginal generalized Gini coefficient is computed in a  similar manner to Chalfin et al. (2021) with the key difference being that  the expected value is subtracted from the observed value. This adjustment  is  necessary  because,  unlike  Weisburd’s  (2015) metric,  a  greater  generalized Gini coefficient indicates a greater degree of crime concentration. When crime is more concentrated than one would be expected under a random distribution, Gʹ *will exceed E(Gʹ). Therefore, a  greater value of ΔGʹreflects a greater degree of observed crime concentration. In other words, it is a generalized Gini coefficient that accounts for expected concentration that may simply occur at random,  unlike the theoretical generalized Gini coefficient that assumes a uniform distribution for statistical testing. If the Gini coefficient can be  interpreted as the percentage of events that would need to be redistributed to achieve a uniform distribution (Rogerson, 2013), then the  marginal (generalized) Gini coefficient can be interpreted as the percentage of events that would need to be redistributed to match a random  distribution, with the cautions noted above regarding how precise this  interpretation can be.

Third, one can generate what appears to be a Poisson distribution  using random sampling with replacement. Any process to generate a  baseline distribution in the context of crime must allow for individual  places to be “sampled” more than once because, as noted above, multiple crimes (events) occur at the same places leading to concentrations  of crime and the law of crime concentration at places (Weisburd, 2015).  Consequently, one may use a Poisson distribution as the null hypothesis,  conduct a statistical test of a crime distribution, and fail to reject that  null  hypothesis.  The  researcher  would  then  likely  move  forward  considering their distribution to be Poisson when, in fact, it is just  random. This ties back to the second reason to not use a Poisson distribution as the null hypothesis and is a reason for never stating that we  accept a null hypothesis or assume that it is true.

From this point, for any context that shows statistically significant  concentrations, we then move to local level statistical testing to identify  those places that are driving that statistically significant difference at the  global level. Here, we use an adaptation of the spatial point pattern test,  developed by Andresen (2009, 2016) and extended by Andresen, Linning, and Malleson (2017) in a longitudinal context and by Wheeler,  Steenbeek, and Andresen (2018) for different testing options. The applications of this test have been in the context of identifying statistically  significant differences at a local level for specific spatial patterns: does  commercial burglary have the same spatial pattern as residential burglary or does robbery have the same spatial pattern as people on the  street.

With these results, a dissimilarity index can be calculated representing the percentage of the areas that exhibit different counts than  expected under randomness. This index also relates back to (complements) the marginal (generalized) Gini coefficient. As noted above, the  marginal (generalized) Gini coefficient can be interpreted as the percentage of events that need to be redistributed to have a random distribution. The dissimilarity index, then, represents the percentage of  places that account for the lack of a random distribution identified using  the marginal (generalized) Gini coefficient; alternatively, the percentage  of places (units of analysis) from which the events must be redistributed  across the study space to have a random distribution of events.

In the original formulation of this test, Andresen (2009) developed it  to compare the spatial patterns of two different sets of events: residential  burglary and robbery, as stated above. One of the datasets was labeled  the base data and the other the test data. Though in principle any data  set could be the base data, it was always another set of events, not a  theoretical distribution—there are tests for complete spatial randomness  in other software programs, for example. In the current analyses, we  generate the base data set through simulations and use actual event data  as the test data. The test performed here is fundamentally the same as  the original SPPT, but adapted for the current research question. In  order to be congruent with the global analyses, we use random sampling  with replacement to generate the baseline spatial pattern to test against  at the local level.

This  relationship  allows  for  more  precise  hypothesis  testing,  depending on the research context. Because of the nature of concentrations, this index will necessarily represent fewer places than events. It  is important to note here that the law of crime concentration at places is  a directional alternative hypothesis with the null hypothesis being a  random distribution. As such, the dissimilarity index represents more  places than are only driving crime concentration: it includes both the  places with greater than and less than the expected number of events,  the total number of places within which the events need to be redistributed. Because of this, we decompose the dissimilarity index into  these two component parts, a concentration and dispersion index.2

This can then be shown on a map to identify places where more or  less than the expected number of events (crimes) occur across the city. It  is important to note, however, that this index is relative to the units of  analysis used. If the units of analysis are changed then the dissimilarity  index may change. This is an application/example of the modifiable  areal unit problem (Openshaw, 1984). Because of this, analyses at  multiple scales are always recommended to confirm or deny locational  characteristics. If a “natural” unit of analysis can be identified (this is  more common in physical geography), the MAUP literature recommends  this unit of analysis to be included (Andresen  &  Malleson, 2013);  however, it is important to note that in the social sciences, including the  spatial analyses of crime, we are often restricted to administrative  boundaries that are not designed for the construct under study. Additionally, it is important to not undertake too many comparisons or  consider units of analysis too “small” in order to avoid analyzing less  signal and more noise, if the signal is of interest (Malleson, Steenbeek, &  Andresen, 2019).

To fit the current context, we generalized the original spatial point  pattern test to the following testing methodology: 

- 1. Randomly sample from all microgeographic units (street segments or  dissemination areas) with the sample size equal to the observed  number of crime incidents within the study area, with replacement.  Count the number of times that each unit is sampled. This produces a  baseline data set by simulating crime occurring randomly across all  units.
- 2. Repeat the previous step 1000 times (or more if desired).
- 3. Count the number of observed points within each unit. This constitutes the test data set.
- 4. For each area in the baseline data set, generate a 95 % nonparametric  non-rejection envelope by removing the top and bottom 2.5 % of all  counts (25 from the top and 25 from the bottom in this case). The  minimum and maximum of the remaining counts represent the non-  rejection envelope.
- 5. Compare the count in the test data set (the crime types being  investigated) to the non-rejection envelope generated from the  baseline dataset. If the test data count falls within the non-rejection  envelope, then the two datasets exhibit similarity in the given unit.  Otherwise, they are significantly different. We create a dissimilarity  indicator,  taking  1  to  represent  difference  and  0  to  represent  indifference.

Because, as noted above (Chalfin et al., 2021), even under  random sampling (with replacement) apparent concentrations may  occur. Hence, this shows the importance of not using a uniform distribution (Gini = 0) as the baseline for testing for statistically significant  concentrations even though we always reject the null hypothesis in these  examples.

The expected  values for the Gini coefficients based on randomization, however, are  also high, ranging from 0.11 to 0.51. Similar to the street segment results, all of the generalized Gini coefficients are statistically significantly  greater than the expected value, but this still points to the importance of  the baseline distribution for statistical testing. Curiously, the expected  value of the Gini coefficients generally decreases as the number of events  increases relative to the number of units. This is a result similar to that  found by Chalfin et al. This is highlighted  with the marginal generalized Gini coefficient that ranges from 0.17 to  0.67. Also similar to the street segment results, the dissimilarity index is  quite low for most of the crime types, ranging from 0.04 to 0.87. Again,  this indicates that despite the presence of concentrations shown in the  generalized Gini coefficients and the percentage of events/crimes that  need to be redistributed, the percentage of places that need to be  included in that redistribution is relatively small most of the time—theft  and property crime overall are the exceptions. Intuitively, the number of  places that need to have events redistributed from decreases as the  number of events for that crime type decreases.

In this context the marginal generalized Gini coefficient shows a  range of 0.14 to 0.50. As such, the percentage of events that need to be  redistributed ranges from approximately 15 to 50 % in order to have a  distribution that is not distinguishable from random. For most crime  types, this is a notable difference from the generalized Gini coefficient  and shows the utility of the marginal approach to measuring crime  concentrations (Chalfin et al., 2021).

Decomposing the dissimilarity index into a concentration index and  a dispersion Index, the results are different from the street segment results. For the lower count crime types (theft of vehicle, theft of bicycle,  residential burglary, and commercial burglary), all of the dissimilarity is  contained within concentrations (the number of events is greater than  expected). Only when the crime counts increase (mischief, theft from  vehicle, theft, and property crime) does dispersion being to present itself. As such, 4.2 to 11.6 % of dissemination areas are responsible for  driving the global crime distribution statistically significantly more  concentrated than a random distribution.

An interesting result, but not particularly surprising, in Table 2 is the  dissimilarity index. In all cases, the dissimilarity index is less than 6 %.  This means that no more than 6 % of the places (street segments) are  responsible for the statistically significant differences in the (generalized) Gini coefficients. Though most people may not be surprised that a  fraction of the units of analysis is driving the overall result, the magnitude of this dissimilarity index was not expected. In the context of theft,  though 43.3 % of events (crimes) need to be redistributed for no concentration to be present (random distribution), this only needs to occur  at 3.5 % of places (street segments). This is still almost 500 street segments, but still a low percentage, overall.

Theoretically, this should not be a surprise. In the extreme, a Gini  coefficient that is one/unity simply means that one place or individual,  for example, has all of the events/items under investigation. However,  these high degrees of concentration are occurring in contexts that do not  always have such high value of the generalized Gini coefficient. This has  important implications, not only theoretically for understanding why  crime concentrates as much as it does, but practically in the context of  prevention.

More recently, researchers have considered using count-based data  generated processes in order to test for statistically significant concentrations (Mohler et al., 2019; O’Brien, 2019; O’Brien et al., 2022; Prieto  Curiel et al., 2018). We have argued, above, that such a method for  testing statistical significance is not advisable for a number of reasons.  These include, but are not limited to, the assumption of independence,  alternative hypotheses that are (paradoxically) less precise, and implications for subsequent investigations into the (spatial) patterns of these  events. We do, however, agree that a purely uniform distribution is not  the appropriate underlying distribution to assume when testing for  statistical significance of the Gini coefficient. Rather, we argue for the  use of random sampling with replacement because it allows for some  randomness in the distribution of the events, including the possibility of  the known possibility to apparently have concentrations occurring at  random.

## 6. Discussion

The Gini coefficient is a statistic measuring concentration that has  been in use for over 100 years (Gini, 1909). Over the past 10 years, the  Gini coefficient has been used in the crime and place literature to  measure concentration (Bernasco & Steenbeek, 2017) as a statistic to  test the law of crime concentration at places (Weisburd, 2015). Bernasco  and Steenbeek (2017) not only introduced the Gini coefficient into this  literature but extended it to address the low event count issue that  commonly arises in crime and place: fewer events/crimes than places.  This issue can make concentrations appear greater than they are.

We  show  that  using  random  sampling  with  replacement  and  extending the marginal crime concentration metric of  Chalfin et al.  (2021) can produce expected values of the generalized Gini coefficient  appearing to show high levels of concentration of crime. Though our  observed generalized Gini coefficients always exhibited statistically  significant concentrations, the expected values are, in some cases, quite  high—greater than 0.50. Perhaps most interesting, however, we find  that the percentage of places that account for the concentrations is  surprising quite low—most often less than 5 %. Taken together, our  illustrative example provides support for the law of crime concentration  at places (Weisburd, 2015) because a small percentage of places account  for the areas where actual counts of crime are greater than expected  from randomness.

One of the practical implications of the law of crime concentration at  places is that tactics such as hotspot policing can be very effective at  decreasing crime (Braga et al., 2019; Braga & Weisburd, 2022). This is  most certainly the case shown here as well. Both the standard metric in  the crime and place literature (on average, 4.5 % of places count for 50  % of crime) and the Gini coefficient have shown this in the literature  (Weisburd et al., 2024). However, we have shown here that even when  the generalized Gini coefficient does not indicate a high degree of concentration, any concentration that is present is still generated by a small  number of places. We have only considered the impact here using  property crime types with an obvious need for replication considering  both property and violent crime. Regardless, place-based initiatives for  the reduction of crime may not only be instructive in  traditionally  considered crime-ridden areas (e.g., inner cities),  but in all areas that  experience actual counts of crime that are greater than expected counts.

However, the point of our techniques put forth is not to support any  testing of laws or empirical regularities, but to have a better foundation  for the testing for levels of concentration. Moreover, it is important to  note that the use of the dissimilarity index is not contingent of the  (generalized) Gini coefficient being statistically significant. This portion  of the proposed methodology could still be used to identify local deviations from randomness. Though one must be cautious of false positives in such a situation, it may be the case that marginal generalized  Gini may not be significant because it is a global statistic: there may be  significant local differences that get washed out at the global level,  similar  to  other  global  and  local  spatial  statistics  and  techniques  (Andresen, 2022; Anselin, 1995; Fotheringham, Brunsdon, & Charlton,  2002). As a result, our approach here is not only refinement of the  application of the Gini coefficient, but an articulation of a framework  that integrates global and local measures.
