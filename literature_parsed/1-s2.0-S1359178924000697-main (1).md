A B S T R A C T

Our paper reports on a systematic review of crime concentration studies over the last 35 years. We identify 47 papers that report on crime concentrations at a micro geographic unit of analysis. These papers produced 49 estimates of general crime concentration for crime incidents at streets segments for a specific cumulative proportion of crime. The median concentration for these estimates is 50 % of crime found at 4.5 % of streets, and 25 % of crime at 1.25 % of streets. The bandwidth for 50 % crime concentration is 9.3 % (1.7–11.0 %), and for 25 % crime concentration 3.0 % (0.4–3.4 %). Using the interquartile range to exclude outliers, we found a bandwidth of 2.5 % (3.2–5.7 %) for 50 % of crime, and 1.4 % (0.8–2.2 %) for 25 % of crime. Crime concentration was generally stronger for specific types of crime, and bandwidths of concentration were generally smaller. Using alternative measures of crime and different micro geographies produces similar conclusions regarding strong crime concentration at place with narrow bandwidths. Our review shows that a general framework of a law of crime concentration applies across a large number of cities across many regions around the world.

1. Introduction

The idea that crime concentrates is not a new one. As Eck and Weisburd (1995) note: “Crime events are not uniformly distributed, a fact known for over a century. At every level of aggregation, some geographic areas have less crime than others” (p. 12). This has been demonstrated historically at multiple levels of geography ranging from entire nations or regions (Ducp´etiaux, 1827; Guerry, 1864) down to neighborhoods (Sampson, 2012; Sampson & Groves, 1989; Shaw & McKay, 1942). However, the study of crime at micro-geographic units of analysis began to interest criminologists only in the late 1980s (Evans & Herbert, 1989; Felson, 1987; Pierce et al., 1988; Sherman et al., 1989; Weisburd et al., 1992; Weisburd & Green, 1994). In 1989, Sherman et al. coined the term criminology of place to describe this new area of study. The criminology of place (see also Weisburd, Groff, & Yang, 2012) or crime and place (see Eck & Weisburd, 1995) pushes scholars to examine very small geographic areas within cities, often as small as addresses or street segments (a street from intersection to intersection), for their contribution to the crime problem. Many studies since the late 1980s have found that there is significant clustering of crime at microgeographic units of analysis (e.g., see Andresen & Malleson, 2011;

Braga et al., 2014; Brantingham & Brantingham, 1999; Crow & Bull, 1975; Curman et al., 2015; Pierce et al., 1988; Roncek, 2000; Sherman et al., 1989; Weisburd et al., 1992, 2004, 2009; Weisburd & Amram, 2014; Weisburd & Green, 1994; Weisburd, Lawton, & Ready, 2012).

Practically, the strong concentration of crime at place has had important implications for the distribution of police and other public safety resources. The places where crime is most highly concentrated are typically referred to as crime hot spots (Sherman & Weisburd, 1988, 1995). These small geographic areas with high levels of criminal activity relative to other places in the city have been the focus of a series of interventions to target police presence, enforcement, and activity. The first formal evaluation of hot spots policing, the Minneapolis Hot Spots Patrol Experiment, found that concentrated patrol at high crime street segments successfully reduced crime calls for service and observed disorder (Sherman & Weisburd, 1995). Since that time, there have been over 70 quasi-experimental and experimental tests of the hot spots policing approach. In a meta-analysis of these findings, Braga et al. (2019) conclude that there is significant evidence that hot spots policing at micro-geographic places reduces crime (see also Braga & Weisburd, 2022; Weisburd & Majmundar, 2018).

Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) observed not only that crime was concentrated, but across a 16-year observation period the level of concentration was remarkedly consistent: 50 % of crime incidents were found at between 4.7 % and 6.1 % of street segments in the city each year. This consistency was even more startling because while crime concentrations remained very stable, the city experienced a 24 % decline in crime incidents during this period. Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) argued that there was a “law of crime concentration” operating in Seattle, WA and perhaps across other cities. In 2015, Weisburd (2015) examined eight cities and confirmed these earlier observations in Seattle across cities. While noting variability between the five larger and three smaller cities, the overall range or bandwidth of crime concentrations observed was between 2.1 % and 6.0 % of streets producing 50 % of city crime, and between 0.4 % and 1.6 % producing 25 % of city crime. Recognizing that crime concentrations may be affected by the type of data used, the types of crime examined, and the specific microgeographic unit employed, Weisburd (2015) proposed a formal statement regarding how a law of crime concentration could be conceptualized: “For a defined measure of crime at a specific micro-geographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages for a defined cumulative proportion of crime” (p. 138).

Our paper reports on a systematic review of crime concentration studies over the last 35 years. We find that most studies employ the metrics suggested by Weisburd (2015) and accordingly allow us to assess the “narrow bandwidth” assumption based on a large number of observations both for general crime and for specific types of crime. At the same time, we identify a number of studies using different metrics and different methodological approaches, which allow us to identify whether the law of crime concentration appears robust under different assumptions.

2. Defining a law of crime concentration

Though the idea of a law of crime concentration at places was first raised by Weisburd et al. (2012), remarkable consistency in levels of concentration of crime were observed in two of the first studies in this area. In a study of “dispatched calls for police service” for one year in the mid-1980s, Sherman et al. (1989; see also Sherman, 1987) report that only 3.3 % of the addresses in Minneapolis, MN, accounted for 50.4 % of all crime calls to the police. In a study done at about the same time, Pierce et al. (1988), found that in Boston, MA, between 1977 and 1982, 3.6 % of addresses generated 50.1 % of “total demands for police services.” Importantly, both studies used emergency calls for police service generated by computer aided dispatch (CAD) systems and examined the same units of analysis (addresses) and a general crime measure (covering the vast array of problems dealt with by the police).

Interestingly, while scholars began to develop a large number of studies of crime concentration in the decades that followed, they did not focus on the consistencies of such crime concentrations. One reason for this is likely the variability in the types of data used, the units of analysis examined, and the types of crime examined. For example, Eck et al. (2000) examined crime calls for service at addresses but looked only at robberies, assaults, burglaries, grand larcenies and auto thefts. They found that the most active 10 % of places (in terms of crime) in the Bronx, NY, and Baltimore, MD, accounted for approximately 32 % of these crimes. Looking only at public places, such as high schools, public housing projects, subway stations, and parks, Spelman (1995) found that the worst 10 % of locations produced 50 % of crime calls for service. Weisburd and Mazerolle (2000) studied drug markets, that often included clusters of street segments. They found that approximately 20 % of all disorder crimes and 14 % of crimes against persons were concentrated in just 56 drug hot spots in Jersey City, NJ, an area that comprised only 4.4 % of street segments and intersections in the city (see also Weisburd & Green, 1995).

The variability of focus of these early studies reinforced the general idea that crime concentrates, and this observation was widely accepted

by criminologists. In this regard, Wilcox and Eck (2011) in a policy essay in Criminology and Public Policy focusing on the importance of facilities (e.g., banks, apartments, bars, or treatment centers) as crime generators, argued that “only a few places within any category of busy facility are really troublesome” (p. 476). This led them to propose an “iron law of troublesome places” that assumes only a small group of places of a specific type of facility generate the majority share of crime problems. Wilcox and Eck (2011) suggested that their observation may apply to micro-geographic studies of crime more generally (Duru, 2010; Pierce et al., 1988; Sherman et al., 1989; Weisburd et al., 2004). But their observation did not define empirically the extent of concentration, nor propose a specific measurement for estimating the consistency of crime concentrations.

This was the main contribution of Weisburd (2015) in defining the law of crime concentration at places. As noted earlier, Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2012) first proposed a law of crime concentration in the book, The Criminology of Place, based on the consistency of crime concentrations in Seattle, WA, over a 16-year study period. The law of crime concentration at places across cities was to be formally stated by Weisburd (2015) in his Sutherland Address at the American Society of Criminology meetings in 2014. Weisburd argued that an understanding of the consistency of crime concentrations could not be gained unless scholars used consistent definitions and data. Differences in levels of concentration observed could be explained by the use of different measures of crime, or focus on different units of analysis, or different types of crime. Accordingly in proposing a definition of the law of crime concentration, Weisburd (2015) included these metrics as key to developing bandwidths for a law of crime concentration: “For a defined measure of crime at a specific micro-geographic unit, the concentration of crime will fall within a narrow bandwidth of percentages for a defined cumulative proportion of crime” (p. 138).

The narrow bandwidth described by Weisburd (2015), referred to the differences that would be observed in the percentage of crime that would be found for defined cumulative proportions of crime, for example, 25 % or 50 % of crime, in studies across cities. Weisburd (2015) does not define the bandwidth in his proposition in part because he could look only at eight cities in his paper. He called for scholars to develop studies of the law of crime concentration across larger numbers of cities.

The law of crime concentration assumes that most cities will have similar levels of concentration, but Weisburd (2015) did not assume that all cities would fall within a narrow bandwidth. Indeed, he argued that particular interest should be given to cities that appear to be deviant in respect to crime concentrations. He suggested that such deviant cases could provide insight into the underlying mechanisms generating crime concentration. As such, the law of crime concentration states that crime concentrates in surprisingly similar ways across cities, but recognizes in its call for establishing bandwidths that there will be variability across cities as well. This type of nuance is difficult to understand if one is looking for absolute relationships, but fits the description of crime concentrations.

Of course, there remains the question of what can be defined as a “narrow bandwidth.” The noted psychologist, Jacob Cohen (1988), in developing the commonly used metrics of small, medium, and large effect sizes in statistical power analysis, noted that the proposed conventions while “arbitrary, will be found to be reasonable by reasonable people” (p. 13). We take the same approach here. Prior research showing that crime concentrations generally vary just a few percentage points around 5 % for the 50 % criterion, certainly appear to be a narrow bandwidth as contrasted with one that would range four or five times that bandwidth. Even when scholars have argued that random fluctuations in crime distributions are likely to lead to departures from a simple uniform spread of crime incidents across a city, the degree of concentrations observed have been striking. The observation of even a narrow bandwidth, of course, raises the question of whether or not we should be using the term “law” given the variability that is observed. There is no accepted definition of what a law in science is. A National Academy of Sciences (1998) report on teaching evolution on the nature of science, defined a scientific law as “(a) descriptive generalization about how some aspect of the natural world behaves under stated circumstances” (p. 5). In a volume on science education, McComas (2014) defines a scientific law as a “basic principle, generalization, regularity or rule that holds true universally under particular conditions” (p. 58, see also McComas, 2003).

Often, scientific laws are described in exact mathematical terms. For example, Newton’s law of universal gravitation is represented by a mathematical equation. It provides an exact representation of how gravity exerts force on objects, such as an apple falling from a tree. NASA, in a teacher’s guide, notes that scientific laws are “often a mathematical statement of how two or more quantities relate to each other” (https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2009/07/371711 main_smii_problem23.pdf). Accordingly, scientific laws often describe exact mathematical relationships.

But laws in science are not always exact in this way and can be descriptions of general relationships that are found to be consistently true across repeated experiments or observations. McComas (2013) notes that “(i)n biology, many laws operate less well and offer less secure predictions than in other sciences. For example, a law called Bergman’s Body Rule states that animals in colder regions are fatter than related animals in warmer regions. This is a useful law but it functions only about 75% of the time” (p. 58). Perhaps the most relevant example of a commonly stated law in science that is similar to the proposition of a law of crime concentration at places is Tobler’s (1970) first law of geography: “I invoke the first law of geography: everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things” (p. 236). Miller (2004), writing in retrospect about Tobler’s law in the Annals of the Association of American Geographers, points out that the law does not specify an exact relationship, but that scientific laws “are not required to be absolute truths” (p. 284).

3. Measuring crime concentrations

The most commonly used approach to crime concentration in prior studies is to describe the percentage of streets that produce a set cumulative proportion of crime in the city—usually 25 % or 50 % (see below for our systematic review findings). This was the original metric that Weisburd (2015) used in his paper introducing the law of crime concentration. It has the benefit of easy interpretation both by researchers and policy makers (Chalfin et al., 2021). However, in response to limitations of this approach, researchers have suggested a number of alternative methods.

Generally speaking, when there are as many events (crimes) as there are units of analysis (e.g. street segments), the approach of identifying the proportion of streets that produce a set proportion of crime has been shown to be robust. However, when the number of units of geography, such as streets, is larger than the number of crime events, this approach

becomes misleading. For example, if there are only 100 crimes and 1000 streets, by necessity the proportion of streets producing 50 % of crime will be a small value. Even if the number of crimes were uniformly spread, with 1 crime on each street of 100 streets, just 5 % of streets would produce 50 % of crime. One solution to this problem was first proposed by Andresen and Malleson (2011; see also Levin et al., 2017), who excluded all streets with no crime. This provides a measure of concentration of crime given where crime actually occurs. This metric, by design, does not allow for zero-event units of analysis to be included in its calculation so the levels of concentration are not exaggerated by a relatively small number of crimes relative to geographic units examined. This approach has been found most useful when examining specific types of crime where the number streets is more likely to be larger than the number of crimes (e.g. see Andresen, Curman, & Linning, 2017; Andresen, Linning, & Malleson, 2017).

Just as the levels of concentration are likely to be overstated when the number of crimes is less than the number of streets, some researchers have argued that the decision to measure the proportion of streets that produce a set proportion of crime minimizes variation and accordingly challenges the idea that there is a narrow bandwidth for crime concentration. Hipp and Kim (2017) for example, sought to identify the proportion of crime that is produced by 5 % of streets (as opposed to the proportion of streets that produce 50 % of crime). While taking this approach led to an average outcome of the top 5 % of streets producing about 50 % of crime (similar to the findings reported by Weisburd, 2015), they identified wide bandwidths of crime concentration. Lee et al. (2017), taking the same approach, again find that on average the top 5 % of micro-geographic places produce proportions of crime close to that reported by Weisburd (2015), but also note very large bandwidths.

This is the case in part because of a statistical artifact of moving the focus of a point in a distribution ranging from 0 to 100 closer to the midpoint of the distribution. This is illustrated by a simulation we conducted using four cities.1 Examining the proportion of streets producing 50 % of crime we find that the bandwidth of crime concentrations varies between 2.7 % and 8.1 % (see Table 1). This bandwidth is very similar to that that has been identified in prior studies of the law of crime concentration. But if we shift the metric to the proportion of crime produced by 5 % of streets the bandwidth now becomes 38.3 % to 60.9 %, following the wider bandwidths reported by Hipp and Kim (2017) and Lee et al. (2017). 4. Search methods

Our paper reports on a systematic review of crime concentration studies over the last 35 years. We find that most studies employ the metrics suggested by Weisburd (2015) and accordingly allow us to assess the “narrow bandwidth” assumption based on a large number of observations both for general crime and for specific types of crime. At the same time, we identify a number of studies using different metrics and different methodological approaches, which allow us to identify whether the law of crime concentration appears robust under different assumptions.

Our search relied on two databases that we thought would be likely to lead to identification of a wide array of studies in this area. The first was Criminal Justice Abstracts that includes 476 journals, and has comprehensive coverage of journals focusing on criminology and criminal justice (https://www.ebsco.com/products/research-databas es/criminal-justice-abstracts). While Criminal Justice Abstracts includes a number of general journals that publish consistently on issues related to crime and justice (e.g., journals in forensics, economics, social work, and sociology), we also sought to gain a wider disciplinary review. We did this by searching in Scopus (https://www.elsevier.com/en-gb/ products/scopus) which covers a large number of disciplines and subject areas. Scopus abstracts more than 25,000 journals and almost 300,000 books from 7000 publishers. Our search was conducted in October of 2023. We also reviewed the references for all of the articles that we included in our final review of crime concentration studies to identify potential gaps in these databases.

To identify papers to review in Criminal Justice Abstracts, we used the following search terms: “crim* concentrat*” OR “concentrat* of crim*” OR “law of crime concentration” OR “geography of crim*” OR “crim* and place” OR “crim* of place”. The search yielded 350 papers. For Scopus, we used the search terms: crim* AND concentrat*. The Scopus search yielded 879 papers after being restricted to relevant study fields (social sciences; psychology; economics; econometrics and finance; multidisciplinary) document types (article, book chapter, book, conference paper), and keywords (crime, violence, criminology, crime concentration, neighborhood(s), police, spatial analysis, spatiotemporal analysis, policing, statistics and numerical data, hot spots, GIS, violent crime, statistics, environmental criminology, spatial distribution, longitudinal study, crime and place).6

We reviewed the abstracts for all of these papers for relevance to our study that left us with 59 papers from Criminal Justice Abstracts and 100 papers from Scopus. After accounting for duplicates between the two databases, an in-depth review of these papers led us to identify 45 papers that fit our criterion of crime concentrations examined at a microgeographic unit of analysis. Reviewing the references for these papers added two additional studies to our list, resulting in a total of 47 papers. The 47 papers are described in Appendix A. 5. Review findings: the proportion of streets producing a set proportion of crime

Below we detail our findings regarding the proportion of streets producing a set proportion of crime for general crime, and for specific crime categories. We found that most of the crime concentration papers examine crime incidents at street segments. Of the 47 papers, only nine exclusively measured crime using crime calls for service (Andresen, Curman, & Linning, 2017; Andresen & Malleson, 2011; Castle & Kovacs, 2021; Curman et al., 2015; Hewitt, 2021; Hodgkinson & Andresen, 2019; O’Brien & Winship, 2017; Pierce et al., 1988; Sherman et al., 1989), and 11 papers examined alternative units of analysis (e.g., addresses, specific meter size hexagons) (Breetzke & Edelstein, 2019; Chainey & Monteiro, 2019; Chainey & Muggah, 2022; Fenimore, 2019; Haberman, 2017; Haberman et al., 2016; Hardyns et al., 2019; Harinam et al., 2022; Khalfa et al., 2023; Pierce et al., 1988; Sherman et al., 1989).

Papers we reviewed are often ambiguous regarding the treatment of intersections in their analyses. Following Weisburd (2015; see also Weisburd, Groff, & Yang, 2012), 13 papers note explicitly that intersections are not included in their analyses. Weisburd, Groff, and Yang (2014) argued that intersections often included traffic related offenses and were in that context very different from street segments. At the same time, the crime concentration levels of intersections and street segments have been found to be very similar (Weisburd, Telep, & Lawton, 2014). Eight papers note their inclusion of street intersections in addition to street segments,7 as contrasted with averaging intersection crime and adding those numbers to street segment counts (Andresen, Curman, & Linning, 2017; Andresen, Linning, & Malleson, 2017; Braga et al., 2011; Haberman et al., 2016; Hewitt, 2021; Hodgkinson & Andresen, 2019; Sherman et al., 1989; Walter et al., 2023). Nine papers mention the removal of certain types of street segments where crime is unlikely to occur and where these streets improperly break up street segments, ultimately inflating the number of units of analysis (Castle & Kovacs, 2021; Chalfin et al., 2021; Jones & Pridemore, 2019; Schnell et al., 2017; Schnell & McManus, 2022; Valente, 2019; Weisburd, Groff, & Yang, 2012, 2014; Weisburd & Zastrow, 2022).8

6. Review findings: alternative measurement approaches

6.1. Excluding streets with no crime

As noted above, a modification that seeks to correct for overestimation of crime concentration was proposed by Andresen and Malleson (2011). Rather than dividing by the number of street segments in the study area they suggested dividing by the number of street segments that actually had any crime. This modification underestimates the degree of crime concentration because the denominator it uses represents the number of places that actually have crime, not the number of places that could have crime. Nonetheless, the findings from papers using this approach are within the bandwidths noted above (see Appendix I). Using data from Vancouver, BC, Andresen, Curman, and Linning (2017) report that 50 % of crime occurs at between 7.3 % and 8.3 % of street segments and intersections experiencing any crime in the years 1991, 1996, 2001, and 2006. Andresen and Linning (2012) find that 50 % of calls for service in Vancouver occur on 8.2 % of street segments with any crime calls in 2001. Hodgkinson and Andresen (2019) report results from a medium-sized Midwestern Canadian city, in which 50 % of calls for service in 2014 are found within 3.1 % of street segments and intersections with at least one call for service. Additionally in Vancouver, Andresen, Linning, and Malleson (2017) find that 50 % of an aggregate measure of certain property crime incidents (commercial burglary, mischief, theft from vehicle, and theft of vehicle) occurs on 12.4 % of street segments and intersections experiencing any crime between 2003 and 2013.

Using longitudinal data from New York City, NY, Chicago, IL, and Philadelphia, PA, Chalfin et al. (2021) report that 50 % of crime occurred at between 8.5 % (New York City) and 11.9 % (Philadelphia) of street segments experiencing at least one crime. One study by Andresen and Linning (2012), finds much lower crime concentration with 50 % of crime incidents occurring on 17.9 % of street segments with at least one crime in Ottawa, ON, in 2006. Beyond North American cities, in Campinas, Brazil, de Melo et al. (2015) find that 50 % of an aggregate measure of incidents of rape, robbery, and theft are found in 12.8 % of

street segments with at least one of these crimes between 2010 and 2013. In the Hague, The Netherlands, Steenbeek and Weisburd (2016) find that 50 % of crime incidents occur within 13.0 % of street segments with at least one crime incidents between 2001 and 2009.

Looking at specific crimes this approach can provide very wide bandwidths, in part reflecting the relatively low base rates of crime and the often relatively small numbers of streets with any crime incidents for specific crimes. For example, using data from Chicago, Schnell et al. (2017) found that, of street segments with any violent crime, 50 % of violent crime incidents could be found on an average of 22.1 % of streets in 2001, 2008, and 2014.

6.2. The proportion of crime produced by a specific percentage of streets

Two papers provide a number of estimates of crime concentration inverting Weisburd’s law (2015). They examine the proportion of crime that is produced by a specific percentage of streets—for example, the proportion of crime in a city that is produced by the top 5 % of streets (Hipp & Kim, 2017; Lee et al., 2017).16 Lee et al. (2017) analyzed 26 studies of crime concentration at place that provided quantitative information sufficient for analysis. It is difficult to compare Lee et al.’s (2017) findings to those reviewed above because they include varying geographic units and measures of crime, and they did not break out the studies according to type when discussing crime concentration levels. Twenty-four of the studies examined crime incidents, but only 13 examined street segments. Their overall conclusion is consistent with a general law of crime concentration. They find that “crime is concentrated at a small number of places regardless of how crime is measured, the geographic unit of analysis used, or type of crime” (Lee et al., 2017, p. 11). At the same time, they argue:

Based on the estimated coefficients and intercepts of model specifications in this review study, the percent of crime explained by a specific percent of place (e.g., 5, 10, and 20%) varies across various geographic units, crime types, and measures of crime. It is only when we aggregate all studies that we find evidence supporting a strong interpretation of Weisburd’s (2015) law of crime concentration. A weaker version, that a relatively small proportion of all places contain most crime is supported.

(Lee et al., 2017, p. 11)

Hipp and Kim (2017) use a similar approach to examine crime concentration in 40 cities in the Southern California region. Most of the cities have populations less than 100,000, and only four of the cities have populations of over 250,000. Like Lee et al. (2017), it is difficult to make comparisons to Weisburd (2015) and other studies we review because of their focus on the percentage of crime produced by 5 % of streets, rather than the percentage of streets that produce 50 % of crime (also see Deckard & Schnell, 2023; Levin et al., 2017). Overall, similar to Lee et al. (2017), they find on average that about 5 % of streets produce about 50 % of general crime. Nonetheless, Los Angeles, which has a much larger population than the next largest city in the sample, has the lowest concentration figure for total crime in 2010 in the sample, with 5 % of streets including only 39 % of crime. 6.3. (Generalized) Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient

Studies using the (generalized) Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient also suggest that crimes are highly concentrated at street segments (see Appendix J). Across different crime types the Gini coefficients generally range between 0.71 and 0.90. These are quite high, and reflect a high degree of consistency, given the threshold of high crime concentration of 0.50 suggested by O’Brien (2019). There are, however, some deviations to this pattern. For example, Vandeviver and Steenbeek (2019), considering crime concentrations of residential burglary in Antwerp, Belgium, found generalized Gini coefficients ranging from 0.50 to 0.60, and Melo et al. (2020), considering crime concentrations of rape in Campinas, Brazil found generalized Gini coefficients ranging from 0.06 to 0.20. The results found by Vandeviver and Steenbeek (2019) do not deviate markedly from a law of crime concentration: their Gini coefficient values are still above the threshold of a high degree of (spatial) inequality. Considering the specifics of Melo et al. (2020), their analysis considers just over 200 rapes occurring across over 35,000 street segments. The generalized Gini coefficient accounts for the disparity between the number of crimes and the number of street segments, but with only 200–300 events and so many street segments, it is not a surprise that concentrations are so low.

7. Discussion

In this paper we reviewed crime concentration studies over the last 35 years. This allowed us to both reassess the law of crime concentration in the context of a large number of studies, including studies that use alternative metrics and methods, and to define more carefully the bandwidths of Weisburd’s (2015) law of crime concentration. Using the median as an estimate of average concentration, 50 % of crime generally in cities is found in just 4.5 % of streets, and 25 % of crime in just 1.3 % of streets. These estimates are very close to those identified by Weisburd (2015) and strongly reinforce the observation that crime is concentrated at street segments. Similar levels of concentration were identified for alternative measures of crime and micro geographies. Average levels of crime concentration were even more pronounced for specific types of crime, though some caution should be exercised here for very rare crimes (because of overestimating crime concentration when the number of crimes is less than the number of streets). Using the median as an estimator just 2.4 % of streets produced 50 % of thefts, and for robberies 3.2 % of streets produced 50 % of robberies. Clearly crime is very concentrated in cities.

A second key question in our paper was to define whether the bandwidths of crime concentration observed across a large number of cities could be defined as “narrow.” Weisburd’s (2015) original paper identified just 8 cities for examination. Analyzing those eight cities he found a bandwidth for 50 % of crime of between 2.1 % and 6.0 %, or 3.9 %; and for 25 % of crime a bandwidth of 0.4 % and 1.6 % of crime, or 1.2 %, for crime incidents at street segments. Our review found 49 estimates for crime concentration at 50 % of crime, examining street segments and crime incidents. The bandwidths observed across this much larger array of estimates of crime concentration are larger than those identified in Weisburd’s (2015) original article. The bandwidth for 50 % crime concentration is 9.3 %, as opposed to 3.9 %, and for 25 % crime concentration 1.2 % for Weisburd (2015) as contrasted with 3.0 % in our review. We think that these bandwidths remain narrow, in part because if there were uniformity in crime concentration (i.e. an even spread of crime across the city) it is possible to have 50 % of streets produce 50 %

of crime, and 25 % of streets produce 25 % of crime. Even when concentrations similar to the ones we observed are compared to an assumption of randomness rather than uniformity, Chalfin et al. (2020) find crime concentrations to be seven times (with a 50 % criterion) and 14 times (with a 25 % criterion) greater than would be expected by chance. Using the Gini coefficient as a metric we found that concentrations were generally between 0.71 and 0.90. Considering that a high concentration level can be assumed with a Gini coefficient of 0.50 (O’Brien, 2019), these findings strongly reinforce the assumption of a narrow bandwidth. In turn, examining the total bandwidth of crime hides a much greater regularity across most cities. To assess this, we also computed the interquartile range. Using this approach, we found a bandwidth of just 2.5 % (3.2–5.7 %) for 50 % of overall crime, and 1.4 % (0.8–2.2 %) for 25 % of crime.

One question is whether the specific metrics of these bandwidths are important for research or policy. Perhaps it is enough to conclude that crime is concentrated across cities. However, the extent of concentration may have important implications for policy and practice. A city with much greater concentrations of crime, closer to 2 % for example of streets producing 50 % of crime, may offer greater opportunities for implementing effective crime prevention strategies, such as hot spots policing, than a city which has 10 % of streets that produce 50 % of crime. In a city with 10,000 streets, 2 % of streets would translate to 200 streets that can be defined as high crime hot spots. 10 % of streets would translate to 1000 hot spot streets. Clearly, the differences here have important implications for the allocation of police resources. We think it important to note as well, that the concentration of crime may vary within neighborhoods within a city, just as they vary between streets in a city (see O’Brien, 2019). Accordingly, crime analysis should consider local as well as city wide trends in developing police deployment schemes.

Overall, looking at specific types of crime, there are generally smaller bandwidths of crime concentration for 50 % of crime. However, there is also strong evidence of the potential for deviant cases in the estimates we examine. For robbery, the lowest concentration estimate for Sao˜ Jo˜ao de Meriti, Brazil (10.5 %) is much lower than the next lowest concentration estimate for Chicago (7.4 %). For homicide, Chicago (3.2 %) has about half as much concentration as the next lowest concentration estimate, Sacre, Venezuela (1.5 %). Philadelphia (5.5 %) and Chicago (7.1 %) have concentrations almost half that of Baraquila, Columbia (3.4 %) for theft/larceny, and Chicago (11.8 %) has about half the crime concentration for vehicle theft as the next lowest concentration estimate for Lanus, Argentina (6.7 %).

The bandwidths we examine provide evidence that despite strong consistency in crime concentrations across cities, there is also important variability to explain. This is emphasized in the alternative measurement approach examined by Hipp and Kim (2017) and Lee et al. (2017). This is also reinforced by the presence of relatively deviant cases in some of these distributions. Why does Chicago stand out in our review as a relatively low crime concentration city? Recognizing the very narrow bandwidth of crime concentration for most cities, what explains those that fall for example outside the interquartile range of our estimates? We do not consider it a contradiction that crime concentrations follow a general law of crime concentration, but that there is also important variability that scholars should consider. This problem is similar to the old adage of seeing a glass as half full or half empty. Our review shows that crime is very concentrated in cities following Weisburd’s (2015) law of crime concentration. However, within that high degree of crime concentration there is variability that is important to examine.

Some variability we observe may be due to differences in the ways data are analyzed by scholars or collected by different police agencies across the world. We noted at the outset the different methodological choices made by researchers in for example, excluding or including

intersections, or specific street types (e.g., alleys or highways). In Khayelitsha, South Africa, which evidenced relatively low concentration of violent crime, there were concerns raised by the study authors regarding geospatial accuracy, and low crime reporting behaviour (Theron et al., 2023). More generally, crime reporting by citizens has been found to vary based on attitudes toward the law or the police (Sunshine & Tyler, 2003; Tyler, 2004). In this sense, the estimates of crime concentration in cities we observe are not necessarily estimates reflecting the true value of crime concentration, or the true bandwidth of crime concentrations, but rather reflections of that value filtered by differences in reporting of crime. Such differences may come not only from differences in citizen reporting, or accuracy of management information citizens, but also differences in the way call takers and police define and report crime. Such inconsistencies, and differences in analytic choices by researchers, may be leading to increases in the bandwidths of crime concentration unrelated to the real rates of crime concentration in cities. This is an important area for future study, but we can say little about the impact of such differences in methods and data collection at this juncture.

But we suspect that there are also key social and structural traits of cities that have an important impact on the extent of crime concentration. Hipp and Kim (2017), for example, show that higher crime rates are associated with significantly lower crime concentrations, while higher numbers of street segments are associated with significantly higher crime concentrations. Higher crime rates for example, could be one explanation for lower concentrations we observe in Chicago IL. Weisburd (2015) observed that smaller cities evidence stronger crime concentrations, though Zastrow (2021) suggests that this may reflect the extent of urbanization in cities rather than city size. Gill et al. (2017) interpret these findings as reflecting in good part the suburban street layout of smaller cities in which many streets have single family homes, but a few streets are likely to have large apartment buildings with large populations (and potentially many more crime targets). More recently, using data from all jurisdictions in England and Wales, Park (2019) found that jurisdictions with a longer mean length of street segments and lower population density had greater crime concentration. But despite these specific efforts, we have little knowledge about what explains differential levels of crime concentration in cities.

We think that understanding the causes of such variability are important for public policies of crime control. Because crime concentration has become a key assumption of crime prevention in cities, and especially in policing through hot spots approaches (Braga et al., 2019; Sherman & Weisburd, 1995; Weisburd & Majmundar, 2018), understanding of the correlates of crime concentration can lead to potential opportunities for improving crime prevention. From the perspective of crime prevention, when there are fewer streets that account for larger amounts of crime, there is greater opportunity for focusing limited policing resources to address crime problems. This is the logic of hot spots policing, and it follows that the greater the concentration of crime the more resources that can be brought to bear at specific places. At the same time, greater concentration in policing activities should lead to less police intrusion in places that are not defined as crime hot spots (Gladwell, 2019; Weisburd, 2016).17 Knowledge of what leads to greater

But this is a matter of “what the police do at hot spots” and not inevitable in the application of policing (Braga & Weisburd, 2010). For example, in a recent experimental study of training in procedural justice for hot spots policing units, training was found to lead both to more procedurally just behaviour on the part of police, as well as lower numbers of arrests, more positive community evaluations of police behaviour, and lower crime (Weisburd et al., 2022).

For example, knowledge about the relationship of land use to crime concentrations, could be used directly by city government as a tool for improving crime prevention. If land use characteristics influence crime concentration, changes in land use could be capitalized upon to increase concentration and through that allow the police or other city agencies to provide higher dosages of intervention for streets producing a larger amount of crime in the city. Similarly, evidence of strong relationships between innovative policing approaches and crime concentration could be seen as support for introducing a greater degree of innovation in policing in a city. While information about socio-demographic correlates of crime concentration, for example, could not be used for direct policy impact, they could be used to understand how changes in a city’s population might impact upon crime concentration. Such knowledge could be used to better plan for what crime concentration might look like in the future. This might impact for example on the number of police that a city considers necessary for crime control efforts.

8. Conclusions

Our review suggests the salience of the law of crime concentration both for understanding crime in cities, and for public policies for reducing crime. At the same time, it is important to recognize that our estimates of crime concentration are necessarily a convenience sample of cities, and beyond this we have little data to explore more carefully possible explanations for variability we observe. Our sample is simply a sample of places that researchers had opportunity to study. In this sense our estimates of crime concentration, and bandwidths of crime concentration, are necessarily limited in their ability to be generalized to cities more generally. At the same time, these estimates are based on a much larger group of cities than prior descriptions of the law of crime concentration, and they have allowed us to go beyond prior work in this area. But it is important that more representative samples be identified to define the bandwidths of the law of crime concentration, and in tandem data needs to be collected on the characteristics of the cities studied.18

What is clear from our review is that the general framework of a law of crime concentration applies across a large number of cities across many regions around the world. In turn, this conclusion is supported when examining alternative metrics and methods for measuring crime concentration.
